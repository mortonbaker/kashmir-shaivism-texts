# The Shivastotravali

*The Garland of Hymns to Shiva, by Utpaladeva — where devotion and recognition are one act*

A plain-English rendering of all twenty hymns, written for narration. The verses are given in order, each hymn under its own header, in ordinary spoken English. Where a verse is terse, the meaning has been pinned by Kshemaraja's vivriti, the old commentary that travels with the text — the way the Virupaksha rendering leaned on Vidyacakravartin. Nothing has been added that the verses and that commentary don't carry.

---

## About this text

Utpaladeva was one of the towering philosophers of the tradition — author of the great Verses on the Recognition of the Lord, the founding work of the whole Pratyabhijna school, and the spiritual grandfather of Abhinavagupta. But he was more than a philosopher. He was a lover of God. In the Shivastotravali the rigorous metaphysician sets his arguments down and simply sings.

The collection is a garland of hymns — devotional songs poured out, the tradition says, in spontaneous ecstasy, later gathered by his disciples into twenty. They carry a teaching that is easy to miss and precious to grasp: that devotion and non-dual recognition are not opposites but a single movement of the heart. In most mystical systems you must choose — either you melt in love before a God who is other than you, or you realize you *are* the divine and so have no one left to worship. Utpaladeva refuses the choice. The Lord he loves is his own Self, and the love he feels is the Lord's own nature. To recognize that you are Shiva is not to lose the beloved; it is to discover that the love, the lover, and the beloved were always one.

So these hymns hold both registers at once, often in the same breath: blazing recognition and aching absence, the wonder of seeing the whole world as the beloved's body and the plain human cry of the times the recognition dims. He praises, he longs, he complains, he exults — and under all of it runs the one conviction that the one you love, the one who loves, and the love itself are the single boundless Light that you are. What follows is that garland, hymn by hymn.

## One — The Play of Devotion

There is a person in whom Shiva simply dawns — no meditating, no muttering of names, none of the prescribed steps. The Lord doesn't have to be reached for; he breaks open inside this person all on his own, like that, with no ritual at all. To that one, the one rich in love, I bow. That bow is the whole hymn opening its hand: praise for the devotee in whom the Lord shines without technique, because the love itself is the shining.

My own self, Lord — it has drunk the nectar of loving you, and that drink keeps it forever young. And yet, to look at, it seems to have gone gray, dusted over with the gray of the world, the way the road's dust settles on a young man and makes him look old before his time. But that's only the look of it. The grayness is just the dust of getting through the day; underneath, the love keeps me young, and the dust never reaches in. It can't even touch the freshness. If anything, all that ordinary coming-and-going only feeds the wonder, like a joke that makes the young man laugh.

Your devotees, the ones who've come into your wealth — they live inside you, inside the city that is your own being, which holds the whole world. And so even when they walk the common road, doing the plain business of life, they walk it in that same wide unfolding, that same overflow of the joy of being merged in you. Even their errands open like flowers.

When this whole world, every realm in it, is plainly and actually made of you, Lord — then for those who love you, where is there not a holy place? Where could their words of power, their *mantra*, fail to come true? For them the world isn't called yours as a manner of speaking. They see it. Every corner of it is you, straight to the eye.

They are riding high, your lovers — drunk, gloriously drunk, on the strong wine that is the nectar of devotion. And here is the wonder of them: they are utterly alone, one without a second, sharing their nature with nothing — and at the very same time they always have you as their second, you their constant companion. No-second, and you-as-second, both at once. The contradiction is the sweetness.

Lord, the ones who truly know what you are — your very reality, the bottomless sea of endless joy — are the ones who have become that sea themselves: soaked through with the dense, thick joy of love, until they are made of the same thing you are. Only what is endless joy can know endless joy. The knower has to be of the kind he knows.

You alone are the Self of everyone, Lord — and everyone, without exception, is in love with their own self. So the love is already there, built into the grain of things: in loving yourself you were loving him all along. A person who simply *sees* that — who recognizes that this love of yours is the most natural thing there is — that person has already won. He stands at the height of everything.

When all the things you could know fall quiet, Lord — when the mind turns inward and there's nothing left to perceive — to whom do you not show yourself then, standing there alone, the one bare Self? And in the other direction too: even in the racket of the world, even with knower and known shaking and colliding, your devotees see you easily, plainly, with delight. Wherever they are, it's you they're looking at.

There is a Goddess, sweet as endless joy — your dearest, your beloved — and she is never, ever apart from you. Let my love for you, the love that knows you, be like her. Let it be like that — never for an instant parted from you. I could have just said, *let me not be separated from devotion*; but say it the other way and you hear the rush of tenderness in it — *let her never leave me.*

Every single thing, Lord, is a way home to you for those who love you. This whole road of awareness, the road of light running through everything we know, lays itself out in three colors — joy, and pain, and the dull fog in between — and not one stretch of it leads anywhere but to you. Step down through each thing known, and you come up standing as the one who knows it all.

There's a state of awareness past the ordinary, Lord — the high, still, peaceful state of Shiva that people say comes only when the body finally drops. Some prize it as the summit. But to me, after tasting the nectar of loving you, that famous state tastes flat — like wine gone sour, like a drink left standing overnight. They call it the peak because they never tasted the love; they let it go stale. The ones who actually drink the love rest so completely in its own delight that nothing else could even seem like the better cup.

Those in whom your devotion — that great knowing, that supreme science — has ripened through practice, they are the ones who truly understand. They see clear through both sides of things: through knowledge and through ignorance, through the bright mantra-natures and the dark bound ones, all of it. Their love itself has grown into the highest knowing — higher than any learning made of mere words — and that is why it knows the real.

This vine of speech, rising all the way up from the root, from the silent ground, and spreading out stage by stage into bloom — let it be watered with the nectar of devotion to you, and let it bear, for me, the fruit thick with that nectar's juice: the fruit that is oneness with you, your own nature become mine.

"Become Shiva, and then worship," the old teachings say — which is only to say, *become a devotee, and then worship.* For you yourself are the very heart of being, the choicest essence — and your devotees have polished that essence spotless with non-duality, scouring off the last splinters of the suspicion that anything stands apart. To love you is already to have become you; only then is there any worship at all.

For those who love you, what reasoning does *not* prove your oneness? Every argument lands there. And for the low ones, the ones still steeped in division, who try to argue against it — what is there that does *not* become a veil over them? Even the sharpest objections, fired against Shiva's oneness, only collapse back into proof of it for the lover; while for those who can't taste even a drop of the joy of merging, those same clever weapons, unnoticed, only drag them down into deeper dark.

"Only sometimes, only somewhere, only by long discipline can you be found, Lord" — what a cheat that is, what a swindle. Because if it were true, how could you be blazing, as you are, at every level and in every state of your lovers' lives? They find you everywhere. The "only by technique" was always a lie.

Here's a great difference, Lord, between your devotees and the yogis. The yogis depend on it all — the drawing-in of the senses, the holding, the fixing of the mind. Your lovers are never even brushed by any of that, and yet — here's the marvel — even when they're up and moving, out in the ordinary world, they stay gathered, steady, merged. Where the yogi has to come down out of his trance, the lover is still inside it.

No yoga, no austerity, no careful sequence of rites is laid down here at all. On this path of Shiva — this highest place, beyond all technique, witnessed only by your own experience — none of the measured methods of the veil are taught, because next to the clear light those methods are themselves a kind of darkness, and no real means to what has no edges. Here only one thing is praised, only one thing called the way: love. Devotion, and devotion alone.

May even the *name* of worry perish in me — me, whose every covering has been blasted away by the light of devotion blazing on all sides, inside and out; me, for whom every single thing now stands plainly present, lit up, nothing hidden. Let not even the word for anxiety remain. Let me live wholly entered into you, the supreme reality, seen face to face forever.

Just the one word — *Shiva* — sitting always on the tip of the tongue: and in those who love you, somehow, the whole taste of every possible thing is in it. One small word, one bare syllable, and through it the full sweep of the world's flavor, the wonder and joy of the whole universe, comes pouring in. Only in the devotees. How astonishing that is.

In that ocean of the nectar of devotion — its waves gone still, cool against the world's fever, clear and spotless because the whole world is mirrored in it, and sweet because it brims with joy — there, lost in a delight no ordinary world can offer, the happy ones simply rest. And once they're resting there, who is even left to be counted? When the differences dissolve, there's no one apart from it left over to add up. The ones who rest in that joy count nothing, because there's nothing else there to count.

Why wouldn't people like me chew on it endlessly — the great healing herb that is devotion to you, Lord? That kind of devotion, the kind whose very next taste, with no gap between, is the juice called liberation. Roll it over in the mind, savor it, keep it under the tongue — and right behind the taste, instantly, comes the bliss they call being free while still alive.

These, and only these, are the riches the good ones beg for, Lord — the riches that swell the free, easy, trusting enjoyment of the bliss of loving you. Not the little magic powers, not the trinkets of the adepts — only this. Only what feeds the open delight of being drowned in the nectar of you. That's the whole of what they ask for; everything else they let go.

It is a strange and wonderful thing, Lord. This mind — which by its own nature is a seed of nothing but sorrow, the very thing the whole world should want to be rid of — this same mind, once it's soaked through with the nectar of loving you, becomes the great fruit, the highest good, the bliss of liberation. The world never once gets anything sweet out of its own gloom and grief. So this turning of poison to nectar — this belongs to your devotion alone, and to nothing else under heaven. And in that, peace.

## Two — Everything as the Self

I bow to you who wear every shape — fire and moon and sun, Brahma and Vishnu, all that stands still and all that moves. You are the form of all of them: fire that burns, moon that nourishes, sun that lights the world; the will, the act, and the knowing; the great gods who run things and the still earth and the moving creatures and the whole heap of what is known and the one who knows it. And under all those faces you are one thing only — pure awareness. That is the heart of you: a single freedom, overflowing, that has thrown out the whole crowded world from inside itself and fills every corner of it.

I bow to you, the great fire — the fire that is the highest knower of all. The whole broken-up world is your fuel. You burn it down, and even the ash you leave behind, the last residue of the burning, becomes a fresh anointing — a smearing that doesn't dull the knower but stokes it brighter. Your blaze is clean, pure, without a second thing in it. They say the truly pure fire is the one born of the friction of sun and moon grinding together; that is the fire you are, the one whose single offering is the entire universe. One world, thrown whole into the flame — that's how fiercely you burn.

I bow to you, the fire of Shiva — thick with the highest nectar, and cool. How strange: a fire, and yet brimming and cold to the touch. Cold because you carry off every fever of this world; sweet because you are nothing but solid bliss and awareness packed close. A fire that is wet, that is cooling — there's a seeming contradiction in it, and I let it stand. I bow to a certain something past all ordinary naming, a fire that, even as it soothes, blazes wild enough to burn the whole world to nothing.

I bow to you, Mahadeva, Rudra, Shankara, Shiva, and Maheshvara too — to a certain someone whose very body is made of mantra. You are the god at endless play, making and keeping and undoing the world for sheer delight, towering over everything, wanting to win, setting every transaction of life in motion, shining, the one all praise and all seeking is aimed at. You are great, because even Brahma and the rest get their making and unmaking from you. You make the heart cry out and you melt it down — that is why they call you Rudra. And because you are the full, unbroken taste of "I," your very form is mantra.

I bow to the fire of Shiva — auspicious, and yet terrible. The three worlds, sliced clean apart at the root by the fog of not-seeing — being, non-being, and what lies beyond both — give up their inmost marrow, the rich fat at the heart of things, and that becomes the offering poured on the flame, the fuel that lights the fire of awareness. Drenched in it, the fire roars up uneven and fierce. A burning-ground fire, soaked in the fat of everything — how on earth is that called auspicious? Because it lifts away all the ill-fortune of this round of birth and death. It is the fire that takes the little measured knower — bound to body and breath — and pours it back into the boundless.

I bow to that god, that certain Shambhu, whose one true mark is that no mark fits him at all. Every sign, every token, every technique meant to reach him — the chanting, the rites, the long sitting in meditation — all of it falls away, and that very falling-away is the nearest thing to a sign of him. Because the only road to him is forgetting every anxious effort. And so he is a certain someone — the crooked phrase is on purpose: it murmurs that he is your own Self, flashing up from inside you.

I bow to the hidden Lord, who breaks the Vedas and the scriptures, who lays them down, and who is their very truth. He can't be caught by any rule, so he runs against the grain of all of them — yet out of his own freedom, wanting to wake the world up, he is the one who set them down in the first place. And he is what they're really pointing at, their final meaning. Because he is no one's object, never something to be grasped from outside — he stays hidden, secret, the one truth no eye can fall on.

I bow to Shambhu — the single cause of this whole turning world and its single undoing, the one who wears the shape of the world and the one wholly free of it. From the veil at the top down to the solid ground, all of it has one source, and that same source is the one who gathers it back. He shines out as the world itself — for the world has no shape of its own apart from him, the pure light of awareness. And even so he stands clean outside it, untouched, beyond all coming and going. Both at once, and no contradiction in it.

I bow to the full and perfect Shambhu — root, middle, and tip; the one whose body is root and middle and tip together; and the one in whom root and tip and middle have all dissolved away. He is the world's root, since he is its cause; its middle, since he is its very being; its tip, since he is the place it comes home to rest. He is these one after another, and he is all of them at once, because his is the world's whole shape, timeless and without sequence. And in his own Self there is no root or middle or tip at all — only pure awareness, single and entire. That is why he is full: he bears everything.

I bow to Shiva, so hard to reach. Even one taking of his name — said just once — is the full ripening of a whole harvest of good deeds, because that single utterance hands you the complete rest no other thing can give. To that one, the one only the great yogis come to, I bow.

I bow to you, the one who plays forever with heaps of the dead — to you alone, made wholly of awareness, the skull-bearer. They tell the old tale of you wandering with skulls, and here is what it really means: every shape that moves and every shape that stands still has gone over into you, the supreme awareness — and apart from you each is a lifeless thing, a corpse, since nothing lives without you. With endless heaps of these you play, always, all at once, joining and parting them in a thousand wild ways. You are the one made of awareness, awareness itself the only truth of you — the skull-bearer who holds the whole bone-pile of the worlds in his hands, every form reduced to a skull he carries.

I bow to the dazzling Shambhu — the wielder of the veil, and yet utterly clean; hidden, and yet wide open; subtle, and wearing the shape of the whole world. The freedom that throws up all this difference, that is your veil, your Maya — and still you are spotless, because you are nothing but awareness. A weaver of illusions, a trickster, and pure all the same: let the seeming clash stand. Hidden, beyond anyone's reach; and yet plain and open, your own being a dense blaze of light. Subtle, missed even by those deep in meditation; and yet wearing every form there is, having freely taken on the shape of the world. That is why you are dazzling — strange and wonderful and full of surprise.

I bow to you, the all-powerful, who must be marveled at. Brahma and Indra and Vishnu carry this whole world along — they make it, watch over it, hold it up — and the undoing of all that labor is, for you, the lightest play. You are the wonder of wonders, the one in whom every power lives, because even Brahma and the rest, in their own work, are forever watching your face to see whether you wish to draw it all back in.

I bow to you, the bottomless ocean of Shiva. People wander the shorelines — the lore of mantras and seals and the wheels and the stages — circling in the outer shallows where the juice of awareness laps the edge. Worn out by spinning the breath and forcing the winds, lost in the conjuror's tricks, they're like the ones the old saying calls "wandering, wandering, far from the highest place" — because they never reach the marrow within. And so they pick up this little power and that, the petty gains — to grow tiny, to grow vast — bits of difference, no more. They're like men who only pace the beach of an unfathomable sea and come away with a few shells. But the ones who can bear to plunge inward — they drink the nectar itself, the deep one that gives release.

I bow to the unstainable Shambhu — the hundred-petaled lotus, gleaming. The world made of Maya is a thick, dense mud, and you live right in the middle of it, since you fill all of it through and through — and you stay clean, the one pure shape of awareness, untouched. You are Shambhu the lotus itself: the hundred-petaled bloom, each petal a power without end, opening and closing in its own season. Sitting in the heart of the mud and never smeared by it — because you are awareness, too dense and pure for any mud to cling to. Let the seeming impossibility stand.

I bow to you — the blessing, the pure one, the treasure, the very ornament of being; the beloved, the highest truth, the one who stands above all. Whatever face you turn to question him by, that's the face of the best there is — because he is supreme every way you take him.

I bow to a certain Shambhu — forever bound and forever free, and free of both binding and release. You can be spoken of as bound and as freed, and so people speak of you that way — but the truth is, since you are nothing but the dense light of awareness, you are neither. Let the seeming clash stand, here and everywhere after.

In this whole vast triple world — being and non-being and what's past them both — a thing so flimsy it's almost a joke, you alone are real: the one without a second, your own unshared shape, seated in unbroken joy. To you alone, the only thing worth having, I bow.

I bow to you, Sharva — the marrow of the right-hand path, who longs also for the left; the lord of all observance, and the one with no observance at all. The straight, clean rite is your essence; you reach as well for the reversed, the wild way. Every practice is your own native pulse, and you have walked clean out the far side of all of them, past every meditation and rite. To you, who are the whole truth of all these paths and the no-path beyond them, I bow.

However he is worshipped, wherever he is worshipped, whoever or whatever he is — to that god I bow. For those who know the truth, any act done anywhere, in any manner, lands as worship all by itself, as a coming home to rest in the Self that is the god. The loose words — "however," "wherever," "whoever" — are there on purpose, to throw out every rule. As the scripture says: worship him however you can, with whatever you have.

I bow to you, the giver of every gift — the one the seekers of freedom love to serve, who carries off every burning grief, a wide spreading ocean of grace. Those hungry for release can serve you freely, with no harness on them. You strip away all the fevers that come of seeing the world as broken into pieces. Your grace is a vast flood, because being solid bliss you are the one thing most worth wanting. And you give the best gift of all: the clean, pure marrow of awareness itself.

I bow to you, the Lord, the unending festival — you who, always and without a break, fill all three worlds full to the brim with the nectar of your own joy. You are their master because you flood the whole of them with your bliss; you are the festival that never ends because you are always making the world overflow — and on a festival day, everything is brimmed with the taste of joy.

I bow to you, the terrible one — the throng of powers that worship you with delight. There is a circle of goddesses, the powers of your own awareness, and they serve you with the sweetest offerings: they taste the bliss at the core of every object and lay the whole world back at your feet, resting in the dense joy of the knower. To that throng — terrible, since it draws all things back into the end — and so to you, I bow.

I bow to a certain you — the one even the sages cannot fathom. The great seers, Kapila and the rest, deep in their austerity and yoga, still cannot know him. And those whose every act is soaked in the bliss of immersion in him, who fold him in their arms and make him the one they take their joy with — even they only ever reach a certain someone flashing up inside their own Self. To that one I bow.

I bow to you — the storehouse of the highest nectar, the very heap and mass of it; the one reached only by the topmost of all the topmost heights. You are the treasure-chest brimful of supreme bliss, and the open heap of it too, since outside as well you are made of nothing else. Of everything there is, from the veil on down, the highest reach is its pure shining; and higher even than that is the dense, wondrous bliss, the flowering of pure power — and by that, only by that, are you reached.

I sing your form, the form made of the great mantra — clear and cool, sweet with a fragrance never smelled before, soaked and swollen with the highest nectar. Made of the great mantra: that is, of the natural, uncontrived sense of "I." Clear, because it holds the reflection of the whole world. Cool, because it lifts away the fever of this turning life. Sweet with a fragrance found nowhere else, a perfume past this world that spreads everywhere and gladdens, lovely and longed-for. And drenched, brimming over, with the taste of the supreme bliss.

I sing your teaching, O Lord. There is a great cloth woven of the knowledge that you and the world are one — a freedom soaked in nectar, dense with joy, threaded through the whole bright weave of the universe. And the wonder of it is this: on that cloth there is no wonder at all — no second, no variety, nothing strange — because every thread of it is only the one truth, that you and all of this are one. The teaching that points there, the scripture that lays it down — that is what I sing. A picture-cloth with no picture in it: that is the marvel.

I bow to the path of the Great Lord. It is the thunderbolt that shatters every anxious doubt — all the worry over whether the substance was right, the mantra correct, the rite mixed wrong — every seed of this tangled world, every wilting of the heart. The scripture itself says: have no doubt, not even a flicker; this fearlessness is plain. It is the world-ending fire for all our joylessness, the conflagration that burns up every ill omen down to nothing. To that path of the Great Lord — the wide flood of pure power — I bow.

Victory to you, O God; I bow, and bow again. This whole entire world rests in you. You are the Supreme Lord of all the worlds. You alone are the one beyond all — and I have come to you for shelter. I have been split off from you, made to seem separate, by clinging to this body as "me" — a separateness your own power of veiling conjured up — and so I come, a refugee, to you. And it's only right that I should, for this whole world rests in you, drowned in your own being, which is pure awareness. You, and you alone, are the Supreme Lord of all the worlds, higher than Brahma, higher than every god up to Sadashiva at the very top. So, O God — you whose nature is play — be victorious: throw off this clinging to the body as "me," and stand out as what you truly are.

## Three — Coaxing the Beloved

Things come and go in two ways — they rise into being, and they fall out of it. Being and non-being: that's the whole of how the world moves, the two roads everything travels. But there's a third thing that won't be filed under either one — that can't be called "is" or "isn't," and so can only be pointed at, the way you point at the fourth state by counting past the other three. To that third, wondrous one — to Shambhu, the astonishment that the whole shining universe is — I bow.

From the great seers on down — Marichi and the rest, the very gods — this triple world is run by something else. None of them is free; they're all caught in the making and the unmaking, swept along in the rising and falling. Only you, who do the making and the unmaking, are free. And here's the sweetness of it: those who live in your shadow, who live on you, who taste themselves as nothing but you — they're free too. Your freedom is contagious. Lean your whole life on the one free Lord and his freedom becomes yours.

There's one medicine for every fever this world of coming-and-going can give a person — one cure for the whole sickness of being born and dying. It's this: to keep remembering your form, the form with the entire universe set into it like jewels. To hold you in mind not as a thought but as a soaking-in — your own awareness recognized as theirs. The ones who carry that remembrance live at ease. Pains still touch the body, the way pains do — the body comes with them, there's no avoiding that. But underneath, in a happiness as thick as the highest bliss, they simply sit, untroubled, at rest.

His white parasol is the moon — the whole field of knowable things, brimming full now with the rays of his own awareness, shining so it shields him from the glare. His fan is the streaming of the heavenly river — the current of consciousness running down the middle, washing away heaven and hell and the fruits of every deed alike, the badge of his glory waving over him. He is the one — not some other — supreme Lord. And read it plainly, on the surface too: the moon and the Ganges for parasol and fan, the regalia that belong to no one but him.

Grant me a sight, Lord. A way of seeing that shines on its own, that's cool and puts out the burning, that's pure and cuts down every suspicion of separateness — one seeing, with no second to it, like a single sliver of moon. Something I've never had before, a vision that runs with nectar. Give me that, my master. Pour it into me.

These flecks of awareness — this flash of blue, that stab of pleasure, every small knowing — they've broken off from you, splashed up out of your ocean of consciousness and bliss. And because they're lit, because they shine, they're nothing but that same bliss in miniature, drops carrying the whole sea's sweetness. So how could they not be beautiful with the taste of nectar, my God? How could each spark, scattering off you, be anything but lovely? They cannot be otherwise. Of course they are.

There are hearts that never sank into you, Lord — never went under in the sweet flood of love that pours toward you, the spreading of devotion. And a heart that won't drown in that has no heart at all. People like that, hollow where the heart should be, are just vessels made to hold the world's grief — and to anyone who loves you, they don't even register. They're beneath counting.

But the one — the rare one — whose heart has met yours, has melted into yours and become one with you in that drowning: he, and he alone, is the cup that holds all your sovereign glories, all the riches of undivided bliss. Just one. Notice it isn't said in the plural — the heartless are many, but the one whose heart meets the Lord's is single, and into him alone is poured the whole inheritance.

The flood of meditation on you — the nectar-flood of holding you in mind until you've soaked all the way through — drowns every joy and every sorrow at once, levels them all together. The way a great rising water doesn't pick and choose: it floods the low ground and the high ground in the same moment, the pure stretches and the impure, and makes one smooth sheet of them all. Once you've gone under like that, the whole of it floods in together, all of it brimming with the highest bliss, joy and grief drowned in the same tide.

What state could there possibly be that isn't loaded to overflowing with happiness — for those who are never, anywhere, for one instant parted from you? You, who are more their own self than their own self is, once the body and the rest have sunk out of sight and only you stand there, blazing, dense as pure awareness. For them every condition there is comes packed full of the highest bliss. They're alive, and never split off from the Lord — and so they are always, always happy.

I'm roaring — listen to me — I'm dancing! Every last wish I had is full. My Lord has been gotten, he's mine, this very one — because you are so utterly lovely, so achingly dear. This one — him I can't even put into words; I've simply tasted him for myself and now I know. So I roar, I let out the great shout, I fling my limbs about in the sheer overflow of joy, shaking off in every direction the whole long stupor of the veil. My wishes are full. There's nothing left to want — that's what's happened to me. And there's wonder in it, the catch of breath of a man who has just recognized the unbounded awareness as his own face.

With that Lord gotten, there's nothing else left to know — no other thing standing apart to be known, no separate act, no separate yoking, no second awareness. Nothing at all dawns on me now but him. And yet it isn't emptiness — there is fullness, brimming fullness. Whatever knowing arises now is the one whole offering of the entire universe poured into the fire of awareness; it is the full "I" tasting itself, awareness in its very act. And such knowing is just this: consciousness itself, the light that is Shiva, opening out and stretching wide — and nothing else.

There are sufferings past counting, past conquering — and from such people they suddenly turn tail and run, all at once, out of their very grip. From which people? The ones in whose mouths the sound "Shiva" never stops ringing. For say it the other way: a mouth empty of that sound is a mouth overrun by every grief there is. From the Creator on high down to the worm in the dirt, not one creature is truly happy; each one just keeps working its little changes, clinging, wanting only to go on living. But let the name keep sounding, and the whole horde of sorrows breaks and flees.

There's a "highest Person" people talk about — Vishnu, marked out as supreme. But marked out by what? By being yours: a special form lifted up over the rest because it presides over the knowers who hold the wisdom of non-difference, which is to say, over you. You are the Great Person, the one, the refuge in whom every single person comes to rest. Read it the other way and it still lands: among all the persons — first, second, and third, every "he" and "you" and "I" — you are the one in whom they all settle and find their ground, because everything that gets pointed at as "that" finally rests in the "I," and the "I" is you. One, without a second. So the word "great" belongs to no one but you.

They triumph — your servants, whom the whole world should bow to, for they are nothing now but Shiva poured all the way in. Servants of you, Lord of the worlds, and yet they triumph, because this very ocean of being-and-becoming — this terrible, drowning sea — has become for them a great lake to play in. Once you've seen to the bottom of it and found it's only awareness, the dreadful thing turns into a pool for sport. The whole world becomes a game you watch yourself play.

Set aside, for a moment, all the other small beggings people bring here — the petty cravings for this little power or that, the wishes of those who already live steeped in you. Set them aside, because for someone like that there's nothing left over to ask for. There's only one thing he might still, in some rare flooded instant, say — *show yourself, become visible to me* — and even that he's half ashamed to ask. And if even that prayer brings a blush, then by plain reckoning every lesser begging is already ruled out. There's simply no room left in him for any meaner want.

"There is nothing higher than me — and yet, even so, I am one who chants." And how can that be? Because of oneness: the truest chanting is the undivided awareness recognizing itself, the full "I" tasting itself, going on forever without a break. This is what you teach, here and there, with the prayer-beads in your hand — Shiva with his rosary, Gauri with hers. Bead after bead returning, the whole string of the powers turning and turning to spin out worlds and draw them back — and through it all the one chant: the unbroken awareness sounding itself. With nothing but a string of beads you teach the whole secret.

Whatever is, must rest on something more real than itself. So there is a Real beyond the real, and you are higher even than that, my Lord. You aren't the unreal — the flower in the sky, the thing that never was. And you aren't merely the real — this blue, this pleasure, the passing things that are. You're other than both, set apart from both, thick with consciousness and bliss. And just because of that, you are made of being and non-being together — real, and unreal, and both, the whole world's very self — not just "is," not just "isn't," not just neither. That state is hard to know. It is some unsayable, ultimate thing. So I don't define it; I only point.

You shine with a pure light brighter than a thousand suns put together — for even the suns shine only by your shining. One single light, pure consciousness, immense and surpassing, and so the light that fills every world there is. And yet you are not seen. Pouring out everywhere, lighting everything, you stand there blazing — and the eyes blurred by the veil don't see you. Shining in plain view, and still not recognized.

In the dead, dumb world — earth and stone, all the way up — you are consciousness. In everything knowable, you are the knower. In every limited, measured thing, you are the boundless one running through it. And just because of that — conscious in the unconscious, knower in the known, limitless in the limited — you are the highest of all, above everything.

Enough now of the other laments, the other cries. This much, just this, right here in front of the Lord: I cry out, and the cry is sharp and tearing. For even this, my master — even now, knowing full well what I know — I lose my footing and fall back into bewilderment. I stand in the truth, I have tasted the soaking-in, and still, when I come back down into ordinary waking, I am thrown. Knowing it, I am still bewildered. May there be peace — may there be Shiva.

## Four — The Welling-Up of Sweetness

Even when you're flighty, my mind — even then you come off well, because look what you keep turning toward. You bow to the one who is the refuge of every refuge, the teacher of all the worlds, the beloved of Ambika, the supreme Power herself. Brahma, Vishnu, the great gods take shelter somewhere; and the one they take shelter in is the one you've set your heart on. Even a flicker of that service is the whole of fullness. So whatever scatters you, you've still landed well.

Here's the strange thing about me. I've climbed past the whole staircase of lesser gods — Brahma, Vishnu, Rudra, the Lord, Sadashiva, rung over rung — and reached the feet of Shiva himself, his very rays, and let them gather me in. And yet, even now, even now, I won't let go of the lowest floor of all: this cramped little sense that I'm only this body, this name, this small made thing. I've touched the highest and I still cling to the dust. Strange.

Show me my own road, the path that is my own innermost power. Hush all the noise of the world, every last story of it, blot it out — until I become, here and now, your servant forever, never not yours, standing always lit at your feet.

Shiva, Shiva, Shambhu, Shankara — you who melt for anyone who comes to you for shelter — be quick, have mercy. For the one whose whole mind rests on remembering your two lotus feet, the riches are not far off. Those feet, knowledge and action, the twin rays of you; and the riches, the very heart of immersion in you, made all of supreme bliss — near, so near.

The ones who settle on the seat of your lotus feet and from there shape whole worlds however they please, savoring their own freedom — they laugh, Lord, at poor Brahma on his lotus, smeared all over with the grime of office, bound to the law of how-things-must-go, not even his own master. He sits there a figure of fun, while they make and unmake at will.

You whose body is sheer light — nothing stands apart from you, and nothing else has any power to shine at all. So you are always already found, Lord, always in our grasp — and at the very same time utterly far off, hidden past nature itself. For whatever manages to flash into appearance, whatever shines, is in truth not separate from that light that is your very form, since only the luminous is ever fit to be lit. The knower himself stands, always and everywhere, as the very being of all that's known.

The nectar of your lotus feet — some come to it with their habits already gone stale with separateness, soured like an oyster shut tight, and they get no immediate taste of it at all. But others — washed clean by a stroke of the supreme grace — drink it down the instant it arrives: ever-fresh, whole and unwounded, free of any second thing, one solid mass of awareness and bliss. They taste it, and the wonder of it bursts open in them.

Master, your radiance flashes in me like lightning sometimes — a pulse of your power steeped in nectar, the highest bliss — for a single instant it lights up inside me when I'm immersed in you, and then it's gone. If only that flash would steady and stay, brushing aside all the dragging pull of the everyday and standing always risen — then you'd be worshipped exactly right, just as you truly are. What else would there be to do? It would all be finished, and I, finished and fulfilled.

You are all this. And whether the world is real, or unreal, or simply nothing — none of it holds, since everything is solid awareness, everything shines only because it is made of your light. However the great wisdom, the pure clear knowing, makes you out to be here in this world — exactly that way, stand fully revealed to me. Even in the broken-down everyday, by the force of immersion, show yourself plain.

Of your own free will, Lord — by you, the Master, and by no one and nothing else — you set my foot on your own road, the path of your unfolding power, and let me rest there. So how is it that I still wander around like any ordinary man of the crowd? In all my daily commotion I understand nothing of what's worthy of your feet, nothing of the intimacy with your rays I was made for.

Some rare and highest sweetness, my God, uncoils in the hearts of those few — the ones already full of love for you — some surge of overflowing bliss, raw and abundant, welling up out of nowhere. And by it, even the ones who only listen — the chatak birds thirsting for the thundercloud-rumble of your story, hearts thrown wide just at the sound of the tale of you — even they are made beautiful, made lucky, for a long time after. The mere telling of you brings the immersion down on them.

For those who love you and serve you with delight, through some game of yours past all telling, this love has ripened past anything common. So far that even in the ground of separation from you — even out in the ordinary, broken day — the simple talk of you, the simple memory of you, ripens into the festival of reunion. To bear the joy of meeting in the very soil of parting — that's how unworldly this love has grown.

That word — *Shankara* — swollen by being soaked again and again in the strangest, sweetest savor, cried out a hundred times and a hundred more, every time fresh with new use — it enters even into the dull and the crooked, even the closed hearts of beasts, and works its wonder there.

They win, the ones in whom the sound of Shiva turns and turns for certain in the round of their joy. It rises, that sound, like the moon climbing out of the ocean of nectar — climbing out of the seen face of the Great Lord, dense awareness made plain — and from it the supreme sweet nectar streams, the very juice of bliss spilling over. Risen straight from the heart's own taste, swelled by being drenched again and again in the wondrous flood of immersion, voiced of its own sweetness a hundredfold — *Shankara* — that sound enters even sideways into stubborn animal hearts, new and astonishing in every instant. They win.

This whole fierce world looks finished now, as good as gone — for the one drowned in you, the outer universe stops shining, lingering only as a faint trace. And the thick, unbroken grime of the mind, the stain of unknowing, has fallen away. And yet, even so — there is not the least cracking-open, not by a hair's breadth, of the bolted gate-tower of your city: that supreme Power, the door into the city that you are, the city all stilled and at peace and yet flowering into the whole world, brimming with the bliss of a risen universe. The last hard shell of forgetting hasn't split. Even now the great gate stands barred, and I am still outside it.

Your face is a lotus forever in bloom — the open mouth of Shiva himself. My whole mind aches to gaze into the heart of it, the deep middle of that flower, the secret of you where the supreme Power and you are one taste, the Shambhava form. Do some small thing, Lord — anything, even as if in play, the slightest gesture, that impossible little showing of the way — do it lightly, by which you flash forth standing face to face with me at last.

For one whose mind has melted into not being separate from you — what other happiness is there, what other glory? None. So why, then — why does the mind of one who calls himself your servant turn aside from that, leave it behind, and stagger off down the wrong road back into the ordinary?

I tell you, I would not be fit, not for a single instant, to be called a vessel of your service — not unless I drink, without a pause, with my whole heart's reverence, the heady wine of not being separate from you. If I do not drink that down unceasingly, I have not even tasted what you are, the solid mass of bliss, and I am no servant at all.

This man here, his sight smeared dark with the habit of seeing twoness, does not see your real form, your true body of dense awareness. And still — you who know everything, who melt for everyone who leans on you, kind to your own — still, why won't you hear me? I cry out, I wail to you. Show yourself — and even if the showing is slow, at least hear this much: the wailing of a heart gone helpless with love. That much, hear.

Do you ever once remember, Lord, my asking you for some pleasure of the senses — anything I went chasing, anything I begged for? You don't, do you. So now hear me, with no ceremony, the way one speaks to a master one knows to the bone: there is only one thing I want, always have wanted — the nectar of looking on your form, the elixir that is your own self made plain. That, and nothing else. Give me that.

The moment you set my foot, Great Lord, on your road, the path of Shiva, by your own will — that very moment a hundred hundred blessings rose for me. What else, then, would I go hunting for, my Lord? What is left to seek?

There, the great Night of Shiva conquers — that rare, unearthly Night, the ground of immersion in Shiva — into which the sun goes down, and the moon, and all the rest with them. A Night, because it draws in and dissolves the whole spread of illusion, the way night swallows the day. And it shines, that Night, by the welling-out of its own light, the blossoming of pure awareness. Into it the sun sets — the first stirring of the whole world, the out-breath of life — and the moon and the others with it, all the in-breaths, all the measures and the measured, sinking down into stillness.

I might rule all three worlds, lord of the gods — and I count it nothing. The whole of it is flavorless, dead, drained dry, with no taste in it at all, once it's drained of the one nectar: the touch of your feet.

Ah, Lord — how tight it is, this knot that binds me to myself, this stubborn conviction that I am only this body. And it's made of nothing but the not-knowing of you, and you yourself tied it — you, the worker of the impossible, fastened it hard; no other power could have. For there you stand, shining out plain in the immersion, blazing right in front of me — and this knot brushes you aside, shoves you down, and will not slacken by even a thread. In the broken daylight it props up the small self instead. You alone tied it; only you can loose it.

Worshipped by the great ones, lord of the gods, even while they worship you, you stand there as none other than the worshipper himself. Seen — outside in the rite, inside in the heart, the seeming difference only a flicker on the surface — you flash forth, always and only, as the very body of the one who sees. The worshipped and the worshipper, the seen and the seer, all one taste in the immersion: a single knower made of pure awareness. So it is. Blessing.

## Five — Pointing to Your Own Power

Lord, all I have done is brush against the lotus of your feet — one touch of those rays of yours, one taste of being taken in — and already I'm clinging. So take me the rest of the way. Drag me, if you have to, by the scruff of the neck; haul me home into your own house, which is nothing but the bare nature of awareness itself.

When will it happen — when does the day come that the dust of those feet, the golden pollen of your highest power stirred to grace, settles on my head and dyes my very hair, so that eight powers wake and shoot up inside me, and I, drunk on the sheer joy of it, break without warning into a wild, boundless dance — flinging my limbs, shaking off forever the small cramped sense of being just one more knower lost in the veil?

You are my one Lord, and this is all I ask of you, ever — only this, nothing else. Let me be the man for whom you alone are the one worth longing for. Let me live housed inside you, sunk into your dense, pure being — and let me be struck dumb that way, wordless, rather than be some learned man who knows everything and has none of you.

Oh — ocean of nectar, my master. Oh — you sweetness, you who fall straight into the place of wonder, three-eyed one. Oh — you taste, you unbroken honey, Virupaksha. Let me just dance, crying your names out loud, naming you plain.

Let me come alive at the touch of your feet with my eyes half-closed and turned inward, swaying, reeling — drunk on the wine of love for you, the liquor of *bhakti*, devotion, that floods a man and tips him into a vast openness when he sees you face to face.

The mind is a mountain — hard, unbudging, the storehouse of every old habit. Lord, let me find some clearing on the slopes of that mountain, some level place where things finally come clear, and live there — there where that one thing waits I have already tasted, that great savor, that unbroken thick murmur of you, a whole way of being that is nothing but the constant naming of you.

There's a city — yours — and inside it you stand in countless forms, together with your Goddess, your supreme power, from the high inner palace of pure intuition all the way out to the gate, which is the senses meeting the world. Let me be a citizen of that city, settled in it, taken up into that form of you that is awareness itself.

Lord, let those rays of yours blaze out — the kind, grace-giving powers — let this heart-lotus of mine open wide and spread, just so it can worship you, just so it can sink into your feet.

Be gracious to me, Lord — the way still water clears itself, all the mud settling out on its own into pure stillness. My mind has fallen at your feet and stays fallen there, rolling in them, tasting this and this and this of you, all of it past saying, swelling past size — until I'm like a man dizzy with drink, like a man melting clean away.

Out of sheer joy, or even out of grief — out of a wall, out of a clay pot, out of anything outside me or anything within — break into the open, my Lord, show yourself. I have no fixed spot where I insist on finding you. Come from wherever you like. You are the one who can rise up out of anything at all.

Outside me and inside me both, let it never stop flowing toward me — that utterly cool nectar, the touch of the lotus of your feet.

The touch of your feet is to sink into the power of Rudra; and that touch is a lake of nectar, an ocean of the elixir that never dies. To drown right down into it — let that be my one strange joy, always: a pleasure that overrides every other pleasure, leaves them all flat and tasteless, even the pleasures the gods would envy, every delight short of Shiva himself.

Everything I have, Lord, I lay before you — desire, anger, all of it, the whole haul I've been gathering since the world began; I hand it over and hide nothing, holding nothing back. Take it. Take it up into your own self-luminous being, draw it close, and then bathe it in the nectar of your supreme power until it turns to nectar itself — and feast on it, together with all who love you, all of them turned to your own substance in that hour.

Master, you are forever full, forever satisfied, having swallowed all the worlds whole. So take your ease — take the seat of bliss — and for one instant turn the look of your grace upon us, your servants. Bestow it. Let us be fit to hold your light.

Eyes shut over the inward thrill of devotion, savoring it deep in the full sense of "I" — let me move through the world saying *homage to me, to Shiva,* until I'm worshipping even the blades of grass underfoot, seeing every last thing as Shiva.

Let me win it — your own being, oneness with you — and then, seeing this whole universe as nothing but the welling-up of my own self, which is Shiva, let me never be cut off from the rich flood of devotion's savor. May no proud little voice claiming "this self is mine, alone" ever set itself up as an obstacle in my love.

Lord, there is nothing left over for you to want, nothing else you could reach for — and just because of that, you, the one without a second, are rightly and wholly full. Complete, because there is nothing outside you to crave.

There's a place where it's all laughter and dancing, where craving and loathing and the rest are eaten clean up, where the nectar of devotion is drunk down. Let me reach that place — where the inward joy is so vast it shakes loose the old sense of being only a body, and swallows desire and hatred whole, and the one thing tasted is the spreading bliss of sinking into you.

Let it strike deep roots in me — that strange, this-world-has-never-known-it gladness of thinking of you, the one flower whose scent I want, the fragrance of dwelling on you — let it take firm hold in my mind, and let it last until the stink of every bad old craving has died out of me completely.

Where is the worth in clinging to desire and the rest of that crowd — and where is the worth in clinging to the lotus feet of Hara? They don't belong in the same breath. So teach this heart of mine, this heart that loves the contradiction — drawn to you when it sinks in, drawn to the world when it rises out — teach it, set it straight, so that even when it surfaces it drops its taste for craving and stays in love only with you.

Even while I'm walking the high stages of yoga, even while I'm pulled back and turned away from every object of the senses — let my heart still be exactly this: reeling, loosened, made tender and dizzy by the wine of dwelling on you, refusing to settle and harden into any one stage, drunk on you alone.

In speech, in mind, in every plan and judgment, in everything the body does, in every act the senses carry out — everywhere, always, let the savor of devotion go out ahead of me, the first thing to flash up, leading the way.

*Shiva, Shiva, Shiva* — Lord, while that name of yours is told over and over without end, let me stand there tasting some nameless thing, some great savor that says itself again and again and is never once a repetition — always new, always new joy.

In the universe that shines as boundless awareness, where every dead, dark, inert thing has been drunk down to nothing, where there is no other city standing apart, no second realm of any kind, because outside the one shining awareness of Shiva nothing exists at all — there let me roam at will, flooding through it in the great absorption, worshipped, your worshipper, given over wholly to the worship of you who have no second.

My own being shines plainly, always, never failing — pure awareness itself, dense light, eternal. To rest in it I need nothing at all — no meditation, no muttered prayer, none of it, because it is already exactly what it is. Let that be my conviction, rock-firm, unshakeable, mine — cleansed as I am by the dust of the pollen of your feet. Let me be one solid mass of the absorption that never sets.

There is some nameless thing, Lord, that flashes up in the heart now and then — for those who carry the touch of your power, the touch of your feet. And where it flashes, this whole universe melts away into a sea of highest bliss, a lake of nectar, and the heart turns to that and nothing else. That thing — that nameless something — give it to me, always, so that I become one unbroken mass of the bliss of absorption that never ends. So be it. Peace.

## Six — The World Flashing Forth

Lord, even for a single instant, when I am torn away from you, I burn — not a little, but all the way through, scorched to the core. So let it be that I never lose you for even that long. Let my sight stay forever fixed on you, the one place worth resting in. And know this about the burning: it isn't the world's heat. It isn't that I've been denied some pleasure out there and ache for it. It's the helplessness of a love gone deep, a man set on fire by you alone, suffering the loss of you and nothing else.

This whole round of birth and death is, at heart, a long separation. So in the middle of it, let me never be parted from you — you who are my Beloved, my Master. Let me stay always merged into you, even if that means being cut off from the entire world. And I mean the entire world: every level of it, from the dust under my feet up to the highest, most luminous tier just short of you. Cut me loose from all of it, gladly — because once a man is truly absorbed in you, the world has already quietly set, the way the sun sets, and is simply gone.

Wherever I go — with my body, with my voice, with my mind — let me find that everything there is only you. Nothing but you, all the way down, because the one real substance of it all is a single awareness, and that awareness is you. Let this truth not just be true in some far-off way, but let it stand complete in me always, lived and tasted, so that I meet you fully wherever I turn.

You are beyond all the wavering and the second-guessing, pure awareness with nothing dividing it, brimming over with a vast joy. Let my speech be just like you, then — undivided, full of that same great joy — so that when it rises to praise you, it is praise worthy of you, fit for what it sings, your own nature answering back to you in words.

Let me walk this world drunk on you — entered by you, possessed by you — seeing, in thing after thing after thing, nothing but you. Each object, each being, every single one, made of you. And so I'd go wanting nothing at all, asking for nothing, because I'd already be filled to overflowing with the sheer delight of it. That's the secret: it's the joy that empties out all craving. When a man is that full, what is left to want?

My Lord, let me see this whole vast world made full by you, brimming with your presence — and with that, just that, I am content. That alone satisfies me completely. So you need never trouble yourself on my account. I won't come pestering you for little powers, for this trick and that gift; I won't crowd your peace with my begging. See the world full of you, and be content with that, and let you, my own Self that is pure awareness, rest undisturbed.

Let all these things — all the world's appearances — keep dissolving back into you, the way little shreds of cloud melt away into the open sky. They rose out of you to begin with, so let them sink back into you, even down to the last faint trace of themselves. And as they go, let them shine for me, always, drifting steadily into a clearer and clearer purity — for that fading is really their homecoming, the showing of what they always were: nothing but awareness, nothing but you.

And out of the very heart of this world, let one nameless jewel shine for me, unbroken, without pause — you. A jewel whose own outpouring radiance has already shattered the long encircling dark, ray by ray scattering the whole sprawling night of not-seeing, the dark of forgetting one's own Self. You are that jewel, the one that grants every longing. So shine in me steadily, never withdrawing, blazing out in full absorption, until there is no edge of darkness left.

Is there any ground you don't stand on? Is there anything at all that is not your very body? Then let me be done with the long exhaustion — worn out by ages of wandering this world, simply because I never recognized my own face. Let me find you without effort, without all the strain of meditations and rituals and disciplines, find you anywhere I happen to look, from any direction whatsoever — because there is nowhere you are not, no inner thing and no outer thing that is not you. So how could I miss you? You are already, everywhere, the only thing there is to find.

To hold your body, to be folded into your embrace — that touch of utter absorption in you — let it come to me by my own will, freely, whenever I please, not as some rare accident that visits once and is gone. And if that much is granted me, my Lord — if I may have you like that, at will — then tell me: what have I not conquered? What is left for me to win? I, who stand here as the highest of all, having gained the only thing worth gaining.

Show yourself. Come out into the open, blaze into the open light. We will not insult you, our own Self, our own pure awareness, by wearing ourselves out with other prayers, begging you for this and that. No — sick with longing for you, undone by a love we can't control, it is you alone we hunt for, you alone we go searching after, and no lesser thing. So show yourself. Shine forth. And in that shining is peace; in that shining is Shiva.

## Seven — Triumph in the Dark Times

Here is the one thing I'm asking for, Lord. Let me merge with you — sink into you the way a river loses itself in the sea, into you who are the ocean of joy itself. Just once, fully. And let that one merging do the whole job: lift this great ruin off me, this whole enormous wreckage that grows out of one thing only — the sense that I am separate, cut off, a thing apart from you. Take that away once, the way you pull a splinter so it never goes back in. Once is enough, if it's done all the way down.

Because the root of the wreckage is hard, and it's buried deep. It's the chain that says *this is mine, that isn't* — this pleasure I'll grab, that pain I'll shove off — the whole machinery of wanting and hating that locks me to the small self. That chain has a thick, stubborn root underground. So let the axe fall on it from the inside. And the axe is one thing only: the steady conviction that I am made of you — that there's no gap, that the awareness in me and the awareness that is you are a single light. Let that recognition come down like a blade and split the root.

Let the smudge dissolve — the whole grimy film of thought-construction, of *this-not-that*, that stains awareness only because it loves division. Let it run off me like dirt under water. And in the cleared heart, let something rise: a fearless, unhampered freedom, nothing holding it back, no second-guessing. Lord, let my own conscious form — the very fact of my being a knower, an "I" — stand flooded, drenched, overflowing with the taste of bliss.

I've fallen. I'm rolling around at the bottom of the world-egg, this round shell of existence made of passion and craving, tumbling down and down inside it the way an unhatched chick lies helpless in the shell. So let devotion be my mother. Let love for you be the mother-bird, and let her feed me — drop after drop of the purest, highest joy, those rich essences poured into the beak — until my wings come in. Until I'm fledged, until the feathers of real knowing and real action have grown strong, and I'm a bird at last: a thing that belongs to the open sky, the clear unstained sky of pure awareness, and can fly there. The chick the mother feeds in the shell — that's me; and the same bird, fed full, lifting off into the open — that's me too.

And this mind of mine — let it learn the one skill worth learning: the connoisseur's knack of tasting. Let it become an expert in savoring the nectar that pours out of thinking on your feet — meditation on you, which is itself the finest distillation of joy there is. Let my mind grow expert in that one delight. And in growing expert, let it finish off the other thing entirely — let it use up to the last drop that old appetite for the poison of the world, the craving for objects that's been a venom in me, a habit of swallowing poison and calling it a treat. Let the love of your nectar burn that habit out at the root.

My mind is a jewel — a sunstone, the kind of gem that catches the sun and answers it. So let it catch your light. Touched by the hot rays of devotion's sun — which is just your own presence flooding into me like sunlight — let my mind-jewel fling away, hurl far off, every last spark it's been carrying: the smoldering coals of passion, hatred, all of it, embers too small to even see but hot enough to burn. Let the sun of love throw them clean out of me.

And then, in that place — the highest place, the path of the supreme Power, the topmost reach — let me stand and sing to you. Let me praise you there forever, at the top of my voice, lifting verse after verse to your face. That place — where Vishnu and Indra and Brahma themselves, the great gods, are still standing *outside*, waiting at the door, hungry to get in and not even knowing the way. There. Let me be inside, singing, where they are still only waiting.

And let me see. By the wild, lit-up play that comes of being drunk on devotion — that intoxication that opens the eyes wider than sober seeing ever could — let me look out through these ordinary senses, my eyes and ears and hands, and let the looking be whole, nothing left out. Let me see the entire world, every last thing in it, as Shiva — as you, as nothing but you. And every act I do, every word and thought and motion of the body, let it all be worship: not worship I perform, but worship it already *is*, the pure delight of awareness spilling over into form.

And last — devotion herself. My mind has taken her as its own, claimed her the way a man takes a bride and makes her the mistress of his life — and she's no ordinary woman; she's the queen of the inmost circle, too precious and too hidden for the common crowd to even see. Let her bear her sons. Let her give birth to the great powers — to becoming small as the atom, vast as all space, light, far-reaching, and all the rest — not as foreign gifts but as her own children, already alive inside, just brought out into the open. And rooted deep now, settled and flourishing like a wife with strong sons and a strong house, let her make one conviction unshakable in me: *all of this is mine — this whole world is mine, and no one else's.* Let her drive that certainty down till it can't be moved.

## Eight — A Strength Not of This World

When, oh when, will the two of them grow up together and ripen into one body? There's a tiny grain of your grace resting in you, Lord — and even that smallest grain matters, because without it no devotion in me would be possible at all. And there's a thing in me that's almost devotion, a faint reflection of the real thing, too small and unfinished to be called the whole. They belong to each other, those two — a sprout of grace from you and a sprout of love in me — leaning toward one another the way two young lovers lean, joined by a fullness of feeling. When will they take root and settle, and grow into that one body of perfect bliss?

Let some joy be born in me — a relish, a deep inner contentment that comes from nothing but chewing over your lordship, tasting again and again the fact that you are the Master. Let that, and only that, rise up and shine in me here in this world, at every hour, even in the ordinary scattered hours when I've come back down from absorption. The grand machinery of knowledge and yoga, the heights of attainment and all the powers that crown them — let all of that stay far away. I don't want it. I want only the sweetness of you.

Let my thirst for things stay wide and full, Lord — as full as any ordinary person's, hungry for the sights and sounds and colors of the world. I'm not asking to go cold and dead to them. Only let me, with every trace of separateness gone, see these very things as your body — as nothing but the living "I" of you, who are pure awareness. Let me look on the whole sensory world and find it is you.

In the rough stages of the body — old age, dying, all of it — and in the mind with its endless inventing, and in the road the breath travels with its touches of pleasure and pain, and along the many winding paths of awareness, the blue and the yellow, this thing and that thing, this whole world broken into pieces — in all of it, stand out for me as my own Self. Let it be you, vivid and unmistakable, shining there as my own deepest "I."

Let these powers of mine — the goddesses of my senses, eyes and ears and all the rest — wake up with a strength that is not of this world, blaze with their own light, and pour out freely into their objects, each into its own field of color and sound. Let them run wild. Only this, Lord: never, not for a single instant, not even a hair's breadth, let me do the reckless thing — let me never crack or lose the taste of being undivided from you. Let the senses range; just don't let the union break.

Light as can be, with the heaviness of the veil fallen away; smooth, soft to the touch; white and clear and cool with the density of pure light — let me become all that, swept up in the rush of being possessed by you. And being that, let me go right on past the whole ordinary traffic of things — past the busy commerce of every knower and every known — moving through it all and never once caught by it.

Let my own form unfold — my form, which is nothing but you. Let all the worlds, from the densest earth up to the highest heaven, come and be my own limbs, become one with me so completely they never rise up as separate again. And then let all of this, all the prancing of duality, this whole show of things-set-apart, pass on into the unsayable — so that even if it drifts back across memory, there's nothing left there to point at and name.

And let it rise in me — that flood of supreme nectar that pours out when I drink in the sight of your face, that face which is grace itself looking on me, the highest Power turned toward me. Let that flood swell up in me so it can do the one thing I need: fill the awful chasm. For there is a cavern in me, a terrible bottomless ravine — the not-shining-forth of oneness with you, the failure to see that you and I are one Light — and it gives off nothing but the dread of this whole turning world. Let your nectar pour in and drown it, and let me see, face to face, that you and I were never two.

Will it really never happen — that someday, just from being touched by the faintest, thinnest spray of nectar that comes of union with you, I'll finally turn my face away from all the world's little pleasures? Turn away from every one of them — and yet not fall into the other ditch either, not drop out of the joy of you in the bargain. Will I really never come to that — free of both, fallen from neither?

Forever, without a break — for I am the great swan that takes its joy at the lotus of your feet, the swan that lives where those feet are, that birthplace of the highest Power — let the lotus-fiber of devotion come up to me to feed on, from above, from the root below, from the very heart of it. From everywhere in you, let that thread of love rise into my reach. (The swan, Lord, is the soul; and the soul comes home to your feet.)

Let all things whatsoever come to me, Lord, as the field where thought plays — and let them come, too, as what the eye drinks in directly, the very ground of seeing. Let everything I look at and everything I turn over in my mind, both the wordless seeing and the worded thinking, shine out as pure nectar — let their innermost essence be nothing but the elixir of awareness, clean of any stain of "keep this, throw that." Let them shine like that on every side, perfectly, and let that be all.

In every hardship, Lord — whatever troubles come crowding in, one after another — let me meet them, and not only meet them: let me be a man whose fear is gone, a man brimming with a strange surplus of gladness, because I have touched you, been taken up into your Power. So much joy that even when the sorrows rise it never once breaks or dims — that's the surplus I mean: a steadiness that the worst of it can't disturb.

The whole universe is held inside you, threaded on the single thread of your awareness; and whatever shines as if it were outside is shone out by you alone, by no one and nothing else. This is the truth I have held close, served with love, tasted in absorption, fixed in me past all doubt. Now — right now, even here in the scattered ordinary state — let it stand plain and shining and obvious before my eyes.

## Nine — The Triumph of Freedom

When will my mind drop everything else — every web of imagining — and turn, hungry, toward the touch of you? When will it be soaked and dripping with the fresh new sap of devotion, aching for the one taste it wants: the long sweet immersion in you?

I'm in love with you alone — not with powers, not with glories, just you. The one wealth I count as vast is the right to worship your feet, to tend the rays of you and nothing more. I can't bear even a moment of coming back down into separateness. So when, when will I see you face to face?

When will my mind go free — let go of every inward technique, the muttered syllable, the breath-work, the fixed meditation, all of it set down — and come to you? When the great bolts crack open all at once, snap, every chain of the veil and ignorance breaking in a single instant past all mending, then I'll reach you, and be one with you, undivided.

The whole circle of deities — Brahmi and all her sisters, all the goddesses who rest in the heart that is the core of one's own awareness, light and the knowing of light — they hold the bound souls captive, feeding on them. Lord, when will I master that whole wheel of power, not by force but by the sheer strength of devotion to you?

When, Lord, will the bliss-sap of overflowing devotion well up in me so high that even the small separate gladness of the world, and the very name of it, becomes something rare and precious — the last faint sign of a perfect merging?

The Lord — seat of every glory; fearless, because nothing stands against him; openhanded, because he gives all things; full, because he wants for nothing; uncaused, because he is eternal — and the great hider, who for no reason at all takes on the shape of this whole world and so veils his own face. One who is not the Lord might hide himself; he need not, and yet he does. When will I suddenly know him — break through in a flash of the Lord's own descending grace and see him plain — and so shame this master of mine, my own true Self, by showing he was hiding in full sight the whole time?

When, Lord, will I come into some little share of being dear to you — some ordinary grace — so that wherever I am, it would no longer be fitting for you to slip away from me, to hide yourself anywhere at all? May you stay always turned toward me, always entered in, never gone.

Truly, every living being, whatever small thing it does, is at the root performing worship of you — for each rests in its own innermost godhead, which is you. When will I be flooded, drowned, carried away on the bliss-sap of devotion that pours up out of seeing them so — out of recognizing my own Self in them all, and being gladdened by the sight of every creature, each one already worshipping you?

Devotion to you is the highest ground that knowledge can stand on, the topmost state that yoga can reach — the very oneness, the falling-together into the supreme awareness that is Bhairava. Lord, when will my longing for that devotion be made full and complete, all my purpose accomplished?

When, all at once, will I come upon the great treasure laid up in your blessed feet — seize it, grip it hard and make it wholly my own so I never fall back into separateness again — and then, helpless with joy, flooded with the highest bliss, go out and show that treasure everywhere, fling open to all what lay hidden, the storehouse of your supreme power?

When will I be made wholly of your light — the pure, undivided light of awareness spreading out on every side — so that nothing is left of me, not the smallest hollow where the shadow of the veil could fall? Maya is only a shadow; let no trace of it stain me.

When, Lord, will I become the one who has gathered all the worlds into himself — every realm from Sadashiva down to earth folded into the single light of awareness — standing without a second, dependent on nothing, the foremost head of the whole company of those who love you?

Lord, you are the strange uncaused ground of every claim a creature ever makes for itself. "I am the maker, I uphold, I destroy; I am the learned one, the hero, the sacrificer" — every such swelling pride, in every soul, is only you, the light, raising it up out of nothing by your own freedom. The doing is all yours; no Brahma truly creates, no one is truly the scholar or the brave man — it is you who stir that sense of self in each, here and there. So when, by your will, will I be filled and overflowing with the sap of devotion, holding fast to the one great certainty: that I am the All, the dense mass of awareness and bliss, Shiva himself? And even then — even with that great pride attained — the sweetness lives in standing before you as your servant.

When, Shiva, will I rest at your cool lotus feet, held steady there in the firm embrace of a glory that leaves nothing out — the goddess of devotion herself, full to the brim with the whole world — and lie down at last in that cradle of your feet?

When will I cross to the far shore of the wealth of worshipping you — that abundance brimming with the wine of devotion, the loveliness of resting wholly in you — and so, when, when will I become a man whose life is fulfilled?

With a flood of joy-tears spilling over, my voice stumbling, wandering, broken and choked, crying out the long inarticulate cry, my face open and bright with laughter — when will I drink down the sap of your touch?

When, Shambhu, will I shake off this state I share with the herd, this cattle-existence of the soul fallen into separateness, and taste instead the form that truly belongs to your devotee — my own nature, the ever-risen, supreme bliss, mine and no one else's?

Having gained the powers — the shrinking-small and all the rest — yet free of every fever and dread that clings to them when they're sought in separateness, untouched by the fear of death that even the great gods can't escape — when will I be the one who lives only to drink, in play, the elixir, the nectar-medicine of devotion to you?

Lord, when will that cry — that one, the kind there are no words for — rise up of itself in my voice, so that the very next instant your form blazes up before me, shining out in the fullness of the merging?

My whole heart given over to the one craving — to embrace, again and again, more and more deeply, the lotus of your feet — when will I, with no effort at all, no meditation, no muttered prayer, simply look upon you, and look upon this whole world of being and non-being, and see it all, truly, as you?

## Ten — Never Cut Off

You who are the one Lord of the whole world — this is the one thing you must never let stand. That your own devoted ones, the people drowned in you and made of you, should be counted as equals with the others, with Brahma and the rest of the gods who still live inside division. That's the thing you can't bear, and you shouldn't have to. It's against your very nature, because you alone, with no second beside you, are the world's Lord. To rank the ones who are full of you alongside everyone else — no. That cannot be allowed.

For there are those who follow at your feet always, out of sheer love, simple helpless love. And wherever they go, in whatever state they find themselves, they're already tasting some draught of the highest joy. They're tied to a ray of you, and that ray feeds them everywhere.

Where you are the master — you, who are Time itself that swallows time — how could any sickness reach a man there? Every disease, every wound that Great Time drives through a body, dies at the root the moment the Lord who eats time is present. And where you are, who would still hunger for some lesser pleasure? What would the splendor of a Sadashiva's high seat be worth, next to the splendor that is yours — the wealth of the one undivided Light, with all division swallowed up inside it?

If even for the space of a single instant some soul lays hold of you — through one tremor of full absorption, one flash of being lost in you — then in that very instant his whole stretch of time gets flooded with your joy. Even the ordinary hours that come after, when he's risen back into the everyday, get poured full and steeped in it. Once a man has tasted the bliss that no clock can measure, the rest of his life is dyed through with it.

A single drop of the nectar of your joy, fallen here onto the earth — that is the moon. A single spark thrown off from your fire, your destroying brilliance loosed and spreading — that is the sun. The moon is your bliss melting and running down; the mind and all it grasps dissolve into it. The sun is that point of devouring fire, the spark that eats all difference, gone forth as light.

So to your third eye we bring our offering, Lord — the eye where moon and sun are kindled and made to blaze, the one true mark of a greatness past all telling. A greatness so far beyond the ordinary that even Brahma and Vishnu can't find their way to it. Into that great fire we hand over everything — even this little self that thinks it's the body, born of the veil. We lay it down at the third eye, which is the single unmistakable sign of you.

By that very rapture, you are seen. Whoever, by the bare touch of beholding you, brims over into joy — somewhere, in some act of worship that knows no division, that one has truly seen you. And whoever, by some other path — the soundless way, where you think of the thing that no word can utter and your mind alone closes on it — feels even a flicker of that joy: not the cheap joy of one who still worships you as separate, but the real thing — by that flicker, too, the dense mass of awareness that is you has been glimpsed and recognized.

For those you've smiled on, Lord — those who have won your heart, who've taken hold of your own nature, light and the self-knowing of light — they reach into you, into your fullness, into your pure awareness, and they draw the whole outer world back inside. They pull the universe in and make it awareness again, fold creation shut like the two halves of a box. For them the whole world rises out of awareness and shines as nothing but awareness, with no seam of difference left in it.

Take you away, and look at what the universe becomes: a flat dead thing, all seen with the same level, divided, two-eyed stare — a mere object, no more. But you, you are its Lord, one and without a second, the one who looks with the uneven eye — the third eye, the burning glance that scorches all division to ash.

And as for the rest — let it go. Without your power, nothing so much as exists. Take those who build their cases against you, the Buddhists and the Samkhyas and the Mimamsakas and the rest. Their very arguing to refute you is itself your own awareness, flashing as the one who refutes. Without that — without you — not one of their clever proofs could even get off the ground. So let their whole heap of reasoning lie there like a man already thrown flat, kicked over before he's struck a blow.

Outer breaks, inner breaks — the contacts and partings of the body, the wanderings of the mind that block your own rest in yourself — let all that fall away, and let my awareness stand only in you, in the bare clear place with none of those interruptions in it. If I could only hold there, fixed on you, lost in you with nothing else cutting in — Lord, what else would I ever need? What's to be had would already be had.

Some men, my Lord, go wandering lost in their very own self, and they are wretched in the extreme. They take the self for nothing, or for dead dull matter, and so they reel from birth to death to birth, on and on, never finding the one who knows. And some men wander too — but in that very same self, and they could not be more at home. They roam through their own awareness and nowhere else at all, settled past all unsettling, one solid mass of the highest bliss. The same word for both, wandering — but one is a man lost in the dark, and the other is a man at play.

And here's the wonder, Lord. Even those who never drank the nectar of devotion to you — who tasted it and felt nothing, who never even glanced your way, never recognized you as awareness itself for one moment — even those creatures, sunk in birth and death, come to perfection. They come to the highest attainment by the mere doing of what your tradition lays down, the bare following of the path. The scriptures themselves say it: sometimes, through devotion, through simple practice, the Lord of all the worlds reaches down and lifts up even the bound. The plain force of it is enough.

We are your servants, Lord. So, just as you carry the three worlds — just as you hold and feed and nourish your own self, since the worlds are nothing but you — in that same way you are surely bound to hold us up and feed us too. And here is why. You bear the whole universe inside you without division, and we are made of you, every bit. Yet because we grab at the body and call it "I," we go on living as though we stood apart — never knowing the world for what it is, undivided you. And that is exactly why you are all the more bound to hold us and nourish us. Because we are your servants. Because we forgot.

And even when I've seen you — even when I've recognized you as the very soul of the world, made of the nectar of the highest joy, shining out as blue and yellow and every color there is — even then, even so, I ache for you past all bearing. For the touch of you, the taste of you, the flooding nearness of being utterly lost in you. I have seen you, and still I am sick with longing for more of you.

Lord — all the griefs there are, every last one, all the pains that shudder through every creature from the worm to Brahma himself — even those become bearable. Even those can be borne. Because they come to a man yoked to a thing called holding-on — that grip, that stubborn brace of "this much I've got today, this much I'll get tomorrow, this is what I'm after" — and that holding-on is yours, is made of you. So even the pain, joined to you, turns sufferable.

You are all-knowing, you are all-powerful, you alone exist, and you are pure awareness. Then how, Lord, does this whole spread-out world get to show itself at all — this world that, on its own terms, by space and time and use and doing, never actually appears, never truly exists outside the light? It has no standing of its own. Yet there it is, spread out. And the only way it can be is this: it shines because it shines in you — in the awareness that is free, and all-powerful, and lights up everything.

These powers of yours stir and shine, every one of them — the movements of intellect and mind and sense — though in themselves they're as dead and dumb as clods of dirt. You breathe life into them; that's the only reason they flash at all. Without you they'd be nothing. Watch how it works: tufts of cotton, mere weightless scraps, blown up high by the wind, go dancing and playing across the sky. Just so, these dead faculties leap and dance — but only because your breath is in them.

So tell me, Lord — if the soul didn't fall into the mistake of planting its sense of "I" down in these dead faculties, what could ever dim the plain truth that the whole world is one with you, you and nothing but you? Nothing could. For your devoted ones, the whole universe flashes as your own single self. The only thing that ever hid it was the soul mistaking these stirrings of intellect and mind for "me."

They are worthy of worship too — even the great ones who went under at the dissolution, the very ones who were destroyed. Kala, Kama, Tripura, Andhaka and their kind. Not only the ones you blessed face to face, your loving devotees — these too. Mighty beyond all the world, and yet swept off into ruin, they were made holy by the touch of the fire of your wrath — that wrath which was only your play, only grace wearing the mask of punishment, breaking them in order to lift them. Burned clean by it. Worthy of our bowing, all of them.

The body of the Great Light stands there, plain and unmistakable, present on every side. So tell me, Lord — why, why do I keep wandering off into the dark? With the all-illumining brightness right here, vivid, everywhere at once — how do I still go slipping back into shadow, swept helplessly down into the ordinary daze? (This is the cry of a man who hasn't yet held the ground he saw — and his not-yet is not the Lord's lack.)

You yourself are the undivided whole, and you are the deathless nectar that is my own true nature. And yet — even so — I am the one and only home of every mortal frailty there is. All the grasping and the letting-go, all the strain of a dying thing — somehow I alone become the seat of all of it, when by rights I am nothing but you.

Whoever has this for the jewel of his speech — the name-mark, "Maheshvara, Lord, ruler of Brahma and all the gods" — and who carries on his bowed head the sign of obeisance, the lowering of the self before the higher Self that knows it for what it is — that one, and that one alone, the single undivided one, is bound fast to you and made great by it. Marked with the bow, sunk deep in you, rich in devotion, lifted into fame by the very touch of oneness with you.

Being and non-being alike — all of it is only you. So then, Lord, why shouldn't the same attainment come to me, with no struggle, no machinery of method, by sheer sweet nature alone? You are the world of what is and what is not. So why isn't your face turned toward me always, freely, springing up of its own accord, without a single contrivance — why is that not simply, eternally, mine?

The servant of Shiva, who is one self with Shiva — what happiness could there be that he doesn't reach? Lost in you, made of you, he becomes the very mass of the highest joy. There is nothing he cannot come to. For it is I who feed even the chief of the gods their fill — even Brahma and the rest, even the bright powers seated in the heart and the senses — I pour out to them the wine of the deathless, the floods of undivided light and joy that surge up the moment the world stands clear in awareness. They drink from me. I am not some beast to be fed upon — I am the one who fills the gods.

You sit between the heart and the navel, in the hollow there, the very fire of the belly in every living thing. You are the great fire, and so you devour it all — everything that stands still and everything that moves, the whole world. By the burning that draws in food and even the outer light of the body's heat, you eat all of it. Through that fire set in the belly of every knower, the Lord is the eater of the universe — and that, in the end, is what truly is. That is Shiva.

## Eleven — Longing and Trust

This whole world — and friends, and family too — none of it is really anything to me. Not because I scorn it, but because none of it is other than awareness, and awareness is the one thing I am. And when I see that all of this, every last bit, is only you, shining — then who is left over to be a second thing? There is no one else. Even the world turns out to be my own face.

My Lord, Great Lord, you yourself are, plainly and directly, the whole world — there is nothing to add to that. And yet here I am asking you to become real to me, to show yourself. Do you see how strange that is? You already are everything. So the very prayer — "let this come true, let you appear" — is, if you think it through, a kind of embarrassment, an asking for what is already wholly here. The petition almost shames itself. I make it anyway; but I make it knowing it is, in truth, redundant.

To be lord of all three worlds — even that, here and now, looks like a dry blade of straw to anyone who has tasted being absorbed in you. So tell me, Lord: what fruit could there possibly be in all his good deeds, any of it, apart from the simple remembering of you? None. There is nothing else worth getting, because the only thing worth having is already had.

Nothing whatsoever stands apart from you — how could it, when everything that shines, shines as your light? Even the makers of worlds, Brahma and the rest, are only you stretching yourself out. And just because of that, even in your most astonishing acts — flinging whole universes into being, drawing them back again — no hymn of praise can quite get going. For where would the praise stand? There is no praiser set off from the praised, no song apart from the one sung to. You are the one praised, the praising, and the praise, all at once. That is the truth hidden in why the song keeps failing to begin.

I am made of you. I live for your worship, laying the whole world at your feet, and I tell myself I am at it always, without a break. I hold to you like that even when I rise back into ordinary life. So why, my boundless one, am I not simply that in dream too — carried by my own sweet will into worship even there? Why, when sleep comes and the deep impressions wake, am I not as lost in adoring you as I am awake? Why don't I melt into you there as well?

Take the ones who have been brushed — even barely, even by the faintest stray drop of fragrance lifting off your lotus feet. To them every pleasure there is, the whole heap of enjoyments, right up to the delights even the gods go begging for, the things the highest powers themselves can't reach — all of it starts to seem stale, flat, a spoiled and sourish smell. That is how thoroughly, for the ones fully absorbed in you, the craving for any attainment gets carried off and set down far away.

In your heart there is nothing but one thing, Shambhu — and your word and your act are that same one thing; there is no double-dealing in you. So for one who is truly real, truly good, only one of two things is ever yours to do: either grace, or refusal — embrace or rebuff — but only one, cleanly, never a mottled in-between. (And the words carry a second shadow: grace and refusal are also your folding the Self away and your opening it out — the closing of the eyes and their opening.) Do the one thing. Don't leave me in the half-light.

I am deluded. I am wrapped in suffering. I am afraid of age and decay and all that rot. I have no strength of my own. I have taken refuge in you. Shambhu — so act, and act quickly, in such a way that I come at once to the highest place of all, the throne of complete absorption in you, and leave the road of sorrow behind me for good.

My poor cries, my thin little wailings, the moment they reach the country of your ear, take on a worth past all price. They are next to nothing in themselves — but reaching you, they are lifted high. The way stray drops of water, fallen by chance into the hollow between two joints of bamboo, come up wearing the rank of pearls.

What is there that even they don't get, Lord — the ones who, even for a single moment, even as a sham, even by some trick, find themselves caught up in your name? Even they receive everything they could want. So you, with the cool moon set in your hair, the one who carries off every fever and burning — do this for me: make me one whose death is cut away, untouched by any untimely end, so that I come into the glories, the powers, smallness and all the rest — true greatness, real lordship. For even Brahma and the great ones, decked in all those powers, still die. Lift me past that. Make me deathless, a vessel of the dense bliss of awareness, holding the powers in their non-dual fullness.

Shambhu. Sharva. You with the moon-sliver in your crown. Shiva. Three-eyed one. You who wear the rosary of beads. Beloved Lord, marked with the fierce skull-sign, blazing terrible trident in your hand. Sea of compassion. You whose very nature is the burning power that makes and shapes all three worlds. Shrikantha — swiftly, swiftly destroy this whole load of impurity, this swarming of separateness, and grant me the supreme attainment, the perfect, non-dual bliss that has no second.

What is there at all, Lord, that the Blessed One is not the maker of? Could any single thing so much as be, any conscious being even exist, that Shankara does not hold sway over? It is all yours. And so — here is the bitter end of it — clinging always to you, who are the Supreme Lord, undiminished great power, I and I alone go on suffering, broken without pause in this round of birth and death. The whole world rests in you; only I keep slipping out, and only I keep aching.

Yes, the pains are here, and old age, and death, all of them puffed up and overbearing — fierce things, even the proud ones say so. Let them sit a moment; let them stay if they must, for now. Even the things the world prizes are unsteady, sound and the rest of it forever sliding away. And even so — even knowing all that — I still long. I am greedy for unbroken joy. I crave a long, long lasting life, one steeped in the taste of the highest delight, a life whose whole heart is the meditation on your two lotus feet. That is the one life I want, and I want it because it alone is worth wanting.

O Lord, master at undoing the pain of all who bow to you, treasury of every good, Dhurjati — for me now, who am nothing but a house of grief, trembling at birth and at death... act, so that the highest unmoving powers — the ones that give the sweet taste of all that delights — grant their grace, even here, even in this life, while I am still living and breathing, not after the body falls.

Homage to the one whose single, unsharing work is this: the smashing of the great dark of delusion, the night of the veil. Homage to the light that outshines every light there is — fire, moon, sun, all of them — the light beyond all lights, the one fittingly marked by the moon. To him, this peace.

## Twelve — Pointing to the Secret

You need no helper to show yourself, and nothing in the world can block you from sight. Everything there is, is already flooded through and through with you. So how, even now, do you still not let me see you?

Past all the things there are, past the first stirring of the senses, past the very middle of every act of knowing — there you are, forever rising of your own accord. Let me look on you that way: turning my back on the whole splintered show, seeing only you.

How are they born — those people, and how, by whatever grace, do they ever fall into your line of sight? Rising up, again and again, out of every single thing there is — out of stone and water and a blade of grass and every object in the world — you come welling up, and you drench them in flood after flood of the deathless nectar that streams from the sight of you.

They have looked on your form with their own eyes and been filled with the nectar that pours from it. Their thirst is torn out by the root. Drunk on you, they wander wherever delight takes them — while everyone else stays a slave to their own wanting.

Not "always," not "then," not "just once" — no thought of time fits it at all. This seeing of you isn't something I can call eternal, and I can't call it anything else either. It simply isn't a thing time can lay a hand on.

My heart opens like a bud at the sight of you — let just this much of yoga, this much union, be mine for good: that by nothing more than the leaning of my will I may walk straight into your house of nectar, your home of the highest joy, and worship you there.

When your seeing arrives without a single dividing thought, the minds of the great-souled ones flower wide open. And out of them, easily, almost carelessly, come spotless deeds and spotless words, plain for all to see — the kind that can lift a whole drowning world across.

Lord, let me live right there, in the very midst of your two feet — your power to know and your power to act — and so, unafraid, through field after wide field of becoming, let me move with nothing holding me back, worshipping you, my Master, in every state I pass through.

Sunk deep in the cup of your lotus feet, melted into them, every other craving gone quiet — every longing but the ache to be wrapped in your own light laid to rest — let me wander as I please, drunk and contented all the way through on the wine of that bliss, going from step to step, taking each new place as your ground.

Even the man in whom the will to worship you rises only for show — even his — that nearness you give is certain to dawn on him in the end, and it is exactly what befits you.

Lord — couldn't I, with a heart that leans on nothing else, a heart turned to one taste alone, simply drink you down to the last drop? You who are so easily found, you who rest beneath everything and hold it all up — could I not drink my fill of you?

You have set everything aside — and all of that very same set-aside world, once known as made of you, is taken up again as worthy. *Reject it as not-you; receive it as you.* That is the whole gathering of the secret, the heart of every teaching.

Whatever lives within you, held in oneness with you, is worshipped first of all, as the Lord himself — and rightly. But what could ever stand outside you? Outside you there isn't even a nothing, not so much as a blank. So how, Lord, could anything be out there — and how could anyone worship it?

Soundless, free of every dividing thought, never scattered, never broken off, unceasing — even in the churn, even while the whole traffic of knower and known goes pouring past — let me look straight at you, and you alone, on every side: you who are nothing but the one light of awareness, O three-eyed Lord.

Show me your own home, God — the place where you live forever, joined always with your supreme Goddess. For aren't the servants who stand always at your feet, level with the very dust on them, the ones you can trust most of all?

You have come into my sight — and yet how could you ever draw away from me, your servant? I have laid hold of you and there I stand. Not for one instant are you out of reach: of what living creature are you ever, even once, not the object of its seeing? Everyone catches you sometime — but I am the servant who never once lets you go.

By the clear, deathless stream of the awareness that we are one, flowing out unbroken forever — by that flooding, O all-pervading one — let me bring you, and this poor narrowed self of mine, into perfect oneness, no seam left between us, and so come at last into pure joy.

Out of this "I" of yours — this "I" that has gathered every world into itself, the very marrow of all knowing, the highest of all that I am — let just an atom, a grain no bigger than a fleck of musk, come and settle into me, so that by it I may become one who worships you, swallowing thing after known thing into the fire of that "I."

Seeing this boundless form, this thing and that thing, fresh in every passing moment — let me see well and truly that all of it is you, my own Lord, wearing the face of the whole world.

Why doesn't my mind, roaming after this prize and that, just wander forever in *you* — since whatever it chases is already your own body, made of awareness, and so already the thing it most wants? Let it roam like that. Nothing in its nature gets hurt by it; my own will to drink you in only gets filled all the more.

There are hundreds of them, Lord — though out of thousands, only a rare few — who, just by your grace, even with these plain eyes wide open, even as they go about the simplest farmhand's work, keep right on seeing your form there before them, always.

No thought rises in me that isn't made of your will. Even what looks unlucky is being done, just so, by you. And so I am made of you, Lord — however I move about on the earth, wherever I go, I stand always in the unbroken festival of worshipping your spotless, untroubled feet.

In your deep, hidden sayings, let true insight dawn in me — fresh, clear, never twisted — rising up before me. And right behind it the power to live those sayings out; and behind that, an endless, unbreakable passion for your worship.

Even out in the ordinary marketplace of getting and doing, let this whole crowd of things keep shining for me always as your own limbs, parts of you — and never as something I'd prize on its own, blind to the fact that it too is you.

When my mind, of its own sweet will, goes running day after day across the fields of its objects, wherever they fall — let it spread out and yet never grow restless or greedy, and let me stay forever skilled, deft, sure-handed, in the one work of serving you.

Lord, it was your will alone that made me your servant — no other power had a say in it. Then how is it that even so, again and again, I cannot find the beautiful mirror of your face? And isn't that the strangest thing.

Those who burn with longing for you, who catch sight of you in object after object — what is it that comes to them, ah, what could it be? What practice, what means, ever ripens into that fruit? It can't even be spoken; only the one it happens to can know.

Let all things be — as present, as absent, however they come — but let them be, for me, only ever filled with you. And so let nothing at all exist, if it would exist as anything other than you.

What the world calls "nothing, not a thing" — let that be nothing to me as well: let the whole world of separateness be the nothing it truly is. And what the world calls "something, a real thing" — let that be, for me, the only Something there is: that single matter witnessed by my own experience and no other. Either way, by just this much, you are found everywhere, and everywhere worshipped — Shiva, in everything.

## Thirteen — The Whole Thing in Brief

Listen, my Lord, to the whole of it, brought down to one thing — what pleasure is, and what pain is, as they stand in me. My pleasure is this: meeting you, coming together with you until we are one. My pain is only this: being apart from you, my Master — not recognizing your own nature, and calling that separation my grief. That is the entire account, the long story folded into a single line.

There is still something in me — and it is exquisite in its own way, this faint dark film, the first thin smear of not-seeing-you, the residue of forgetting you that has clung in me even through the deepest absorption. Even that, Lord — wipe it away from everywhere, inside and out, and let your own spotless nature, which is mine and everyone's, blaze clean and open.

And this, in truth, is what I long for most of all. Your body is the whole world, brimming and full; it is made of the nectar-juice of pure awareness; it never perishes. Let me stand in that body always and always worship the Lord — and as for the rest, whether life comes or death comes or anything else at all, let it come. It doesn't matter. Standing in you and adoring you, I have no concern left for which state I happen to be in.

For the one who loves you — only for him — this kind of pride actually shines: *I am the Lord. I alone have beauty. I am wise, I am blessed and fair; who else is there like me anywhere in the world?* When the one who has entered you and become one with you says such things, that proud self-celebration glows, because it is your own infinite "I" speaking through him. In anyone else the same boasting is small and soiled. But in your lover it lights up, because it is no longer the small self talking — it is you.

God of gods, Lord of all that is — there is an awareness that is born when the not-seeing of our oneness, the failure to know your deathless non-dual joy, is finally dissolved; an awareness that takes each thing exactly as it stands, sees every object as the single light of consciousness. By that awareness, make me fit to worship at your feet, fit to be one of your own rays adoring you.

The Supreme Lord himself is meditated upon, and then he is seen, and then — himself, of his own accord — he is touched, held in the closest embrace, made utterly one with the one who adores him. Where that happens, there is a festival of fullness, an overflowing celebration. Let that festival be mine always, my Lord, by your own grace, through the working of your own touch in me.

And so I am made of you. However I move about on this earth, whatever I do, I stand unceasingly with this festival of worshipping your flawless feet — and no one, nothing, can push that joy aside or take it from me.

In your deep, deep sayings — the words that look on the surface like talk of separation but carry a hidden secret folded inside them — let true insight rise in me, clear and unmistaken, ever-fresh and new. And out of that insight let the power to actually live what they say rise too; and out of that, an addiction to worshipping you that never once pauses for breath.

Even down in the marketplace, in the thick of ordinary dealings, let this whole crowd of things keep shining to me always as your limbs — as parts of you, your body, never standing apart from you. Let me never again take any of them on its own terms, as if it were a source of happiness in itself, worth wanting for itself without knowing it is made of you.

Let my mind run wherever it likes, day after day, ranging out over all its objects — and even when it runs and reaches and grabs, let it not get greedy, not get tangled. Let me stay, always, skilled and deft at one thing: the service of you, the worship of you, no matter where the mind wanders.

My Lord, it was by your will alone that I became your servant — no one else had any power in this; the grace that fell on me was your own free act. And yet, even now, even having been given this servanthood — how is it that I still do not see your beautiful face, this lovely path of the supreme power, when I drop back into my ordinary embodied self? Strange, isn't it.

Those who burn with longing for you — who turn toward you through one object after another, seeing you in each — ah, what is it that comes to them, what rises up in them then? And what could ever be called the means to it? It cannot be spoken; only the one who tastes it knows. We can't even imagine what that path would be.

That seeing of every thing exactly as it stands, and that festival of worshipping you — these two lean on each other, each holding the other up: there is no true seeing of things without the festival of non-dual worship, and no such worship without that clear seeing. This pair forever unfolds and spreads in those who are rich in devotion, because you yourself light up both of them at once.

Through this sense and that sense, through every gateway of perception, without a break, let me keep drinking the elixir, the heady wine of worshipping you. Every single thing in the world is a cup, a drinking-bowl — and when all these cups are filled to the brim with the nectar of oneness with consciousness, let me drink and drink from all of them and become drunk. That is my one prayer; that is all I ask of my Lord.

Where there is not one atom of anything to be known as separate — where the whole of it, all that is grasped and all that grasps, blazes up as nothing but self-luminous light — there, in that city of yours, that fullness of pure awareness, set me forever, your worshipper, in deep unbroken absorption.

You set me in the place of a servant, Supreme Lord, by your own free will, and you did it yourself. And then — have you not made me a vessel by letting me see you, by the supreme realization where you reveal yourself? Have you not made me worthy even by the work of tending and pressing your feet, by the gladdening flood of your power coursing through me?

At the moment grace fell on me, you never paused to weigh it or deliberate — you never do; that fall of grace is wholly free. So what has come over you now, today, toward me — why do you delay in the one thing that must surely happen, the showing of your own self-luminous nature? Don't put it off any longer.

In this object and that object, you shine outwardly; and inwardly too, in this pleasure and that, you shine — always joined with your supreme Goddess-power, brimming as all three worlds. Let me always look upon you so, and behold you, worshipped by my own hand. For the hand is power itself: as the old teaching says, "the hand is declared to be Shakti" — and worship offered through that awakened power is the worship that truly befits you.

With nothing more than the turning of my will toward it — no recitation, no ritual machinery needed — let me climb, freely and without obstruction, into my Master's high palace, that most longed-for height of pure power. And there let me be, always, drinking the supreme nectar-wine of your grace, brought at last to perfect rest, full to overflowing with joy.

Into all the lovely, desirable things of the world, by the merest touch you pour a flash of wonder, a thrill that lights them up from within. And so those who are rich in unshakable devotion worship that very thing — your body, the body of awareness — adoring you through it, and coming to rest in you.

You unfold the whole universe out of yourself, shining as it; you touch and savor the entire world by savoring your own nature; and when you whirl, spun by the joy of your own essence, the whole field of beings rises up and dances into view. The world's blossoming is your own delight overflowing.

The one who looks on this whole circle of objects, Lord, having made it free of separation, and sees the entire thing as your body, your very form — for that one, in a world filled to its edges with his own Self, forever happy, where could any fear possibly come from? There is nowhere left for fear to come from at all.

Even the world-poison lodged in the corner of your throat, Lord — even the great black venom — is, to me, the highest nectar, because it sits there touching your body, shining as one with you. As the saying goes, even poison turns to nectar. But the actual nectar of the gods, even offered to me to drink and keep, I would refuse — if its way of being still tasted of separation, still failed to touch the non-dual seeing of you. Anything that breaks your oneness, however sweet, holds no appeal for me. It is not what I want.

Let my face be lovely, lit up and graced by an ever-busy mouth pouring out sweet, beautiful little songs — songs flushed with love, breaking from me on their own in the helplessness of absorption, all of them just babbling of you. And along with that, let my heart be forever wholly taken, won over and held, by my dearest beloved: the very act of worshipping you. Let her be the one who has my whole soul, and let me be one who knows her through and through.

Ah, the play of the Supreme Lord cannot be reckoned, cannot be counted up — it is sheer wonder. And so it is with me: this body, this nature you have given me, brimming with deathless nectar — you will not let me simply go on drinking it as I please, without pause. You keep sending me back down into the ordinary waking ground again. And that is exactly why this longing in me never ends.

You — unfathomable, beyond all measuring, free of every division, without a second, the very Self of everyone and everything, the awareness that devours all objects whole. Entering you, O Lord of Uma, master of the supreme Goddess, let me always worship you, and praise you from every side, with the whole of my being knowing you as one with itself. And that is bliss.

## Fourteen — The Hymn of Victory

Victory! For one moment let me stand here, right in front of my own Lord — the very treasure-house of all glory, the resting-place of every splendor — and let me drink in, like a draught of pure nectar, the sweetness of crying out his triumph. Just for a moment, again and again, I taste it; and the joy of it is so close to hand, so endlessly easy to reach.

Victory to you — the one Rudra, the one Shiva, the Great God, the Lord of all. The old word says it plainly: there is one Rudra, one Shiva — not a crowd of separate gods, the way people imagine when they think the world is split into pieces. You alone, with Parvati at your side as your own highest power, the firstborn before all the shining ones, the eldest of every god there is.

Victory to you, sole sovereign of the three worlds, marked with that one unearthly sign no other being carries — the eye blazing in your brow. Victory to you, whose throat still holds the dark stain of the poison: when the churning came up bitter and the whole creation, gods and demons alike, stood drinking down its agony, you took that black venom into your throat and bore it there — and so you carry the world's pain on your own neck, and lift it off the rest of us.

Victory to you, whose hand flashes with the trident that is your three living powers made solid — the will, the knowing, the doing — a sharp blade that cuts clean through the round of birth and death. Victory to you, whose lotus feet are worthy of all worship — feet at whose mere wish the whole purpose of a thing is already accomplished, the wanting itself enough to make it done.

Victory to you, who wear a body beyond this world, streaming with a hundred kinds of radiant grace. Victory to you, who took the slender thread of Ganga thinned to almost nothing in your single matted lock, and crowned your head besides with ash.

Victory to you, anointed all in white, as if smeared with the cool moon-glow spread shimmering across the sea of milk. Victory to you, adorned by the great serpents — Shesha, Vasuki and the rest — their hoods bright with the jewels that rise from the very touch of your body.

Victory to you, whose only fit companion is your own self — for nothing matches the sliver of the unwaning moon except you, who are unwaning and undivided too; that crescent on your brow is only a sign of what you truly are. Victory to you, forever being crowned and consecrated lord of the whole universe, the stream of Ganga pouring down the unending anointing.

Victory to you, the touch of whose lower limb made the cattle-world holy — for the bull you ride was touched by your feet, and from that touch every creature of its kind became pure. Victory to you, who never fail to come and stand among those who gather in love — wherever the devoted draw close in fellowship, your nearness is certain, sure to be there.

Victory to you, who in sheer play put on the garb of an austere ascetic and so threw the foolish into confusion — they took the matted hair and the rest for penance, some saying you wore it to cleanse the sin of striking off Brahma's head, others that you wore it to win some power, others still insisting this was your true and final form. None of it is so. The free, self-luminous Light that you are could never be bound to any such shape. Victory to you, the one fit vessel of that beauty everyone longs for — the loveliness worthy of Gauri's embrace.

Victory to you, greedy for one gift above all — the offering of a heart gone soft and melting, drenched through with the sap of love; the moment it is held out, you make it your own. Victory to you, delighted by your devotees gone wild and roaring with that same intoxication of love, contented by the song and the dance of them, by their garlands of shining praise.

Victory to you, from whom the very power of Brahma and Vishnu and Indra and all the great gods — their power to make and rule and unmake worlds — itself rises and falls away. Victory to you, whose command the whole rank of world-rulers, the line of guardian-gods led by Indra, carries upon their bowed heads — for in every rite they take their stand only by following your order.

Victory to you, whose sovereign power stands revealed because your own seal is stamped on all that is — across the whole of creation no being is found but bears the mark of god and goddess, and so this entire universe is yours. And leave aside the gifts of glory you hand to Brahma and the rest: to everything that exists you give its very self, its very being — for without your self-shining nature nothing could so much as be. You give right down to the self itself, Lord of all — and just because there is no other to lay claim to being, you are the one great Lord.

Victory to you, who in the moment of willing the three worlds into being had no second beside you — no helper, no raw material to lean on, nothing. If there is no second, people ask, how can we speak of a power and the one who holds it? Because in bearing the full weight of that sovereignty, your only companion is the Goddess — your own innate might, your own highest power, by which the whole journey of the worlds is lit up at your sole wish. That is your lordship: the doing of all five great acts.

Victory to you, who in one timeless instant — all at once, with no before or after — wholly overrun and fill the three worlds. Where Vishnu took the three worlds in measured strides, you pervade them in no steps at all, instantly, completely. Victory to you, whose name "the Lord" is sung in one unbroken voice, sung even by little children, with no quarrel, no contradiction anywhere.

Victory to you, whose greatness is sheer and inborn, owing nothing to mercy or to any other virtue, leaning on no quality at all — for ordinary greatness is measured by saying a thing is high because it tops something else, but yours rises from nothing outside itself, unlike any other. Victory to you, the strange new Bhairava who swallows down even the dread, world-shaking Great Death — terrifying to the very thing that terrifies, and yet, shining in the heart of those who fear death, the giver of fearlessness.

Victory to you, against whose fierce blazing act of dissolving the world nothing can stand in the way, nothing can block it. Victory to you, the speaking of whose name draws a hundredfold of every blessing running after it.

Victory to you, who in idle ease handed over to your devotees this whole sea, this very mine of nectar — as once you gave the ocean of milk to Upamanyu. Victory to you, the sudden devouring fire that flares up to dissolve the universe — whose flash of wrath, gone in an instant, is enough to end it all.

Victory to you, the one lamp of this world of living souls blinded in the dark of delusion — the dark of simply not seeing, where every sense of oneness has been folded shut; you alone are the light that shows them what is truly real. Victory to you, the inmost Person who keeps watch wide awake over a world fallen asleep — when the whole of creation lies dulled and drugged in the swoon of Maya, you stay forever awake, forever the watching Self at the center.

Victory to you, the life that quickens the living things crying out in the dark caves of their bodies — for the body, being inert, is like a mountain hollow, and the souls calling there are like the *jivajiva* birds that cry in mountain caverns; you give them all their life. Victory to you, the supreme royal swan, the one fine waterbird gliding in the open sky of the clear minds of the good — for the heart of the devoted, washed pure, is itself a sky, and there you move and play.

Victory to you, whose mountain-lord Meru towers up, heaped with gold and bright with veins of living ore. Victory to you, who to the wicked are like a cursing meteor, a falling star of ruin, an omen-moon — for when your dissolving power descends on them the wicked revile you, and the reviling itself becomes the cause of their undoing; yet under the form of the moon you are forever the spring of pure delight.

Victory to you, so hard to reach that even sages worn thin by cruel penance and even the great gods cannot come near you by that road — for as it was said before, neither yoga nor austerity is the way; love alone is the way. Victory to you, seen face to face by those steeped in love through every state there is, waking, dreaming and deep sleep alike.

Victory to you, who make those who take refuge in you the vessels of your own overflowing wealth — handing your own people the taste of that supreme bliss. Victory to you, whose single purpose is the cherishing of all who turn to you — for of those forever devoted you have said, *their welfare and their keeping, I carry it myself.*

Victory to you, whose one great deed is to be the sole cause of the making, the holding and the unmaking of all things — always, only for the joy of the play. Victory to you — our highest master, whose very name is Utpala — the great festival of the blue lotus of play, the lotus that love sets trembling and swaying, longed for and pervading everything, when devotion floods in and overflows.

Victory to you, vessel of all victory — victory to you who have conquered birth and old age and death — victory to you, eldest and first of the world. Victory, victory, victory, victory, victory, victory, victory — victory, victory, victory, victory, victory, victory, O three-eyed Lord.

For to you alone belongs all victory: you are pure awareness, and so the highest of all, and "victory!" is the cry the soul makes to turn its own face back toward the Self it had hidden from while it was lost and busy among objects — a calling-home. You have conquered birth and old age and death; you are the eldest of the world, for you are without beginning. And that crying of it over and over, *victory, victory* — that helpless flood of it — is the very mark of a heart swept away in love, overwhelmed. O three-eyed Lord: the name itself proclaims a greatness past all common measure. And so — blessing.

## Fifteen — The Hymn of Devotion

There are scriptures that wash away the three stains — the stain of being a small separate self, the stain of the veil that splits the world in two, the stain laid down by all our old doings — and there are people who have read those scriptures cover to cover and can quote you the whole of them. There are yogis. There are scholars. There are the ones at peace in themselves. But when you look at what these words actually point to, your devotees are the only ones who are any of those things in truth. They alone have crossed to the far shore of the teaching. They alone are the real yogis, because they live always plunged in you. They alone are the real scholars, who know the one knowing that is your own power and your own being, and rest in the bliss of it. They alone are truly at peace, settled in their own nature the way you are — opening and closing their eyes on the world and, by that very rhythm, making and unmaking it, never once stepping outside themselves. All of it, every bit of it, belongs in truth to your devotees and to no one else.

Lord, the rest of the world goes around being fed by Maya and time and necessity and limitation and craving — propped up, drop by drop, by the very things that bind them. But your devotees feed on something else. They have come into their own boundless nature, the one no time can measure, the one that fills everything and wants for nothing, that knows all and does all. So on this shore of the world they alone are the happy ones — full, brimming, moving through their days in joy.

These devotees of yours — weeping or laughing, it makes no difference — cry your name aloud, in every state they pass through, calling out to you at the top of their voice. Their wailing and their laughing are themselves the clear seeing of you. They are the only real devotees, and for them the mere shout of your praise is the whole of worship, the whole way of serving and adoring you. Set apart from everyone else, they stand outside the ordinary run of people entirely.

I don't want to be one who has renounced the world. I don't want to be a lord swollen with powers. I don't even want to be the man who worships you in order to win his freedom — each of these in his own fitting way coming to you with a request still in his hands. None of that. Let me be the one drunk on the wine of an overflowing love for you — reeling, undone, lit up with a joy that pours out of the rapture of being merged in you, asking you for nothing at all.

The one who takes the whole outer world and gathers it home into his own heart — into that inmost place that is nothing but light and the light's own knowing of itself — keeping nothing back, leaving not a scrap outside, and from there worships you with floods of the nectar of devotion: Lord, to that one I bow.

Right in the middle of what the world calls virtue and vice, what it calls knowing and not-knowing, pleasure and pain — there, in the thick of the very things that toss everyone else around — your devotees are tasting something. Something nameless. Some matchless, surpassing sweetness no one else has any word for. Oh, the wonder of it.

Father of all that moves and all that is still, my Lord — even the blind, even the lepers, even those the world turns its face from, shine. They shine gloriously, decked out in the one ornament that outshines every other: their wild, unstinted love of you.

A man may live on gleaned grains and a heap of fallen feathers for his bed and board, his limbs worn to shadows by want — and still, Lord, blazing with the great heat of devotion to you, his own nature flaring up bright and strong, he lords it over the lord of riches himself, overpowering the king of kings by sheer inner wealth.

Some, my all-pervading one, can no longer even keep their footing — they tumble and roll in the rain-softened ground of love for you, the soil drenched with the nectar of supreme joy — and yet, still reaching, still climbing toward you with a hunger that needs no prop, they worship you with the heart alone, not with outward flowers at all. These are the great yogis. They stand all around you, inside and out, come to rest in you everywhere.

This must be guarded. It must be grown. It must be prized above all things, Lord — this great treasure of devotion to you, the one wealth that lifts a soul clear of the long misery of being born and dying again and again. Guard it, so no slip back into the scattered mind can rob you of it. Grow it, step by step, opening it wider as it sinks deeper in. And hold it dearer than anything else there is.

Lord, your crowd of devotees is in love with you — every one of them, like a lover with her beloved. So let her, your own supreme Power who is mistress of them all, lay her jealousy aside and be always glad, always blossomed open, holding them all to her at once.

Once love for you takes root and you yourself start to come near, the day of actually becoming you is already close — no need to go on begging for it. When you've got the great pot of milk safe in your hands, all that grasping after a little curd is wasted, pointless: the milk holds the curd already, folded up inside it.

Is this not itself the one matchless attainment? Does the deepest happiness not come flooding out of it on every side? — this devotion to Shambhu, deepening and deepening as it grows, until it ripens into the love that never ends, the love that is itself the goal. Yes: it is the one attainment beyond compare, and out of it the highest joy pours and pours.

Here is the pity of it. In this grimy mind of mine — fouled by every fall back into the scattered, ordinary self — the jewel-vine of devotion to you lies sunk and smothered. It is the vine that mothers every perfection; its branches hang with gems. And buried in my mud it cannot put out the splendors that are its own by nature — those uncanny, more-than-human glories, the powers to shrink small and grow vast and the rest of them, which it would unfold of itself if only I let it breathe.

Devotion to you, the Blessed One, the Lord of the three worlds — surely that itself is the great attainment. And if someone says it isn't complete, that it still lacks the little powers, the shrinking-small and the rest — well, that gives me no worry at all. I am not troubled in the least.

Outwardly and inwardly both, the yogis carry the fragrance of the bright, full-bursting flower-cluster of the three-eyed Lord — pure, perfumed through and through by their worship of you within and without. And just by living near them they perfume everyone close by, even the ones whose hearts had set hard against you, turning them too toward your worship — the way a single sweet-smelling bloom scents a whole hedge, even blossoms of another kind.

Is there no light here, then — nothing at all you could put a name to? Is the whole wide world sunk in deepest sleep, every scrap of it gone? Yes — for here, Lord, in this place that is the very night of Shiva, where all the world's chatter of names has been drawn back in and the spread of all our partial knowing has gone dark like nightfall — here in that dark your devotee worships you, forever.

Let the pure light shine out and spread wide in the worship of you — Shiva, the Blessed One in whom every true power really lives. Let the rays stream up from the crown, the bright dust shaken from Shambhu's feet, dancing in the topknot. And let the dark rise too, swelling up only to burn away clean every last trace and seed-print of craving, so nothing is left to be born again. Or — better still, Shambhu — let the whole weight of the three worlds simply dissolve away into you, every last thing coming to rest, melted utterly into your Self.

The road of this wandering world stretches out endlessly far. My poor frame stands scorched by every kind of fierce disease. The good things were never truly mine to enjoy; and whatever happiness did come never stayed for long. So here I am, turned out useless, a wasted man. And yet — my head has been touched and made radiant by the press of the moon-crowned one's feet, the crown of me lit up and made beautiful by your grace; and I am yours, your devotee. So because of that, and quickly, give me the great riches — the lasting ones, the boundless, undivided wealth that is nothing other than you.

## Sixteen — Cutting the Bonds

For ordinary people, there is nothing — nothing at all — standing between them and you, no veil over your face. And for those who love you, there is nothing between them and you either. The words are the same, but they mean opposite things. For the worldly, "nothing" is bitter: between them and your true nature, your light-filled Self, stands the whole universe — everything, split into pieces, the full wheeling machinery of separate powers thrown up like a wall, so that nothing of you gets through. For your lovers, "nothing" is sweet: there is truly nothing in the way, because for them every last thing, right up to Shiva himself, has become their own body, their own to hold.

You can be reached by a slow climb of methods — knowledge, ritual, breath-work, the disciplines the scriptures lay out, step after step. And you are thick with attributes beyond counting: all-knowing, all-doing, made of every power. And yet — to those who love you, you flash up whole and pure and all at once, a single clean taste of awareness, never coming in pieces, never coming late. Reached only by stages, crowded with a thousand qualities, and still arriving instantly and spotless: it sounds like a contradiction. It isn't. That is just what you are.

Some people, Lord, are winning, and they laugh — they open like flowers, because winning never went to their heads, they never made it a separate thing to clutch. And some are losing, and they laugh too — even dragged back out into the ordinary world they keep blooming, carrying the taste of you with them. Drunk on the nectar of loving you, they laugh at victory and defeat alike. There are a few like that. They are not of this world.

Don't let me be dry. Don't let me be "perfected," don't even let me be "freed" — not if it comes dry, drained of the rapture of loving you. The cold liberation the dualists prize, their tidy enjoyment and their tidy release — let those go. Fill me instead, fill me to the brim, with the sweetest thing there is, that flooding bliss of merging into you, risen to its highest pitch. Soaked in that, the dryness simply ends.

This taste of loving you that has welled up in me — I never knew anything like it before, not in a thousand lifetimes. It came on me suddenly, out of nowhere. So now, Lord, let it grow. Let this one thing flourish and feed itself fuller and fuller — you alone know the way it should.

In all honesty, Lord — and I say it out of sheer closeness to you — I have no other prayer than this. Just this one: let that nameless plunge into love, the kind no words can reach, be mine, and be mine always. Nothing else.

Let me, drunk on love, even rage at this round of birth and death — look on the world with disgust, the way you'd recoil from something coarse. Let me ache with regret: how could I have stayed so blind for so long? Let me laugh, bursting open with joy. Let me weep, dissolved in tears of bliss. Let me cry out "Shiva! Shiva!" until my mouth runs over with it. Like a drunk man, let every kind of feeling rise up in me, one after another — that is all I want.

Let me be brought low and let me be at ease, weeping and laughing, deep and unhinged — all of it, Lord, by loving you. Beaten down by hard times, and still resting in my own joy, watching the world's little pleasures as a man perched on a heap of needles watches comfort, seeing them as the pain they are. Weeping at a friend's deathbed, and inwardly blooming because the awareness within has opened wider. Laughing along at the world's celebrations, and pitying them. Looking deep and grave to the people around me while I'm secretly out of my mind with the touch of you; and looking witless, gone, lost to memory, while inwardly I'm flooded full of the bliss that fills the depths. Let me be all of these, by love.

For those who love you, there is nothing — inside or out — that stands apart as a mere "thing" to be known. Everything is your own nature shining, awareness itself; and at the center of all of it, you, free of every thought, stand of your own accord, needing nothing else to hold you up.

Even when your lovers must mouth some insult — playing along with the petty slander that battered, small-minded people stitch together — even then they thrill. Inwardly they are pierced through, from every side, by sharp little needles of rapture, the gooseflesh that breaks open the bonds; and where the rest of the world would feel a sting, they feel your nectar falling on them, drop by drop, and they only bloom.

For those who love you, even pain turns to pleasure. Even an aching, wounding feeling becomes something to savor — because for them all awareness is wet with nectar, made of your moonlight. Every sensation, even the bitter ones, opens into the wonder of being the one who tastes, the dense and blissful knower at the heart of things; so suffering itself becomes a delicacy.

Wherever your lovers are penned in — in pleasure, in pain, in whatever causes them — there is, plain and unmixed, free of the muddy stains of old habit, the one even sweetness: the taste of touching your very body, which is nothing but awareness. And it is the same everywhere — equal in all of it. As the old saying has it: the one who meets everything with an even mind is the one set apart.

Lord, in the worship that is loving you, there is still a thread of self-abasement, the little crouch of "me down here, you up there" — the posture that belongs to seeing two where there is one. A few cut even that away. They tear out that last shred of lowliness, and then, with the difference dissolved, they taste your body itself: clear, spotless, made wholly of nectar, the pure essence of bliss. For love split in two still aches toward Shiva, still wants what it thinks it lacks; but love made one is instant merging — it has already arrived.

Those who go by the scriptures, the seekers at the sacred fords, are lost — and being lost, they fall into camps, sect against sect. For division itself comes only from being lost; it's bewilderment that dreams up the lines between things. There is one Reality with no rival anywhere, nothing to set against it — and for those who love you, that one Reality is you, blazing.

Take a man whose mind has been scoured clean — burned utterly free of honor and insult, of craving and aversion, every seed of them charred past sprouting. That is what loving you does to a heart: it cooks it down to a stainless, flawless thing. How could such a person ever be like the common run of people? He couldn't.

The dark of craving and hatred — even that has been beaten back in some, conquered by the sheer blaze of their love. And before these great ones, these towering souls, what are the merely learned, the ones who only know? The knowers and the lovers reach the same wide ground; but the lovers have this over them — they have routed the darkness of craving and hate.

For the one whose every act — bathing, drinking, all of it — is fed by the nectar of loving you, there is, at the beginning and the middle and the end of whatever he does, a high seat of ease, a resting in the deepest bliss.

You alone are the one worth praising, the one to set the mind upon, the one to seek, the one to worship — you are all of these. So for those who love you, even the daily round of living, the ordinary going-about, is something to be celebrated; for it has all become full of you.

What they call "liberation," Lord, is only love for you ripened to fullness. We who have just set foot on its first step — even as we strain toward greater and greater heights, we have already found our footing here. And so we are almost free. By a hair's breadth, fully free — not yet free, but very nearly.

Let pain come to me in floods, Lord — let it pour, as long as my whole self stays brimful of loving you. But a happiness that turns its face away from you — even an unbroken stream of it — let it never, ever come.

You are pleased by love; and love only comes alive once you are pleased, Lord. So the two lean on each other, each waiting on the other — and how that knot ties up rightly, you alone know. You who do the impossible, by your own sheer freedom you make both happen at once. Here human logic simply gives out.

With form or without it, inside or outside — to those whose very self is drunk and glad with love, you are, every way and everywhere, made of nectar. For they see you as the Self of all, and so wherever they look, there you are, sweet.

Even here, in this very world — to the one who loves you, joy is the whole fruit, the lighting-up itself; and the rest of the world just stands there, something else entirely. To everyone else this world is a place of sheer terror, glimpsed only in passing. To your lovers the same world shines on like anyone's — but when they actually look into it, they find it is nothing but the dense bliss of your light.

Love in the hidden, love in the highest, love in the Lord of all the worlds — love in you, Shambhu, Shiva, God. Love — what an astonishing thing it is. Some matchless, uncommon thing, beyond anything ordinary.

Love, love, love in the highest — love, the very name of it, swelling, overwhelming. I cry it out at the top of my voice, because it has seized me past all holding: let love be mine, fierce and rising and overflowing, love for you above all else — love that needs no practice, no preparation, that simply leaps up like a flame already lit and blazing. And yes, it is right that it should.

Because you are the very source from which all loveliness, all radiance, springs — anything at all, the moment it clings to you, becomes priceless. Whether it's a jewel, prized as the finest of its kind, or a worthless blade of grass that nobody would stoop to pick up — let it touch you, let it be joined to you in that merging, and it turns priceless on the spot.

From the one who tells, to the very thing told, all the way along the path of awareness — those who suffer no parting from you anywhere on that road, those who serve you in love and never let go: they are the ones who triumph, the ones who belong to you.

Outside the great hall of the world, beyond the crowd of birth and death, some embrace you — yogis fixed in one still place, sunk in the closed-eyed trance where the eyes turn in and the world drops away. But others, my Master, embrace you right there in the middle of the hall, in the thick of the world itself — eyes open, drinking it all in, undone, shaking, every restraint thrown off, faint with the depth of their longing. For that is exactly how it goes with those who love hard enough.

By her — Shiva's own Power — who has drunk down the whole universe through draughts of guarding and keeping it, who has eaten it whole in the swallowing-back, who has decked herself in it in the showing of her splendor, who has feasted on it and made it her ornament in the great festival that is its dissolving — by her, in the wild rush and joy of that festival of unmaking, Shiva is held fast in a tight embrace. And that Shiva, awareness itself, fierce and tender at once, I bow to. I praise him. I sink and merge into him.

Your supreme lordship, Lord of all the worlds — it triumphs, and there has never been anything like it, because it has nothing left over it to rule: it has taken the whole universe up into its own self, so there's no separate "other" to be its subject. And your lower lordship too — the more outward face, the one that wears the shapes of the lesser gods — that triumphs in just the same unheard-of way, because it is yours. By it this world shines forth — and the strange thing is, the world both shines exactly as it appears, and at the same time does not appear at all the way it seems. For all this — every separate blue and pleasure and body — is only your light, melted into the one radiance of "I." These dead, dumb things have no being of their own; they are nothing but the shining. Only the light is. And so it stands, and so it shines.

## Seventeen — Wonder at the Divine Play

Oh — some nameless festival is winning the day. There's a sweet, riotous celebration of worship going on, and victory belongs to it, because it's so full of the taste of nectar that it brings even tears — tears that aren't grief, but the overflow of a joy too big for the body. That's where I want to begin: in the sheer wonder of it.

Every act a devotee sets in motion as the opening of your worship turns out to grant whatever it reaches for. But for those who love you, the truth is sweeter still: the acts don't just *grant* fulfillments — they already *are* the fulfillments, because the whole thing is made of you. Nothing has to be waited for. It's all already arrived.

There are those who worship you ceaselessly, always, in every state, finding you all at once in every form — you, who hold the whole boundless universe in a single timeless embrace. They worship you without a pause, in every time and every place. Those ones — they're my gods. The ones I'd bow to.

And here's the worship I want for myself, forever: not the kind won by the strain of meditation and muttered formulas and ritual effort, but the kind that simply happens when I touch you. Set all that labor aside — the chanting, the breath-work, the effort of holding an image in the mind — and what's left, the pure festival of your touch arriving on its own, that's what the masters have always called the real rite. As the old word has it: in the vast open sky of awareness, beyond all thought, worship is just this — dissolving, out of sheer love. Let that be my worship, always.

For those whose whole being has melted into the one taste — the recognition that they and Shiva are one — it's always the equinox, the holy hour when worship is due. Every moment is that balanced, sacred turning. And the nectar they pour in that worship is nothing other than the sweet rush of feeling you, you, you.

There's an act that has no beginning and no end, that doesn't even unfold in steps along the way — it rests, whole, in the immersion itself. That's the real worship. And only those who've tasted you, who know the truth of being absorbed in you, are truly the ones who perform it. No one else.

Even Brahma and the great gods — *their* lords are the ones I mean: those rare souls in whom the festival of your worship goes on standing even in dream, even in confusion, even when the mind has wandered. They're the truly fortunate ones, drunk on joy, the envy of everyone. Not only awake, but in dream and dreamless sleep too, the celebration of your touch keeps rising up in them on its own.

People chant, make offerings into the fire, bathe in holy water, sit in meditation — and that's fine, that's worship of a kind. But for the real lovers, it isn't only at those marked moments. Their worship of you is — oh — always, whenever, however, in every breath and every minute. There's no edge to it.

Those who are happy forever, drinking deep at the banquet of your worship as if it were the nectar of immortality — among Indra and Brahma and all the high gods, who is even their equal? Not one. The logic is plain: nothing the heavens offer can match it.

There is a great festival of your worship that, all by itself, shakes the whole universe loose — the body of thirty-six elements, gross and subtle, and through it the entire world, thrown into a sweet helplessness as its hard edges melt away. And in that upheaval, something is found — something whole, made of the highest bliss, one's own true self, the only thing worth reaching. What that something is, only the lovers know.

Standing in your radiance, the home that is pure awareness, I worship you with everything — with all thirty-six elements at work, with body and speech and mind and every act, recognizing each of them as a pulse of you. For the whole of this — flesh, breath, the thirty-six layers of the world — *is* you. The scriptures say it many times over: the body is made of all the gods. Earth is your hardness, water your flowing, fire your shining — and the Triple-Headed Lord stands here, directly, having filled the whole universe with himself.

Let it all flow on — every bit of my time, even were it endless — toward only this: that I stay happy, drowned in the bliss of being given over to your worship. I ask for nothing else. Let the whole of time, without remainder, spend itself on this one thing.

Let my hunger for the nectar-taste of your worship grow greater every single day — let me be that greedy for it — and let it always bear fruit, the fruit of carrying me higher and higher into it.

When the whole world dissolves, what's left is an ocean brimming with a single nectar-taste — and that ocean is you. Let me stand there forever, in that sea of you, worshipping you, the Great Self who fills all things. Let me just *stay* there.

In your sweet rite of worship, the lovers offer up their very minds — minds gone simple and straight, because every last knot of old conditioning has been cut clean through. No more crookedness, no more winding deceit. Just a clear mind, laid at your feet.

The senses themselves — eye and ear and all the rest, each a goddess in her own right — take hold of their objects, color and sound and the rest, and through them, in the rising rhythm of creation and keeping, they send up the nectar-wine of your worship from within. They turn even their reaching-out into an offering to you.

For lovers whose whole being is swept away by the great heat of devotion's surge — the blaze of love flaring up in them — what other path to peace could there be than to plunge into the nectar of your worship? There is no other. Drowning in you *is* the release.

So let this be arranged for me, my Lord, forever: the unending festival of drinking the nectar of worship at your feet — the worship that is itself the one sure way your grace arrives, your grace that is pure awareness and pure bliss, the clean shining of one's own true nature. Let it be granted. Let it just keep happening.

Let me taste it, O Lord — over and over, with every single act, moment after moment after moment — the great delight of drinking the nectar of your worship, that surging, brimming, intoxicating sweetness, and the vast joy that rises out of it, a joy that only grows greater the longer it goes.

For the lovers, the great labor of your worship is its own reward, paid in full on the spot — because right then, in the very moment of worshipping, they taste a happiness that should have been impossible. No waiting for some later fruit. The joy is *there*, in the doing.

Until I'd won this festival — the sweet, intoxicating taste of the nectar of your worship — I tell you, I hadn't tasted so much as a drop of real happiness. The ordinary pleasures of the world aren't happiness at all; they're counterfeit, manufactured things. The only real wealth of joy is this uncontrived bliss of being absorbed in you.

For the lovers, that standing in your radiance arrives in worship effortlessly, on its own, blazing up without the strain of hunting for ritual objects — no scrambling after flowers and incense and sandal-paste, none of that searching that is itself the labor. It comes free of all of it.

The lovers have nothing left to attain, and nothing they find hard to reach — for they've already gained their own full nature, which is the whole of Shiva. So they simply wander the world, drunk and reeling on the wine of your worship, doing nothing for any purpose at all, just gloriously free.

Oh, the largeness of heart in those whose minds are flooded with devotion — and the strangeness of it: there's a way of worshipping you, the giver of every boon, that I can only call praiseworthy beyond all the rest, because it is never once stained by *asking* for anything. These are the ones who, with you right there ready to give, beg you for nothing at all. There's nothing of this world in it.

What beauty is not here, what gladness, what abundance, what fresh good fortune — what liberation is not here — when this nameless Great God, the very self of awareness, is the one being worshipped? Every splendor, every joy, every release is in it. And the freedom won here outshines every other freedom the schools have promised, because it is the freedom of the highest bliss, the freedom that is fullness flooding the whole universe.

Let this body of mine be of use in your worship — this body nourished within by the clear, clear nectar of devotion welling up inside, the immersion-nectar bright enough to mirror the whole world. Like copper touched by the alchemist's mercury, its tarnish burned away till it shines as pure gold — let it be made fit for serving you. And then, its ripe taste of absorption complete, let it dissolve into you, into the dense sweetness of awareness and bliss.

Let me be this forever, my Lord: utterly given over to the joy of worshipping at your feet — and in that very surrender, a lord myself, sovereign over the world, alone and acting freely as I please. There's the wonder of it: bound completely to the rapture of you, and in that binding made boundlessly free, a master with no equal. Slavery to your feet *is* the rarest lordship.

In a few rare souls there rises a thirst — to meditate on you, to see you, to touch you — and where that thirst burns, a great lake of your worship opens up, cool and sweet, slaking it. For those you've touched with your own grace, a fresh ocean of worship is born — an ocean of wonder that carries every fever away, full and brimming and new.

Just as you alone, of everything in the universe, are the worthy vessel of all this worship and its sweet enjoyment — so too, only the one who truly loves, the one absorbed in you, becomes the same kind of vessel. Lord and lover, the cup is one.

Victory to it — this nameless, towering festival of your worship, my Master — in which even the thirty-six elements of the world are thrown gloriously into upheaval, every settled level of things shaken loose and set blazing.

I bow to those great ones — by the nectar-water of whose devotion even the mere *tools* of your worship become worthy of worship themselves. Bathed in you, the flowers and the rest take on the very shine of Shiva, and turn holy. To those souls, I bow.

At the start of worship, the lovers meditate on the truth that the formula itself is to be filled with you — *I become Shiva* — and meditating so, swept up by the surge of love, they turn into Shiva and can no longer be contained. They can't be held anywhere, not even in their own selves, brimming over with a joy too vast for any vessel.

Some, blooming wide open as if they'd just won a kingdom, enter the festival of worship through the great gesture of expansion — and with that nectar-wine they share out the whole world. All of it, knower and known alike, is poured full, made into one's own bliss, divided round like the cup at a coronation feast.

Those whose every moment's enjoyment is the drinking of the nectar of worship — what are they? Gods? The liberated? Or some nameless other kind of beings altogether — souls a hundred hymns couldn't begin to praise?

The whole universe becomes the equipment of their worship, and they pour it all into the ground of awareness — and out of that comes, oh, some unspeakable *greatness* in the lovers, and at the very same time some unspeakable *lightness*. Heavy with majesty, weightless with ease: heavy, because all of it is theirs; light, because the whole burden of separateness has simply dropped away. How both at once? That's the shimmer of the paradox.

From the very stirring and tumult of the senses caught up in worship, the nectar wells up for the lovers — the way, for the gods, the immortal nectar rose from the churning of the ocean of milk. When the lovers' senses are stirred — even in that upheaval where knower and known seem to come apart — what surfaces is pure awareness, pure bliss, brimming over.

Some think of worship as the wish-granting cow that fills every desire. But others, the inward-turned ones, drink down that worship itself — worship whose flowing sweetness outpours even the streams of nectar — they suckle at it like the wish-cow and are flooded with wonder on the spot.

For the lovers, even the senses spilling outward — the very thing the world calls bondage, the round of birth and death — even *that*, the worship turns inward and feeds it back into the festival, bearing some nameless bliss within. The same outward rush that traps everyone else becomes, in them, more fuel for the rite, and ripens into the full rest of their own true nature.

When devotion's surge sweeps me away and worship breaks out toward you — you who are my very own self — here's the marvel: it never once turns into wretchedness, never bears the fruit of any littleness or begging. For others, worship is mostly *asking*, and asking is a kind of poverty. But this is the opposite: it's the farthest, fullest fruit even of worldly longing, the fruit beyond all the fruits of wealth and power — the whole and final reward.

For some, worship is a means, a set of motions, a way to reach your station. But for the lovers it's nothing of the kind — it's the sheer outpouring, the blossoming, the spreading delight of being utterly one with you. Not a road to you. The bliss of being you.

And here's a wonder: even a worship that makes no sense — clumsy, out of order, flung together by lovers so mad with devotion that they've forgotten all the rules — even that, when it's offered up, finds in you some footing, some nameless standing that the careful, by-the-book worshippers couldn't dream of. The rulebook would call it botched. Love makes it land.

When the mind, drinking the sweet taste of devotion, goes still — its restlessness stilled at last and even its stillness flowing out into worship, into the act of offering the whole world up — then, Shambhu, source of all good, *you* alone are the lovely and fitting vessel of all those acts of worship. You alone, the very self of awareness. It could be no one else — for everything else is shot through with separateness, and nothing made of separateness could ever be a worthy cup for this.

So let my instruments of worship be these, my Lord: whole, and pure, and full of devotion, and steady. Let my eyes and senses be complete, and pure because they're made of nothing but the rays of awareness, and devoted because they're given over to your service in the offering-up of the world, and steady because no animal craving has ever so much as brushed them. Let them be always, only, like this.

In your worship, my Lord — that gleaming treasure-house where every kind of worship is stored, where everything comes to rest in you, the full dense sweetness of awareness and bliss, the glorious Lord Bhairava — oh, what a splendor unfolds in the whole company of the senses. Some nameless radiance, witnessed by awareness alone, stretches itself out and shines.

This grace, this lovely simplicity, my Lord — surely it's seen in you alone: that though you are the Lord of all the worlds, even of the high gods, your own servants get to worship you, and get to *win* you — to take you, with nothing held back, and make you their own.

Let it rise up in me always — in one made worthy — this great festival of your worship: rising from the formed or the formless, from any existing thing or even from no thing at all. Put every "is" and "isn't" beneath my feet, and let me surface there, in you. From the solid like a pot, from the bodiless like joy, from the shards of a broken pot, even from sheer absence — from all of it, let the festival of resting in you rise.

To those who worship you with their very desire and anger and pride made into the offering — who take the cravings themselves, weigh them, and lay them at your feet — to them I bow, for in truth you are pleased with them. As the saying goes: *the one freed from joy and rage and fear and wrath, that one is dear to me.* Every movement of the mind, claimed and offered up, delights you.

Victory to it — the supreme rite of those who love you — the worship in which even straw, when it's offered, turns to jewels in the offering. Performed with nothing but blades of grass, it works as if it were heaped with gems, because for those absorbed in you it grants the full and final rest. To them it is whole, it is supreme, it is victorious. And in that — peace, and all blessing.

## Eighteen — The Showing-Forth

Lord of the world, your true lovers find you by reaching past the world, in the very middle of it — and then they find this whole world again, inside you. For them nothing here is far off anymore. Once a man has truly recognized that you are the Self of everything, every single thing stands close to him, his own, like his own body shining. "He who sees me everywhere, and sees everything in me" — it was for these that the old song was sung.

Somewhere you are the Lord; somewhere you are the Goddess, the great power pregnant with the whole unfolding order of things, from the first stir of time down to the solid earth. But come to the real, the place where all our imagining drops away, and there is no gap at all — not between you and the Goddess, and not between you and these three worlds. If even the power isn't separate from you, how could the world be?

People go about so puffed up, and they never know the one beautiful thing — that every single thing is lovely, every thing shines, because every thing is made of awareness. Never mind knowing the things; here is the wonder — the very awareness in me, lifted up, straining at the door, and still it can't catch its own face, can't taste its oneness with the Light. Oh, I am undone. (This is the cry of a man who has felt the rising and not yet reached the height.)

The man who has come to rest in his own Self — which is you — and out of that boundless sweetness honors your feet; the man who, eating this or going without, never once needs food or covering, because he is full; the man who simply worships, asking the world for nothing — that man I bow to.

Always this creature lives at home in your body, well and whole, made of bliss in its own ground — and still, inside, it burns. It burns because your will, flowing out as separateness, scorches it. So by that same will of yours, by your wish to take me up, do this for me here: let me become nothing but the joy of worshipping you.

Drinking the nectar that is the worship of your two lotus-feet — worship that rises on its own, with no effort — let my heart be set utterly free. And then, across the whole spread of things, in everything I take up and everything I lay down, let me walk through the world touching only sweetness, my whole life nothing but the overflow of my own joy.

You shine out plainly, my Lord, deep inside the whole field of doing and living — and within that shining of you, things come and things go, ceaselessly. Let them. Let all my things forever rise and fade in you, the way reflections rise and dissolve in a mirror and never stay, never stand apart for an instant — let them only shine and pass. Let me, full of you, be the very one who pours the world out and draws it back.

Let me wander forever in the city that is yours alone — that still, full place — steeped in your power. Or let me wander parted from you, lost in you another way. Or let me simply be you, free by nature. And whatever state does not carry me higher than the last, whatever moment is not your triumph in me — may not one breath of such a moment ever be mine.

Even though it is flooded on every side with the cool sweet nectar pouring down off your body — off your own supreme power — your lovers still walk here, in the middle of this sea of the world, rich with the wealth of worshipping you. The lake of birth and death, brimful all around with that nectar that cools the fire of every grief — that is where they move, at ease.

In your great forest, my Lord — cool under the shade of the great tree that is the supreme Word, the pure I-awareness branching out in a hundred limbs of power, a forest that takes away the fever of all separation — that great forest which is you yourself, awareness and bliss, a wide place of rest: there, in my own true nature, let me dwell forever, your worshipper.

In every thing, in every being alive, you shine — you, who are nothing but pure awareness, the only reason a man can ever grasp a blue or a sound at all. Unrecognized, you are still really shining there. So shine like that before me too, my Lord, your servant — everywhere, plain and known by an unmistakable mark all your own, blazoned with the three eyes and the trident. Step into the open. Break out into me.

I take my own pride — my I-am-the-doer — and pour it out to you as the offering, the cooked oblation, in the act of grasping the higher "I," the "I" that is you. When, my Lord, will you be so pleased by that — pleased by a love that has made everything level and equal — that you become, everywhere I look, the resting-place of my seeing? When this clinging to the body falls away, let me see, face to face, a whole universe that is only you.

Let me live in the middle of the great sea of supreme nectar — awareness and bliss — my mind sunk wholly in nothing but the rite of worshipping you, and from that place let me go about all the ordinary business of the world, doing everything people do, and yet tasting, in every last thing, that one unworldly thing: the sweetness that is your own bliss.

Let this much at least be settled: every thing here, whatever it is, is yours — a form of your own glory. So be it, yes. But who is fit to lay open the real truth of you in words? No one. For the moment we so much as set out to think it through, the very first thing to flash up — that unaccountable, self-evident *something* we live by — names and forms and acts, "the Great Lord," all of it — that you carry off. You steal it, you slacken our grip, you leave us emptied, our work forgotten. And rightly so — for you are Hara, the one whose very name means: he who takes away.

In these men, drunk on the love of you, there is not the faintest itch for pleasure, not the smallest wish even for the peace of grief's ending — the drunkenness of love has burned it clean out of them. And standing here before you, plain in front of their faces, they cannot even remember to ask for liberation, fruit of the whole long search though it is — not before you, the heart-thief, who quiets every smaller knower. Their very hearts have been stolen. How could men with no heart left go asking for anything else?

Waking, or the other states — dream, deep sleep — or some still subtler condition than any of them, the faintest flicker of being: for the man whose heart is given to you in love, whose mind has you for its lord, every one of these, all of it, top to bottom, is one great festival. It is all jubilee — simply because you are its master.

All the workings of the wheel of the senses, my Lord, right up to the mind itself — restless things, slack and scattered every which way — how is it that in the men warm and strong with the wealth of your love, the moment those workings reach you, the pure awareness, they go firm and steady and long, settled into your oneness, become your very self, pure waking light? (And this is a wonder he marvels at in himself.)

In the very first making, you never made one separate thing — there is nothing anywhere that stands apart from you on its own, for everything, being known, being made of awareness, simply cannot break off into otherness. And so you never made a single grief-thing either, never made one painful, divided thing — only, where your oneness goes unrecognized, it is felt as grief, felt as severed. House of unequalled wonder, peerless ground of astonishment — to you, my praise.

The harsh ravine of your hiddenness, where you go un-known — that pit in me — has been flooded with the nectar of your own non-difference, flooded till it brimmed and overflowed and washed every stain of wavering thought clean away; and that hard, unbeatable enemy, doubt, lies crushed to powder. For a man left like that, let the sight of you — the dense, unbroken flashing-forth of you as my own Self — be unceasing, without a break.

Enter me, my Lord, plainly — come out into the open, not hidden — and then, so entered, let me enter you, forever; for I am yours always. Come so near there is no nearness left, and let me, in a rush, worship your very body face to face — your real form — let me sink wholly into it.

No one has the power to praise you — not really, not Brahma, not Vishnu, not Rudra, none of them, while they still hold themselves apart from your dense, supreme awareness. And yet — the power is there after all, in anyone who recognizes you as his own Self, because you are so achingly beautiful, awareness and bliss in one, the heart's whole longing. So this is the one thing I keep asking, over and over, without end: that I might go on gazing at you, my Lord, and never once look away.

## Nineteen — The Blazing-Up

Praise to you, Shiva, the wishing-tree that grants what no wishing-tree can. Every other wish-granting tree hands you the thing you ask for — and stops there. But you give what could never even be asked for, gifts past the reach of any prayer. That's the wonder of you: the one whose story has no precedent.

They say the whole world can be had from your own Self, the single storehouse of every good thing. Fine — let the world have it. But here's my trouble: that very Self of mine is *you*, and you are the one thing I can't seem to get my hands on. The whole universe, take it, keep it — I don't even want it. It's you I want, and you alone slip away. The moment I come back down out of absorption into the daylight world, you don't show. Forget the world; it's you who won't quite appear.

Because this Self — the body made of pure awareness, knowing and acting at once — this Self is, in every way, the Supreme Lord himself. He is the real being inside all things. If even a clay pot has any being at all, it's borrowed from him; and if he weren't shining as its ground, the pot wouldn't so much as have a *name* — let alone a shape, a use, a cause. Nothing stands without the light. So how could the one Lord be the inner being of so many different things, in so many places, doing so many different works? Like this: take the light away and there's nothing left to call anything. Not even the name survives.

So let it be this, Lord. There's a path that is simply the direct seeing of you, and that seeing burns off the fever of this whole grinding round of birth and death. Let me dissolve into that path — and let my one destination, the only place I'm going, be made entirely of you. Nothing left of the journey but you at the end of it.

When, Lord — when will it finally come to me, that perfection wreathed in the wishing-jewel vine that is your spotless feet? Your feet, I mean the pure rays of you, knowing and acting and all the rest; and to dwell on them, over and over, turning them in the mind — that dwelling itself is the jewel-vine, because it hands over every treasure there is. When will that full perfection bloom in me, the perfection that comes straight from you — the kind that leaves even the masters, the seasoned yogis, standing there astonished?

When, Lord, will I look full on the clear mirror of your face — that face all peace and stillness — the face that streams a flood of nectar like nothing this world has ever poured, a flood that drowns the whole universe, seer and seen and all, under its sweetness?

And your form — the form that rises the very instant it's remembered, the moment the thought of you begins — when will it pour its waves of supreme nectar, forever, into the deep aching hollows in me, the chasms that are nothing but the hunger to be un-divided from you? For there is no liberation but this: to be undivided from you. Let that nectar flood every gap.

This mind of mine — when will it let go its restlessness at last, drowned in the savor of you, in the surge of supreme awareness that is yours alone? Even now, even after I've tasted that absorption again and again, the old fidgeting hasn't fallen away. When, Lord? Soon — let it be soon. I can't say it any plainer than that: soon.

And the pairs that pull me back and forth — heat and cold, pleasure and pain, all of them, every one, every time — let them keep coming. But let them come dry now, come bitter, come tasteless in my mouth. Because I've offered them all up to you first, dissolved them into you as nothing but awareness, and carried the taste of that absorption back out into ordinary waking — and now, brimful of the nectar of touching your non-twoness, every one of those old opposites comes to me flat and parched and sour. Let them come like that. I won't mind.

Lord, let your pure rays turn and face me — your kind powers, the ones that lift a person up. Let them sink this show of body-and-the-rest beneath them and blaze out instead. And this fever of the body, this fever of the mind — it's only darkness, that's all it ever was — let your rays wipe it out completely, drive it off on every side.

Be gracious to me, God — be gracious until they bend to my will, the highwaymen who block your road. You know the ones: the gunas, the strands of nature, the bright and the busy and the dull, the very things that bar a soul from stepping onto the ground of the real, that rob me of the supreme truth, that steal away the seamless awareness and leave me holding something I can't even enjoy. Make those thieves my servants. When the master is pleased, even the robbers come to heel — and once they serve a knower, they stop being thieves at all.

Let my mind be flooded — and soon, all-pervading one — flooded with the pure essence-nectar of devotion to you, a lake brimming over, until the bad waters float clear up and out. The mind is a pool, and into it had seeped a foul standing water, the leftover habits of a divided life. Pour your sweet flood in and watch those stale tendencies rise to the top and spill over the edge and run off, gone, every drop of them.

Here's the thing that stops me cold: in the state of liberation — full Shiva-hood, freedom while still alive — there's devotion to you. The freed one still loves. So why on earth not me? Why shouldn't a mere mortal, a man who hasn't yet recognized what he is, have that same devotion now? It shines even there, at the summit — so raise me, unborn one, raise me up to the perfection-ground that matches it, the ground where the master and his servant both stand out clear and luminous at once. Lift me there.

And don't misread me, Lord. Don't look at me hungry for a scrap, a crumb of the lesser powers, and write me off as greedy and proud. I'm not after the little siddhis, the small split-off magic — shrinking tiny, growing huge, all that. The scriptures promise the seeker can taste those powers and *then* walk on into Shiva; so a person might think me low-minded for wanting them at all. But hear the reason. Even the liberation that waits just past all those glittering powers — set it down beside the sheer joy of devotion to you, beside the bliss of being absorbed in you, and that liberation looks small. Thin. A meager little thing. Your love outshines even freedom. That's all I'm after.

So be gracious to me, your servant — just this, this much, nothing more. Not the powers. Just your grace. The giver here is the Lord of all three worlds, the one whose gift is so far past anything I could deserve that — well, a gift fit for *him* to give could never be the kind of thing a man like me would even know how to want. So I ask only this: be pleased with me. Let that be the whole of the request.

May your two lotus-feet open and bloom, always, in this mind of mine — a mind brimful now with the nectar-savor of remembering you, of holding your very form in awareness. Let those lotuses unfold, shedding all their tightness, all their dividedness, and let them stream down honey — some honey not of this world at all, sweetness past anything earthly, the taste of the supreme joy itself — dripping endlessly into the flooded pool of my heart.

Let him be my lord — this dense mass of pure awareness, the one who shows me grace. Let him be my father, Tryambaka, who wakes the knower in me. And let her be my mother, Bhavani, the supreme Power, my queen. For one who has come to recognize himself as the Great Lord, there is no one else in all the world — no second thing anywhere, nothing and no one that is *mine* but this. And on that alone — needing nothing further, full past the brim, happier than I have any word for — let me wander the earth. Let me wander, and be at peace.

## Twenty — The Savoring

I bow to the Lord, the Lord of the three worlds. He is white with ash, the ash that is the whole shining universe worn close against him. He has three eyes — will, knowing, and doing — the three lights by which every division is burned away. He carries the trident, that blazing spear of knowledge that splits all separateness apart. The serpents of his outgoing powers he has gathered back and laid quiet against himself, wound round him like a sacred thread. And crowning him, his highest and truest face, is the sliver of the moon — the power of awareness that gives life to everything, worn lightly, the way a man wears an ornament for the sheer play of it. To him I bow.

I praise the one whose white robe is nothing but the halo of rays streaming out from his own body of pure awareness — light pouring off him and wrapping him round, his own circle of power forever embracing him. And his only finery for the dance is a garland of skulls, blazing and alive: the heads of every knower there is, from the highest down to the least, strung together into one necklace for the festival of his ecstatic, world-making dance.

I bow to those for whom Shiva himself is the only god — whose every act is fit for Shiva, an act of making, unmaking, of grace given freely; whose every breath leans toward Shiva alone, drunk on the one taste of merging into him. Those breaths are their very life. And so they are mansions of good fortune, full to the brim with the highest joy, the envy and the longing of the whole world.

This whole world is only your play, Lord — and there is another play behind it, riding higher still than your lordship over all things. For in those great deeds of yours, those wonders that come about by nothing more than your wanting them, I find that I myself reach the perfect means: drawing on my own power of awareness, merged into you, I become a doer of the five great acts just as you are. And that — that I should do what you do — is yet one more of your games.

In this house of yours, this radiance of yours that the whole world bows to, when all this immeasurable play is going on inside it — how vast, my Lord, how endless is the welling-up of the nectar of joy that comes of it. How much sweetness. There is no edge to it at all.

How could he not be the loveliest of all — Shiva, the beloved of Gauri, the one the whole world aches for? And how could she, Gauri, the supreme Power, be anything but his dearest of all? He is dense joy itself, held in the arms of the Goddess; he is Hara, who steals the heart with the wonder of merging and dissolves every trace of twoness. Of course he is her one beloved, and she his.

There is someone whose very root, whose own deepest Self, is made of the nectar of contemplation — undying, because it is pure awareness and nothing perishable. He is a wondrous tree, a tree past all finding, that takes away every sorrow. And on that tree, every tendril of his knowing — every blue, every pleasure, every passing thing he perceives — grows of that same nectar of contemplation, and tastes of it.

When the sweet itch of devotion rises up in someone — devotion, that helpless love of God that overtakes a man like an itch he cannot help but scratch — then the Supreme Lord himself becomes the one great whetstone of worship: that towering pillar of touchstone rock against which the itch is rubbed away, until it stills, and the soul comes to rest in its own dense joy.

To the one who plays forever at making worlds, who reclines forever at ease in their keeping, who is forever full and content, fed on the food of the three worlds — to that Master, my bowing.

To those who go nowhere else, who let go of nothing, who do not climb off to some far heaven, do not chase pleasures below, do not even quit the body — but who look right here, at this very world that the unawakened despise and want to be rid of, and see it as your house, your dense field of awareness — to those great-souled ones, possessed of the divine and magnificent vision, again and again I bow, and bow again. They, and only they, know the highest truth.

For those made rich with the wealth of devotion, what else is there to ask for? Everything to be had is already had. And for those who are poor in it, what else is even worth asking for? Without it nothing real is reached, so why beg for the husks?

Where pains themselves turn sweet, where poison itself turns to nectar, where even the round of birth and death turns into release — that road is Shiva's road. That is the way that runs through Power herself.

At the beginning, in the middle, at the end, there is no sorrow for those who delight in you. And yet, Ishana — and yet we sink. We falter. Tell me how. Tell me why, in the rising and the spreading and the settling of awareness, we still let ourselves be dragged back down into the ordinary.

For some, Lord, you have to be sought through knowledge, through yoga, through this discipline and that. But to the ones who love you — to the free ones, who need no method, who have already won everything by merging into you — to them you are simply Light, openly, always, with nothing in between.

Those who love you have no afflictions left; and there is no straining contemplation either, no reaching after you, because you are already shining as their own Self. And yet — even so — there is this: *Shiva exists.* Some nameless something, for them, that turns a little outward. The pure overflow of supreme joy, breaking the surface for no reason at all but its own fullness.

You who are the shining of every appearance — the light in which all things appear — and who, even so, are swollen full with self-awareness, the wonder and savor of supreme joy: "I am all this" — so I praise you, Lord. So I praise that power of yours, your power of doing.

Every living being without exception — yes, even Brahma, Indra, Vishnu, and the great gods above them — every one of them is forever devouring its own world, swallowing experience down, leaning hungrily out toward what it knows. And seeing this, O God, I bow to the whole universe — for it is nothing but you.

Whatever seems to stand over and above me, swollen into a separateness it does not have — all of it is false, bound for dissolution, melting back into pure awareness. Just so, my Lord, just exactly so it is spoken — by you, in your game of drawing the worlds back home.

For those thick with devotion, the merest thought of you and you are already there, already present the instant the contemplation begins. And toward you — you who cannot be conceived even by the small, measured yogis — they reign supreme; toward the power that authors every unthinkable wonder, they themselves stand victorious, the very wellsprings of an astonishment beyond all common astonishment, surpassing everything.

By the soaking, as it were, of the wine of devotion's nectar to you, the cores of their being are made glad and set trembling — and so the heroes dance in the night. In the very thick of illusion the awakened ones dance, blazing wider with the unfolding of awareness, roused to ecstasy by their hosts of ghouls — the gathered bonds of bondage themselves turned to dancers in their hands, the swarms of powers that rise on the heels of every perception, all of it stirred into one wild festival of joy.

This bowing-down to you was begun, Shambhu, with some part of me, some unaccountable part beyond all marking. By that same part, let me go on, through this whole unending stretch of time, firm and unwavering, never breaking off — let me go on like this forever.
