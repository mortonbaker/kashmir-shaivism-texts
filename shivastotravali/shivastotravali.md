# The Shivastotravali

*The Garland of Hymns to Shiva, by Utpaladeva — devotion and recognition as one*

A plain-English rendering, written for narration. Faithful to the spirit and teaching of the original hymns, in everyday modern language.

---

## About this text

Utpaladeva was one of the towering philosophers of the tradition — the author of the great Verses on the Recognition of the Lord, the foundational work of the whole Pratyabhijna school, and the spiritual grandfather of Abhinavagupta. But he was something more than a philosopher. He was a lover of God. And in the Shivastotravali, the rigorous metaphysician sets down his arguments and simply sings.

The text is a garland of hymns — devotional songs poured out, the tradition says, in spontaneous ecstasy, later gathered by his disciples into twenty hymns. They are among the most beautiful devotional poetry of the world, and they carry a teaching that is easy to miss and precious to grasp: that devotion and non-dual recognition are not opposites but partners. In most mystical systems you must choose — either you melt in love before a God who is other than you, or you realize you *are* the divine and so have no one left to worship. Utpaladeva refuses the choice. For him, the deepest love and the deepest recognition are the same movement of the heart.

How can this be? Because the Lord he loves is his own Self — and the love he feels is the Lord's own nature. To recognize that you are Shiva is not to lose the beloved; it is to discover that the love, the lover, and the beloved were always one. What follows renders the heart of these hymns in plain words, following their devotional movements rather than numbering every verse, since they were sung as outpourings of feeling rather than as a tight argument.

---

## The Pleasure of Devotion

The garland opens by bowing — not to a distant deity, but to the devotee in whom Shiva spontaneously shines. Utpaladeva praises the one to whom the Lord appears without any technique at all, without forced meditation or anxious recitation, simply through the fullness of love. This is the whole spirit of the collection in its first breath: the highest realization comes not by grim effort but by devotion so complete that the Lord shines of his own accord.

And then the great theme: the love we feel for our own deepest Self is the very nature of Shiva. When you love anything truly, what you are loving, underneath, is the one Self that is the heart of all things — for the Lord alone is the Self of everyone and everything. So devotion is not a relationship between two separate beings. It is the one Consciousness recognizing and delighting in itself, wearing the form of a lover and a beloved for the sheer joy of the love. To know this is to feel the whole of existence saturated with the bliss of devotional love, every particle of the world soaked in the same delight.

Utpaladeva prays for this devotion above all other gifts. Not for liberation as an escape, not for powers, not for heaven — but simply for love, for the unbroken nectar of devotion to the Lord who is his own Self. He calls this devotion a seed, and says that when it is watered by the sweetness of love it bears the fruit of the highest freedom. Liberation, for him, is not the alternative to love. It is what love ripens into.

## Seeing the Self in All

Through the hymns runs the steady recognition that the Lord is everywhere and is everything. Utpaladeva sings of contemplating all things as the Self — of letting the boundary between worshipper and world dissolve until there is only the one divine "I" present as every form. The whole universe becomes the body of the beloved, every experience a meeting with him, every object a place where he shines.

This transfigures ordinary life completely. For one who sees this way, there is no profane moment, no place the Lord is absent, no experience that is not secretly a meeting with the beloved. The hymns express a kind of holy intoxication — a delight that spills over the edges of formal worship into every waking instant, because the Lord is not confined to the shrine. He is the awareness in which the whole day is appearing. To walk through such a world is to walk through the body of the one you love.

## The Cry of Longing

But the hymns are not all serene recognition. Utpaladeva is too honest a lover for that, and some of the most moving verses are cries of longing and even of complaint. He pours out the ache of feeling separated from the Lord, the grief of the times when the recognition dims and the world goes flat and the beloved seems to hide. He pleads. He protests. He asks how the Lord can let his own devotee suffer the pain of forgetting.

This is not a contradiction of the non-dual teaching — it is its emotional truth. Even knowing that he is one with Shiva, the embodied soul still passes through stretches of veiling, when the contracted self closes back in and the radiant recognition fades to a memory. And in those stretches the lover cries out. The hymns honor that cry. They do not pretend the path is a smooth ascent. They give voice to the real texture of a devotional life: blazing recognition and aching absence, intimacy and longing, the beloved found and the beloved seemingly lost, over and over, until the recognition finally steadies.

There is deep comfort in this for anyone who walks the path. Even this great sage, even the grandfather of the whole tradition, knew the dry times, the times of feeling shut out, and met them not with cold philosophy but with a lover's open complaint. The longing itself, he shows, is a form of love — and even the cry of separation is the beloved calling to himself.

## The Triumph of Freedom

Other hymns ring with triumph — the exultation of the soul that has recognized its own sovereign freedom. Utpaladeva sings of the absolute freedom of the Lord, which is the soul's own freedom once recognized: the power by which Consciousness pours out worlds and draws them back, plays every role, and is bound by nothing. To know oneself as this free Lord is to be lifted above every fear, for what could threaten the one who is the source and self of all?

He sings of the play of the divine — the whole cosmos as the Lord's joyful game, created out of fullness and freedom for the sheer delight of the playing, with no compulsion and no lack behind it. And he sings of the conquest of all that binds: the veiling forces, the contraction, the fear, the sense of being a small separate thing, all of it overcome in the recognition of the boundless Self. These are hymns of victory — not the victory of one being over another, but the victory of recognition over forgetting, of the Light over its own self-imposed shadow.

## The Secret Teaching

In the heart of the collection Utpaladeva points, again and again, to the secret that holds devotion and recognition together. The Lord he worships is not somewhere else. The "you" he addresses in every hymn, the beloved he longs for and exults in and complains to, is his own innermost Self — the one Consciousness that he is. And yet he keeps the form of address, keeps the "you," keeps the posture of the lover before the beloved, because there is a sweetness in that posture that bare recognition alone would lose.

This is the genius of the Shivastotravali. Utpaladeva could simply say "I am Shiva" and fall silent. Instead he chooses to say "I love you, my Lord, who are my own Self" — and in that choice he keeps both the truth of oneness and the sweetness of love. The one Consciousness, fully recognizing itself, does not collapse into a featureless blank. It blossoms into worship, into song, into the endless delight of loving itself in the form of an other who is not finally other at all. The lover and the beloved are one, and precisely because they are one, the love between them is infinite and unbreakable.

## The Closing Prayer

The garland ends as it began, in devotion — asking only for unbroken love, for the recognition that never again dims, for the privilege of singing the Lord's praises forever. Utpaladeva does not ask to stop existing, to dissolve away, to be done with the world. He asks to go on loving, to remain a devotee even in the fullness of recognition, to taste eternally the nectar of devotion to the Self of all.

And this is his final gift to everyone who comes after. He shows that the summit of the path is not a cold and empty absolute, but a living, loving, singing fullness — the one Consciousness delighting in itself, worshipping itself, adoring itself in every form, forever. To reach the end of the path, he teaches, is not to stop loving. It is to love at last without limit, without fear, and without end, having recognized that the one you love and the one who loves and the love itself were always, only, the single boundless Light that you are.

## Fidelity note

This rendering conveys the spirit, themes, and teaching of Utpaladeva's Shivastotravali — its twenty hymns of devotion — following their devotional movements rather than numbering each verse, since the hymns were composed as spontaneous outpourings of feeling rather than as a sequential argument. The collection's defining teaching is kept central: that devotion (*bhakti*) and non-dual recognition (*advaita*) are complementary rather than opposed, because the Lord who is worshipped is the worshipper's own Self. The major registers of the hymns are all represented — the pleasure of devotion, the vision of the Self in all things, the cries of longing and separation, the exultation of recognized freedom, the secret that unites love and oneness, and the closing prayer for unending devotion. Because these are devotional poems, this rendering is an original English composition conveying their meaning and spirit; it does not reproduce, paraphrase, or track any modern translator's verse renderings, and quotes no copyrighted translation. It is drawn from the public-domain Sanskrit hymns and the well-attested themes of the collection. This is an original plain-English rendering for audio, not a reproduction of any modern translation or commentary.
