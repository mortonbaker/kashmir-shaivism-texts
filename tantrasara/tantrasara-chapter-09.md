# Chapter Nine: The Division of the Categories

The last chapter told us what the categories are — the rungs by which one Light becomes a world. You might expect this chapter to simply count them out, top to bottom. But Abhinavagupta does something subtler and far more interesting, and you have to catch the shift or the whole chapter slips past you.

His question here is not *what* the categories are. It is: the very same category is a completely different thing depending on **who is perceiving it**. A lump of earth seen by a sleepwalking soul and the same earth seen by an awakened master are, in lived truth, not the same earth at all. So the real "division of the categories" is the division of the **knowers**. That is what this chapter maps: the kinds of perceiver, from the most asleep to the most awake, and how the world fractures or simplifies depending on which of them is looking.

## The Seven Knowers

The tradition counts seven kinds of perceiving subject, seven grades of "I." Picture them as a ladder of wakefulness.

At the bottom is the **embodied soul** — you, me, the ordinary person, fully identified with a body and a mind, convinced the world outside is solid and separate and other. This knower sees the most division, the most multiplicity, the most foreignness.

Above that are souls more and more withdrawn from the outward world and more and more awake to themselves — those dissolved into a kind of cosmic sleep, those who have shed the body but not yet the last trace of separateness, and then the higher knowers who have begun to recognize the world as their own: the Mantra-beings, the Lords of Mantra, the Great Lords of Mantra — radiant subjects for whom the universe is increasingly transparent, increasingly *themselves*.

At the very top is **Shiva** — the knower for whom there is no outside at all, no separation between the one who knows and the thing known, because everything is recognized as his own undivided Self.

So the same single reality is beheld along this whole spectrum: as utterly foreign at the bottom, as utterly oneself at the top, and as every gradation between.

## Why the Same Thing Splits Fifteen Ways

Here is the technical heart, and I will keep it light, because the point matters more than the arithmetic. Take any category — say, the earth under your feet. For each of the seven knowers it shows up differently, and each knower beholds it through their own characteristic power, so that single category can be divided fourteen ways — the seven knowers and their seven powers — with its own bare nature making a fifteenth. The tradition calls this the fifteenfold division.

Don't memorize that. Catch what it *means*. It means the world has no single fixed face. What a thing "is" depends entirely on the wakefulness of the one meeting it. The same flower is a mere object to the sleepwalker, a play of one's own consciousness to the awakened. Reality is not a finished thing sitting out there; it is a relationship, and the quality of that relationship rises and falls with the quality of the knower.

And here is the liberating turn. **As the knower awakens, the divisions collapse.** For the most bound soul, a category shatters into fifteen pieces. Climb to a more awake knower and it splits fewer ways — thirteen, then eleven, then nine, seven, five, three. And at the summit, for Shiva, there is no division at all. Perfect fullness. One seamless Light, beholding itself with nothing left over to fracture.

So this dry-seeming catalogue is secretly a map of liberation. The fracturing of the world into countless separate things is not a fact about the world. It is a symptom of the knower's sleep. Wake the knower and the fractures heal — not because the things vanish, but because they are recognized, one by one, as the looker's own Self.

## The Whole World in a Single Perception

Abhinavagupta then proves this is not abstract by showing it inside one ordinary act of seeing. When you simply look at something blue, that single perception is not the instantaneous, seamless thing it appears to be. Watch closely — as the masters, trained by their teacher's instruction, learn to watch — and you find the breath itself unfolding the perception across a fine series of inner beats: a first flash where knower and known are still undivided, a swelling where the perceiver rises up, a stretch where the object stands fully formed, a closing where it merges back. The same fifteenfold structure that divides the whole cosmos is rehearsed, in miniature, in your noticing of a patch of color.

And this is the doorway. As the perceiver grows quieter, more discriminating, less grabbed by the object, those beats reduce — two by two — until perception narrows to its final pair: one beat of pure fullness, and one beat in which awareness, resting there, becomes capable of knowing and doing all things. That last narrowing is absorption into Shiva. An old master called it "the falling-away of the beat" — the point where the measured, sequential, fractured act of ordinary perception collapses back into the timeless, undivided knowing it always rose from. Practiced from the higher grades of perception, this is also the root of the yogic powers, but that is a side-road; the main road is the collapse into unity.

## Waking, Dream, Sleep, and Beyond

The same play of knower and known generates the familiar states of consciousness, and the chapter reads them in this same key. In **waking**, awareness leans entirely outward and the object stands over against you as something external to be grounded. In **dream**, the object is recognized as a projection, a thing held up by the mind itself. In **deep sleep**, it withdraws into pure seed, subject without spread-out object. Then **the Fourth** — the witness, awareness turning back to taste its own fullness, no longer carried off by any object. And **beyond the Fourth**, the state that transcends them all, where there is no longer any seam between the knower and a single thing it knows: pure plenary Consciousness, all objects recognized as itself.

These are not five different places. They are five depths of the one awareness, named by how much it has woken up to itself.

## The One Repose Beneath Every Knower

The chapter ends with a verse that gathers the whole teaching into a single thrust, and it is worth hearing plainly.

In the entire web of categories — every level, every world, every grade of knower — there is no form of any perceiver, however bound, that shines for even an instant *without* some repose in Consciousness. Even the most asleep soul is, at root, awareness resting in itself; it has only forgotten. The "I" appears in countless manifold disguises, but it is always, underneath, the one self-aware Light.

Therefore, the verse says: in this whole web of knowable things, take up *that* ground — the silent, non-conceptual awareness whose very essence is the free self-knowing of "I am" — dwell there, and attain your own true Self-existence. Not by escaping the world of objects, but by finding, beneath every perception, the one reposing awareness that was doing the perceiving all along.

That is the division of the categories: not a wall chart of thirty-six boxes, but the discovery that the whole fractured world is the measure of how asleep the knower is — and that waking the knower is the entire path home.

Here ends the ninth chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Division of the Categories.
