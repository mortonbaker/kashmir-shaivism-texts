# Chapter Twenty-Two: The Kaula Rite

We have come to the last chapter, and the book ends where the most demanding and the most secret of the practices begins. Everything so far has been worship growing steadily more inward. Now Abhinavagupta turns to the rite he calls the Kula-yāga, the Kaula sacrifice — the worship that belongs to the *kula*, the totality, the whole living body of reality. He sets it down plainly, and he sets it down only for those in whom the right inclination has already taken firm root. This is not a beginner's practice. It is the practice for the one who is ready to worship reality with nothing held back, including the very things the cautious world is taught to fear.

He opens by quoting the older scripture, the *Yogasaṃcāra*, and the warning it carries is blunt. The Brahman that is pure Bliss, it says, dwells in the body in a threefold way, hidden in the last of the lip-formed letters — the sound *ma*, the sound at which the lips close. The ones who turn away from that Bliss, who refuse it, are left without Bliss; and the worshippers of the sacred circle who keep the rite but withhold the wine, the *madya*, fall into a terrible darkness. Therefore, the text says, hold to this settled rule. We should not soften what is being said here. The Kaula path uses the cup of wine and the union of the pair — the very substances and acts that ordinary religion sets outside its walls — and it uses them deliberately, as the proof in the body that the one Light is present even there, with no exception left standing. To flinch from them would be to keep believing, secretly, that some corner of reality is not divine. The whole point of this rite is to leave no such corner.

## The sacrifice in six forms

The Kula sacrifice, Abhinavagupta says, is sixfold. The same worship is performed in six ascending arenas, each one more interior than the last. First, in an external reality — on a real altar, with real substances. Second, in Śakti — in the living power of the partner. Third, in one's own body. Fourth, in the pair — in the union of the two. Fifth, in the vital breath, the *prāṇa*. And sixth, in Consciousness itself, in pure awareness with nothing outer remaining at all.

These six are a ladder. Each later form is higher than the one before it, and yet each earlier form is the cause of the splendor of the next — the outer rite is not discarded but is the ground from which the inner rite catches fire. He gives a precise instruction about who emphasizes which. For the one who seeks powers, the *siddhis* — the second, the fourth, and the fifth are to be cultivated by every means, because those are the forms charged with the energy that yields attainment. But for the one who seeks liberation, the *mumukṣu*, the sixth is the heart of the matter, the worship in Consciousness alone. And even for that seeker, the forms from the second onward are still performed during the occasional rites, simply to fulfill the rule and keep the whole intact. Liberation is the aim, but the body of the rite is honored in full.

## The first: the external sacrifice

The outer rite is laid out with its furniture named. There is the altar. There is the heroes' cup, the *vīra-pātra*, filled with wine. There is the red cloth, and the *liṅga* spoken of before, and the rest of the appointments. Whatever is to be done — the washing, the preparing, all of it — purity is not achieved by avoidance but by resting in perfect Bliss; that repose is the only real cleansing.

Then the worshipper contemplates the unity of three things that are usually felt as separate: the vital breath, Consciousness, and the body. They are one. And because Consciousness here is nothing less than Paramaśiva, the Supreme Śiva himself, that unity is divine through and through. Now the mantra is uttered twenty-seven times — placed on the head, the face, the heart, the genitals, and the sacred images, in forward order and then in reverse — and through this the worshipper realizes fullness across the whole sweep of the cosmic courses, holding them at once in their three aspects: the Supreme, the Supreme-and-not-Supreme, and the not-Supreme.

The twenty-seven are not arbitrary. Abhinavagupta maps them. In the lowest aspect, the not-Supreme, they run down the ladder of the categories of existence — from Māyā through the individual soul, through Nature, the qualities, the intellect, and on down to the element earth. In the middle aspect, the Supreme-and-not-Supreme, they are the five Brahmā-forms. And in the highest aspect, the Supreme, they are the five Powers of the Lord, each one unfolding into five forms of its own — five times five, twenty-five — which, when their distinctions dissolve, are seen to be a single undivided Power, one Śakti. The whole architecture of reality is being placed into the body, named and consecrated, and then collapsed back into one.

Next he binds what he calls the Flame of the Fire of Consciousness — the living flame whose substance is the intellect and the breath together. Uttering once, he fastens this flame to Supreme Śiva so tightly that nothing separate, nothing dual, can slip in between. Through that penetration the worshipper makes the ingredients, the house, the directions of space, and the supports all identical with That, with Śiva — and then he fills and worships the vessel, and pleases the altars and the gathered mantras seated in the body's energy-centers, the *cakras*, touching left thumb to ring finger as the seal — and then he carries the worship inward, into the breath.

On the altar he is to imagine a seat: a trident rising into its three prongs, which are the three powers of the Lord — the Will, the Knowledge, and the Action. He builds it letter by letter. Up to Māyā, the seat is the letter *sa*. The seat of the three powers themselves is the letter *au*. And above even that is the Power whose very nature is *Vimarśa* — the self-aware pulse of Consciousness reflecting on itself, the throne above the throne. Having performed the placing, the *nyāsa*, over the support and the supported, he then sees the universe right there within Consciousness — for the universe is Consciousness and Consciousness is the universe, each enclosing the other, a double embrace with no gap left in it. Then he offers the sandal paste, the incense, the liquor, the flowers, and the rest, each offering ending in absorption back into the Self; and resting in his own Self he murmurs the mantra in a low voice; and at the last he casts the remnant into water. That is the external sacrifice.

## The second: the sacrifice in Śakti

In the second form the worship moves into the living power of the partner. Here Śakti plays at being the Hero — at being Śiva himself — and the Hero plays at being Śakti, each taking on the nature of the other, back and forth. Because both are in truth of the nature of both, this reciprocal play awakens Śiva and Śakti within them — rising from joy, through delight, all the way into manifestation, which is the union itself. And by the Lord's own power of governance, his *niyati-śakti*, a pure nature comes to predominate in the two of them, so that what would be ordinary becomes sacred.

Again the seat is built ending in the three powers, but now with the Emitting Power, the *Visarga-śakti* — the very power by which Consciousness pours itself out into the world — placed at the center of the three corners. The worship is performed in the energy-center of the body, and then carried up into the secondary centers, into the *Brahmarandhra*, the opening at the crown, and the rest.

## The third and fourth: the body and the pair

Now the rite reaches its most explicitly heroic form, the sacrifice in the pair, the *yāmala*. Abhinavagupta gives the principle in a single verse. The mark of a Śakti, he says, is non-separation from the one who possesses her — she is never truly apart from him. And so, regardless of age, regardless of caste, regardless of any of the distinctions the world insists upon, through union there arises an identity that is born of the pair itself, an identity that lies altogether beyond the world. The two become one, and that oneness is not of this world's order.

A run of verses follows, and they hold nothing back. The central center and the subordinate centers are worshipped — outwardly with flowers, inwardly with sandal, with food, with liquor. The goddesses of the surrounding circles are drawn gradually inward toward the central center, the center of Consciousness, as awareness itself widens through the enjoyment of each of their respective delights — each pleasure, fully received, becoming a doorway rather than a snare. The pair enters what is called the Upper Abode. The peaceful emission and the risen emission spring up together, at once, in the Śakti and in her possessor alike. And above both of these stands the unchanging Abode called *Anuttara* — the Unsurpassable — which is the joy made of both of them together, the Highest Kaula, which is neither the peaceful nor the risen but the source from which both arise.

He speaks of *Khecarī-mudrā*, the seal of the one who moves in the sky of Consciousness, held all through the drinking and the enjoying and the playing and the laughing of Śiva and Śakti — the union lived not as grim discipline but as the play and laughter of the divine pair. He speaks of the eightfold sound, rising from the unmanifest sound at the bottom up to the unstruck sound at the top, and of the eightfold Bhairava made of light and of sound, which is the supreme penetration of the mantra. And then he says the thing that this whole arc has been moving toward: this is liberation while still living. The *jīvanmukti* the book has circled again and again is realized here, in the body, in the pair, in the play — sealed by the *Vyomacārī* and *Khecarī* mudrās, the postures of one who already walks free in the open sky of awareness. Here the sacrifice of the pair ends.

## The fifth and sixth: the breath and Consciousness

The last two forms are stated briefly, because by now the movement is almost entirely interior. When the indwelling breath has penetrated in the way already described, the sacrifice of the vital energy is its ratification — a sealing of it — accomplished by entering that same Consciousness, casting into it the fuel, the food, the perfumes, the incense, as offerings made now within the breath rather than on any outer fire. And the sacrifice of Consciousness, the sixth and highest, is simply this: an establishing of oneself in repose, the resting in pure awareness that has been pointed to all along. There is no outer act left. The worship has become the worshipper resting as what he is.

## The seventh: the rite in the body of the Guru

Then comes the turn that completes the whole. Having performed any one of these sacrifices, if the disciple's heart has been made pure by such a doubt-free state — by the direct certainty the rite produces — then the master, simply by showing him that sacrifice, and regardless of whether the old preliminary oblations of sesame and butter were ever offered, may initiate him by a single glance. The method is *anusandhāna*, the conjoining of awareness, working through the very penetration that has been described: the teacher's recognition entering the disciple's, awareness meeting awareness, and the transmission complete in a look.

And there is a seventh form, named last and named highest. The Kula-yāga performed in the body of the Guru, the *guru-deha*, is the best of them all. It fills up and completes everything when it is performed together with the sacrifices that came before — the living teacher's own person becoming the final altar, the place where the whole sixfold rite is gathered and made whole. With that, Abhinavagupta sets down his blessing — may there be welfare — and signs the work: the labor of the great devotee of the Great Lord, the best of teachers, the glorious Abhinavagupta.

So the Tantrasara ends not in abstraction but in a rite — concrete, embodied, ascending from the cup on the altar to the union of the pair to the silent rest in Consciousness, and finally to the living body of the one who can transmit it all with a glance. The wine and the union are not transgressions smuggled in at the close. They are the path's last and most honest claim made flesh: that the one Light is present everywhere, without exception, and that the one who has truly understood this can worship it everywhere, without exception, and in that worship be free while still alive.

Here ends the twenty-second and final chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Kaula Rite.

Here ends the Tantrasara, the Essence of the Tantras, of the great master Abhinavagupta.
