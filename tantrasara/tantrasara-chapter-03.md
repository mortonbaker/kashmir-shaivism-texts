# Chapter Three: The Path of Will

The Light I have been describing is what the tradition calls the Shiva-nature — pure Consciousness, undivided, shining by its own power. The pathless path of the last chapter was for the one who steps straight into that wholeness. But almost no one can. The undivided circle is too vast, too immediate, too much like trying to see your own eyes. So this chapter is for the one who cannot enter wholeness head-on, and who needs the smallest possible foothold to begin.

This is the path of will. The tradition calls it Shambhavopaya, the way of Shambhu, of Shiva himself. It is the highest of the three methods, and the subtlest, because it uses almost nothing.

## A Foothold Made of Almost Nothing

Here is how it works. The person on this path cannot grasp the whole, so instead they catch sight of one thing: the Power of Absolute Freedom — the living self-awareness at the core of Consciousness. And they perceive it, for a moment, as if it were something extra, something additional to themselves, something they are turning toward.

Now, that perception is not strictly true. The freedom is not extra; it is their own deepest nature. Seeing it as "something additional" is, in a sense, their own imagining, a kind of useful illusion. But it is exactly the right illusion, because it gives the will something to lean into. And when the will leans fully into that freedom, thought drops away, and the person falls into a thoughtless absorption in Bhairava. No words. No mental commentary. No image of self or other. Just direct, undivided immersion in the living Light. That immersion is the whole of this path.

## Why Will, and Not Thinking

You might ask why this is called the path of will rather than the path of knowledge or action. The answer is in what it leaves out.

On this path there is no technique you perform with the body. No breath you regulate. No mantra you recite. No object you concentrate on. All of those belong to the lower, slower paths, and we will come to them. Here, the only instrument is will itself — the bare, wordless leaning of awareness toward its own freedom.

Think of the difference between three ways of warming a cold room. The slowest way is to do many physical things — gather wood, build a fire, tend it. A faster way is to understand the thermostat and adjust it. But the fastest way, if you had the power, would be to simply will the room warm. The path of will is that third thing. It skips the machinery entirely. It does not work its way to thoughtlessness through a ladder of thoughts; it arrives at thoughtlessness directly, by the sheer force of intention turning toward the Light.

This is why it is the best of the three methods, and also why it is available to so few. It asks for almost no equipment, which means everything depends on the strength of the will itself. A weak will has nothing to lean on here. It needs the supports the other paths provide.

## The Continuous Gap

Picture your normal mental life as a string of beads — thought, thought, thought, each one a small contraction of the infinite into a particular shape. Between any two beads there is a tiny gap, a flicker of pure awareness before the next thought forms. Most of us never notice these gaps. We live entirely on the beads.

What happens on the path of will is that the gap opens and stays open. The thoughts fall away, and what was a momentary flicker between them becomes a continuous, unbroken stream. And by the power of will, the person on this path can enter that gap and remain in it for as long as they choose. The string of separate beads becomes one continuous thread of light. That is the thoughtless state, and resting in it is resting in your own Shiva-nature.

## The Goal of Thoughtlessness

Do not mistake this thoughtlessness for blankness or sleep. It is not the absence of awareness. It is awareness with nothing laid over it — Consciousness no longer contracted into the shape of this thought or that one, finally experiencing itself as the boundless Light it has always been.

The purpose of all this is the one purpose of the entire book: to recognize that your innermost self is Shiva. On this path, that recognition arrives not as a conclusion you reason your way to, but as a direct flash, sudden and wordless, when the will turns home and thought lets go.

Here ends the third chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Path of Will.
