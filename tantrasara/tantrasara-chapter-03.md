# Chapter Three: The Path of Will

The Light I have been describing is what the tradition calls the Shiva-nature — pure Consciousness, undivided, shining by its own power. The pathless path of the last chapter was for the one who steps straight into that wholeness. But almost no one can. The undivided circle is too vast, too immediate, too much like trying to see your own eyes. So this chapter is for the one who cannot enter wholeness head-on, and who needs the smallest possible foothold to begin.

This is the path of will. The tradition calls it Shambhavopaya, the way of Shambhu, of Shiva himself. It is the highest of the three methods, and the subtlest, because it works almost without instruments — by the bare turning of awareness toward its own freedom.

## A Foothold Made of Almost Nothing

Listen, child, to how this begins. The seeker on this path cannot grasp the whole at once, so instead they catch sight of one thing: the Power of Absolute Freedom — the living self-awareness at the very core of Consciousness. And for a moment they perceive that freedom as if it were something extra, something additional to themselves, something they are turning toward.

Now hold this carefully, because it matters. That perception is not strictly true. The freedom is not extra; it is your own deepest nature. To see it as "something additional" is, in a sense, your own imagining — a useful illusion. But it is exactly the right illusion, because it gives the will something to lean into. And when the will leans fully into that freedom, thought drops away, and you fall into a thoughtless absorption in Bhairava — in Shiva. No words. No mental commentary. No image of self or other. Just direct, undivided immersion in the living Light. That immersion is the whole of this path.

## The Teaching He Gives: The World Is a Reflection

Now I will give you the teaching that is given to such a seeker — and it is the heart of this chapter, so attend closely. It is this: everything you see, this entire crowd of things that exist, is only a reflection appearing in the open sky of Consciousness.

Let me show you what I mean by a reflection. A reflection is something that cannot stand on its own and shine by itself — it can only appear leaning on something else, mixed into another surface. Think of a face appearing in a mirror. The face in the glass is real as an appearance, but it cannot exist there on its own; it appears only upon the mirror. Think of the taste of something that floods your mouth with water — the taste appears, but it appears in the saliva, not standing alone. Think of a scent appearing in the nose, the touch of an embrace appearing in the body's capacity for pleasure, the touch of something sharp appearing in the body's inward sense of pain, an echo appearing in empty space. In every case, the appearance is real, but it is a reflection — it shows up only by resting on the surface that holds it.

Abhinavagupta is careful here, and I want you to follow his care. He is not saying these things are mere tricks, that the taste or the scent or the echo is nothing at all. They genuinely appear; they have real effects. He is saying something more precise: they appear the way a reflection appears — never freestanding, always borne upon a ground that holds them. And just so, he says, the entire universe appears upon the Light of the Supreme Lord. Every thing that exists is a reflection held in the mirror of Consciousness.

## A Reflection of What?

Here a sharp question arises, and you should feel its force. Every reflection we know is a reflection *of* something. The face in the mirror is the reflection of a real face standing before it. So — a student might ask — if the whole universe is a reflection in Consciousness, then what is the original object being reflected? Where is the face that casts this image?

And here is Abhinavagupta's startling answer: there is no original object. There is nothing standing outside Consciousness casting its image in. Let the question go — there simply is no external "face" being mirrored here.

But then, the student presses, is the reflection uncaused? Does it appear from nothing? No, he says — it has a cause, but the cause is not some outside object. The cause is the Lord's own Power, his Absolute Freedom. Consciousness does not receive the world's image from anywhere. It throws up the whole universe as a reflection within itself, out of its own free power, with no second thing required. This is the deep difference between Consciousness and an ordinary mirror, and it is the secret of the whole chapter.

## The Mirror That Knows Itself

Consider an ordinary mirror. It holds a whole landscape within it — mountains, sky, moving figures — an intricate scene reflected in its surface. But the mirror does not know that it holds anything. It has no awareness of the world it carries. It is inert.

Now consider Consciousness, which holds the entire universe reflected within it. The one and only difference — but it is everything — is that Consciousness *knows* what it holds. It is aware of itself and of the whole world shining within it. If it were not self-aware, Abhinavagupta says, it would be exactly as dead as glass; a Consciousness that did not know itself would be a contradiction, an awareness that is not aware. So this self-knowing is not some added feature. It is the very thing that makes Consciousness conscious rather than inert. The universe shines within awareness the way a vast and intricate design shines within a mirror — but, unlike the mirror, awareness savors and knows the whole of it as itself.

That self-knowing has a name in the tradition: it is the inner "I," the self-awareness that is inseparable from Consciousness, the silent source of all sound and speech. And out of that one self-awareness the Lord unfolds his whole array of powers — the entire spread of expression, every letter of every word, the full alphabet of creation from the first vibration to the last. You need not hold the technical detail of how each sound unfolds from each power. Hold only this: the one Consciousness, by knowing itself, spontaneously pours out of itself the entire structured universe of names and forms, and then shines it all back to itself as a reflection it fully recognizes as its own.

## Why Will, and Not Thinking

Now you can see why this is called the path of will rather than the path of knowledge or action. On this path there is no technique you perform with the body. No breath you regulate. No mantra you recite. No object you concentrate on. All of those belong to the lower, slower paths, and we will come to them. Here the only instrument is will itself — the bare, wordless leaning of awareness toward its own freedom, until it sees the whole world as its own reflection and rests, thought-free, as the Light that holds it.

Think of the difference between three ways of warming a cold room. The slowest way is to do many physical things — gather wood, build a fire, tend it. A faster way is to understand the thermostat and adjust it. But the fastest way, if you had the power, would be to simply will the room warm. The path of will is that third thing. It skips the machinery. It does not climb to thoughtlessness by a ladder of thoughts; it arrives directly, by the sheer force of awareness turning toward the Light and recognizing everything as its own shining.

This is why it is the best of the three methods, and also why it is available to so few. It asks for almost no equipment, which means everything rests on the strength and clarity of the seeing itself. A wavering awareness has nothing here to lean on. It needs the supports the other paths provide — and those are what I will give you next.

## The Goal of Thoughtlessness

Do not mistake this thoughtlessness for blankness or sleep. It is not the absence of awareness. It is awareness with nothing laid over it — Consciousness no longer contracted into the shape of this thought or that one, knowing the whole universe as its own reflection and itself as the boundless Light in which that reflection shines.

The purpose of all this is the one purpose of the entire book: to recognize that your innermost self is Shiva. On this path, that recognition arrives not as a conclusion you reason your way to, but as a direct flash, sudden and wordless, when the will turns home, the world is seen as a reflection in your own awareness, and thought lets go. And in this, the text reminds us, no mantra, no ritual, no outer instrument constrains you at all. There is only awareness, free, recognizing itself.

Here ends the third chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Path of Will.
