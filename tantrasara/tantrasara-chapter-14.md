# Chapter Fourteen: The Liberating Initiation

The last chapter brought the candidate across the threshold. This chapter describes the great initiation that follows — the one that does not merely admit a person to the path but cuts the very root of their bondage and seals their liberation. The tradition calls the recipient a spiritual son of the teacher, a putraka, because this initiation is a true second birth: the soul is reborn out of limitation into freedom. Abhinavagupta tells us at the outset that he has set all of this out at great length elsewhere, in the larger work, the Tantraloka, and that what he gives us here is the condensed form. So listen to this as a distillation — the essential shape of a rite whose every detail could fill a book of its own.

## Setting the Stage: The Trident-and-Lotus Mandala

The rite does not begin abruptly. The disciple has first undergone the preliminary consecration, the one that makes him a samayi — a person bound by the sacred commitments, brought to the edge of the deeper mystery. Then, on the third day after that, the teacher prepares the great sacred diagram, and it is worth picturing carefully, because this diagram is the stage on which the whole drama unfolds.

It is called the mandala of the trident and the lotus. Imagine a three-pronged spear, a trident, rising up out of a root, and at the very top of each prong a white lotus blooming open. This is not mere decoration. The trident is the body of the cosmos itself, and the teacher is about to populate it with the living powers that govern reality.

He works from the outside in. First he honors what stands outside the diagram — the outer retinue and the guardians of the doorway, the powers at the perimeter. Then he moves into the first portion of the diagram, and here he worships a sequence of presences in order, sweeping from the corner that belongs to Shiva around toward the southeast. He honors Ganapati, the remover of obstacles, first. Then the line of his own teachers: his Guru, the Guru's Guru, and the Guru before that — the grand-teacher and the great-grand-teacher — and then the whole company of past masters, the Acharyas who carried this knowledge down the generations. Then he honors the circle of the Yoginis, the fierce feminine powers of the tradition; then Vagishvari, the Goddess of Speech herself, the living power of the sacred word; and finally the protector of the field, the guardian of the very ground the rite stands on.

Only now does he take up the central act. He establishes the entire course of reality along the trident, laying it out from the root of the spear all the way up to the white lotus at the top. And then he installs the three great Goddesses on the three prongs. On the middle prong he worships Para — the Supreme, the highest — together with Lord Bhairava, the two of them united at the center. On the left prong he places Apara, the Goddess in her immanent, worldly aspect. On the right prong, Parapara, the Goddess who holds the middle ground between the transcendent and the immanent. And on the two flanking tridents he installs these powers again — Parapara in the heart of the right one, Apara in the heart of the left — so that the whole structure is saturated with the Goddess at every level.

And here the text says something that is easy to rush past but is the doctrinal heart of the whole arrangement: because the Goddess presides over every single place in this diagram, everything within it becomes Full, becomes Perfect and Complete, by virtue of Her supremacy. The diagram is not a map of separate compartments. It is one undivided fullness, because one Power saturates all of it. Then, at the very center — on the middle prong of the middle trident — the teacher gathers the entire host of deities in unity, all the way down to the very weapons held by the guardians of the directions. Everything is present, and everything is one.

## The Union of the Five Substrata

Now comes a contemplative act that is the secret engine of the whole rite. There are five places where the sacred is concentrated in this ceremony: the small ritual vessel, the great vessel, the mandala itself, the sacred fire, and the teacher's own Self. Ordinary thinking would keep these five separate — five different objects, five different locations. The teacher does the opposite. Through non-dual contemplation, he performs the union of these five substrata. He holds them as one. The water in the small pot, the water in the great pot, the powers in the diagram, the living fire, and his own consciousness — these are not five things sprinkled around a room. They are one reality wearing five forms, and the teacher's contemplation dissolves the seeming boundaries between them.

Then comes the special worship — the flowers and the other offerings — but offered, the text is careful to say, nourished by the sap of non-duality with the Supreme Lord. The offering is not a transaction with a distant deity. It is the savoring of one's own non-difference from the Lord, and the flowers are simply the outer form that savoring takes.

## The Generosity of the Rite, and Its Honesty

There is a strikingly practical, almost ethical instruction folded in here. The teacher is to fill the sacrificial site with a satisfying offering of food, and he is to do this without any dishonesty regarding money. No cheating, no cutting corners on the expense. And then a hard rule follows from that: when there genuinely is no money, the great-mandala sacrifice simply must not be performed. The rite is not to be done on the cheap, faked, or half-resourced. Either it is done fully and honestly, or it waits. The integrity of the offering is not separable from the integrity of the act.

## The Compassionate Sacrifice

Now we come to a part of the chapter that the modern listener may find difficult, and it deserves to be heard exactly as the text gives it, not softened away. The rite involves the offering of living animals.

Abhinavagupta does not flinch from this, but he frames it in a way that turns the usual logic inside out. The animals offered, he says, themselves become receptacles of Grace. The creature given into this sacrifice is not destroyed and discarded; it is lifted, made a vessel of the very descent of divine favor that the whole rite is about. And so, he says, the teacher should not be plagued by doubt about these animal offerings — provided, and this is the crucial word, they are done with compassion. The compassion is not a softening of the act. It is the condition that makes the act sacramental rather than cruel. The animal is not prey; it is a participant being carried into Grace.

Then the specific sequence: having satisfied the Supreme Lord with sesame and clarified butter poured into the fire, the teacher places another animal before him for the offering of its marrow. With that marrow he satisfies the whole assembly of deities, and then worships the mandala once again. After this, he turns directly to the Lord in a kind of pleading contemplation, holding the recognition that his own Self is completely full — full of the entire sixfold course of reality, in unity with everything that is. And in that state of fullness, he calls the disciple to stand before him.

## Reaching the Absent and the Dead

Before the purification proper, the chapter pauses to handle a beautiful edge case — and it tells us something important about what this initiation actually is. What if the one to be initiated is not present? What if he is far away, or even already dead?

The rite reaches him anyway. The teacher meditates upon the absent one as though he stood there in front of him. Or he places before himself an image — a small figure fashioned out of kusha grass, or cow-dung, or some such material — and treats that image as the disciple's body. He sprinkles this stand-in with drops from the sacred vessel, worships it with flowers, and lays the entire course of reality upon it, just as he would upon a living body present in the room. The initiation, in other words, is not finally a transaction between two physical bodies. It is an act of consciousness, and consciousness is not stopped by distance or by death.

## Whose Karma Is Purified

Here the chapter makes a genuine doctrinal distinction, not just a procedural one — and it turns on what the disciple actually wants. Three cases.

There is the one who still desires enjoyment, the bubhuksu, the seeker of worldly delight who is not yet done with the world. For him, the teacher does not purify his good action. That good karma is left intact, because it is precisely the vehicle that will carry him to the enjoyments he is still reaching for.

There is the one who desires liberation, the mumuksu. For him, the teacher purifies both the good and the bad — because the liberation-seeker is to be freed from the whole machinery of consequence, and good karma binds just as surely as bad, only with a golden chain instead of an iron one.

And then there is the most complete form, the seedless initiation, the nirbija-diksa. Here the teacher purifies even the bonds of the samaya commitments themselves — not a single seed of future binding is left in the ground. Nothing remains that could sprout into another life.

And the chapter insists, with real tenderness, that this rite must be performed even for someone utterly dull, even for a person of the slightest understanding whose death is already near. This is the Supreme Lord's own command. For such a one, success does not depend on comprehension or capacity at all. It comes through mere resolution — the sincere turning of the will — joined to devotion: devotion to the Guru, or to the deity, or to the fire. That is enough. The door is open even to the one with almost nothing to bring but his willingness.

## The Vasana Doctrine: Why the Teacher Must Read the Soul

Now the chapter gives us what is arguably its single most distinctive philosophical claim, and it explains why this initiation can never be merely mechanical.

The differentiating factor in all of this — the thing that makes one initiation work one way and another work another way — is the teacher's perception of the vasanas. The vasanas are the latent impressions, the deep residual tendencies that a soul has accumulated, the grooves worn into it by its history. And here is the principle: the mantras produce their various effects according to these vasanas. The same sacred word does not do the same thing in every soul. It acts upon what it finds. So the teacher must read the disciple — must perceive the latent impressions lying everywhere within him — because the mantra's power will follow the contour of those impressions like water following the shape of the ground.

This is why the purification is not a stamp pressed identically on every candidate. The teacher, having explored the particular vasanas of this particular soul, then purifies — through a special, focused remembrance of the principal mantra — the entire course of reality residing in the disciple's body, all the while holding the contemplation of that soul's unity with Shiva.

And he does this along a precise somatic path. He sweeps the purification upward through the body, in succession, beginning at the great toe and rising all the way to the dvadashanta — the subtle point twelve finger-widths above the crown, above the aperture at the top of the skull. From the lowest point of the standing body to that point in the space just beyond the head: the whole course is lifted, so that in the end the disciple's body and consciousness are brought into perfect unity with the teacher's own. Two souls, one fire.

## The Fire Ritual: Purifying the Tattvas

Then comes the offering in the fire itself, and here the chapter is exact about the form. Pronouncing the venerable mantra of Para, the teacher speaks the formula of purification: "I purify such-and-such category of reality" — naming each tattva in turn — and seals each utterance with the word svaha as the offering goes into the flames. Three oblations for each category. And when a category is complete, a full oblation, sealed this time with the word vaushat.

In this way he proceeds up the entire ladder of reality, purifying the tattvas one after another, all the way up to Shiva at the summit. This is the great ritual idea at the center of the chapter: the whole cosmos that the soul is spread across is taken up, level by level, recognized, offered into the fire, and dissolved upward.

What is happening here, seen rightly, is the reversal of creation performed upon a single soul. The long descent — Consciousness contracting itself down through the thirty-six categories into a limited person, the descent this book has been mapping all along — is now run backward. The denser levels melt into the subtler, the subtler into the subtler still, until what remains rests in the pure Light at the top. The disciple is walked back up the staircase of the worlds until he stands once more at the summit, undivided.

After the tattvas are purified, a few final offerings complete the work. A full oblation by the method called the yojanika, the joining; another offering for the sake of leading the enjoyment-seeker toward the place of his enjoyment; and another for the manifestation of the pure categories of reality. And then the worship of the Guru, with the payments due to him. This is the initiation that makes a person a spiritual son. And the chapter states its precise effect: all karma, past and future, is purified — with one exception. The karma that has already begun to bear fruit in this present life, the momentum that set this very body in motion, that remains. It must simply be lived out.

## Cutting the Knot, and What Is Really Removed

So there is a decisive severing at the heart of this rite. The bond that ties the soul to its limitation — the deep knot of ignorance that has held it through countless lives — is cut. The cord that bound the boat to the dock is severed, and the soul is free to return to its ocean. And because the karma already in motion must finish, the liberation is sealed but not always instantaneous: the disciple is destined for freedom either in this very life, or with certainty at the body's death, when the last of that present momentum is spent and nothing remains to hold him.

But be careful not to picture this purification as the removal of some real substance from the soul. The soul was never actually bound by a real chain; it was bound by a misunderstanding, by the contracted self-experience this book called existential ignorance in its opening chapters. What the purification truly removes is not a thing but a false identification — the soul's mistaken sense that it *is* these lower levels, that it *is* small and separate and caught. The elaborate ascent through the categories is the ritual enactment of a single recognition: that none of these levels was ever the soul's true identity, that the soul was always the Light at the summit, merely dreaming itself down the staircase.

## The Role of the Teacher

This places an enormous weight on the teacher, and the chapter does not hide it. To lead another soul up the entire path, the teacher must himself be established at its summit. You cannot lift someone to a height you do not stand on. He must be one in whom the recognition is firm — one who has perceived the disciple's vasanas, united the five substrata in his own contemplation, and become a true and living channel of Grace, so that when he reaches across to the disciple it is the Lord's own power that flows through his hand.

The chapter closes with two verses that say it as plainly as it can be said. After combining, inside and out, his own existence — which consists of all the courses of reality — the well-awakened teacher, in whom duality has fallen away, should make the intellect, the vital breath, and the body of the disciple one with his own. And having instantly attained that unity with the disciple, when he comes to rest in That which is full of Great Bliss and Consciousness, then the limited being — the small bound soul standing before the fire — enters the very State of the Self of Shiva.

That is the whole movement of this chapter: one awakened soul reaching down to where another is sleeping, taking it by the hand, and walking it home — through the trident-mandala, through the compassionate fire, up the staircase of the worlds from the great toe to the space above the crown, past the severed knot, into the open Light at the top.

Here ends the fourteenth chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Liberating Initiation.
