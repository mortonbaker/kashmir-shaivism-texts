# Chapter Nineteen: Rites for the Dead

There is a moment in every tradition when it has to turn and face the thing it has been quietly walking toward the whole time. Death. The book has spent chapter after chapter on the living soul — its bondage, its initiation, its recognition of itself as the Lord. Now it stops at the deathbed and asks a harder question. What do we do for the one who has died? And the answer the chapter gives is two answers at once, held in the same hand, and the tension between them is the whole teaching. For most of the dead, there is a great deal to be done. For the one who knew the truth, there is nothing to be done at all — and his death is not a grief but a festival.

Let us take them in order, because the order is where the meaning lives.

## Who the Rites Are For

Begin with a distinction the chapter is careful to draw. The funeral rites are not a blanket obligation laid over every corpse. They are aimed at specific people, in specific predicaments.

First, those who died holding inferior doctrines — and the text does not flinch from saying this includes even the Gurus of those doctrines. A man may have been a teacher, may have led others, may have died respected, and still have spent his life inside a smaller understanding than the one this tradition holds. His standing in his own school does not deliver him. He needs the rite.

Second, and this is the subtler case, those who held the superior doctrine but let it slip. The word the tradition uses is *samaya* — the binding observances, the vows and disciplines that an initiate takes on — together with *ācāra*, right conduct, the practice lived out day to day. A soul could have been properly initiated into the highest teaching and then lost its grip on those commitments, fallen out of the observance, and died without performing the *prāyaścitta*, the expiation that would have set it right again. Such a soul, for all its initiation, dies with an unsettled account. It too needs the rite.

So immediately after death, the qualified one performs what the tradition calls the *antyeṣṭi* — literally the final sacrifice, the last purificatory ritual, which here functions as a kind of initiation administered to the dead. And he does it through a genuine *śaktipāta*, a descent of Power that arises for this very purpose: the deliverance of the dead. This is not the officiant's private invention or a comforting ceremony for the bereaved. The chapter is emphatic. This is the command of the Supreme Lord. The authority to act on behalf of the dead comes from above, not from custom.

## The Funeral Sacrifice on the Corpse

Now the chapter describes what is actually done, and here the imagery is vivid and strange and should not be softened.

Everything the tradition has already taught for the deliverance of the dead is performed upon the body itself. The corpse is not an empty husk to be disposed of. It is the site of the work. And the one performing it does so by the three great instruments the whole system has trusted all along — *kriyā*, action; *jñāna*, knowledge; and *yoga*, the disciplined union — and he does it with conviction. The text adds a phrase that tells you it knows exactly what it is doing. He performs the rite with conviction in order to establish conviction among the foolish. The rite is partly for the dead soul and partly for the watching living, who need to see that something real is happening, so that their faith is steadied rather than scattered.

The procedure runs like this. The officiant places the *mantras* upon the corpse by the method of dissolution — drawing the layers of the dead person's manifest existence back down, folding the cosmos of that individual back toward its source. Then, by what the tradition calls the method of the net, he draws the departed soul near, as a fisherman gathers what has drifted away. And then come the operations that sound almost violent and are meant to: *rodhana*, the binding or arresting; *vedhana*, the piercing; *ghaṭṭana*, the stirring or agitation. He moves the vital energy — the *prāṇa* — through the great centers of heart, throat, and forehead, and at this the corpse itself trembles. The body shakes, and that trembling is the sign that the work has taken hold, that the soul has been seized and is being moved.

Then comes the union the whole rite exists for: the *yojanikā*, the joining of that soul to Supreme Śiva. The deceased is wedded back into the one Consciousness from which it was never truly separate. And only then, with that union accomplished, is the corpse burned — burned while the full oblation, the *pūrṇāhuti*, is poured into the fire. The cremation is not the disposal that precedes the spiritual work. It is the seal placed upon it after the soul has already been delivered.

## The Offering-Rite for the Dead

The deliverance does not end at the pyre. For those purified by the funeral sacrifice — and the chapter notes, even for others — there is a further rite, and this is the *śrāddha* proper, the offering to the dead that gives the chapter its name.

This is an initiation in its own right, and it unfolds across three days. It is performed on the fourth day after death, and then again each month, and then again each year — the rhythm of remembrance stretched out across the calendar so that the dead are not delivered once and forgotten but held in an ongoing relation. After the ritual that culminates in the *homa*, the fire-offering, the officiant prepares an offering of food and takes it up in one hand. And here the outward gesture becomes an inward contemplation, which is the heart of it.

He meditates on a certain power. The tradition names its nature as *vīrya* — virility, potency, the seminal force of consciousness itself. And the form of this power is precise. It is the knowable that has come into identity with the knowing-power as that power exists within the limited individual. Hold that carefully, because it is doing real work. Every limited being is, at root, the universal knowing Consciousness contracted into the shape of a knower. Within that contracted knower, the objects it knows — the *vedya*, the knowable — have become bound up with the very power that knows them. The officiant takes that whole knot, the knowable fused with its knowing-power, and offers it to the Supreme Lord, who is named here as the Enjoyer, the one for whom all experience is finally a feast.

And the teaching that follows is the pivot of the entire offering. When the state of being-a-knowable — *vedyatā*, the condition of being an object, of being something merely known and therefore something less than the knower — when that condition ceases, the soul becomes the Lord Himself. To stop being an object is to recognize oneself as the universal Subject. The whole machinery of the rite, all the fire and food and gesture, is in service of that single dissolution: the melting away of objecthood, so that what remains is nothing but the Lord recognizing himself.

## Many Rites, Many Fruits — and One Truth Beneath Them

The chapter then steps back and does something honest about its own apparatus. It admits that any single one of these rites would suffice. The funeral sacrifice alone, or the deliverance of the dead alone, or the offering-rite alone — any one of them can accomplish the end. So why perform all of them?

Because of what the one performing them wants. For the soul that still desires enjoyment — that wants the many fruits the world can yield — a multitude of fruits requires a multitude of acts. If you want abundance, you do everything, because each rite yields its own harvest. And even for the one who desires not enjoyment but *mokṣa*, liberation, the rites are still worth doing, but in a different spirit. For that soul, the whole sequence functions like a daily religious practice — a steady, repeated returning whose only aim is identification with That, with the Supreme. The same actions serve the pleasure-seeker and the liberation-seeker, but they mean entirely different things depending on who performs them and why.

And then the chapter turns, and the turn is everything.

## The Knower Needs None of It

For the one who knows the Truth, no rite is needed. Not the funeral sacrifice, not the deliverance, not the offering — not one act from the first to the last. The entire elaborate machinery of deliverance, which the chapter has just described in such loving and gruesome detail, simply does not apply to him. He requires no help across the threshold, because for him there is no threshold. He never mistook himself for an object that could be lost.

And so his death is not a thing to be mourned. The chapter makes a claim here that overturns the ordinary human relationship to dying. The death of the knower is a day of festival — a holiday, a celebration — for those who stand in the spiritual lineage of his Knowledge. Why a festival? Because his death fills the portion of Consciousness. When such a one dies, nothing drains away; something is completed, poured back full into the single reservoir of awareness that all knowers share.

And here is the reason it works, stated as plainly as the tradition ever states it. That lineage is really only one Consciousness. The teacher and the disciple and the disciple's disciple are not a chain of separate lamps passing a flame down the line. They are one light, appearing at different points. So the death of the knower is, for the lineage, no different from a day that is already openly joyful — the day when a child of the line received Knowledge from a teacher who was still alive. In both cases the one Consciousness recognizes itself a little more fully through one of its own apparent points. The transmission that happens at death and the transmission that happens in life are the same event, because the one in whom it happens was never really two.

This is why grief misreads the situation. Grief assumes a loss, assumes that something here has gone there, that a someone has become a no one. But if the lineage is one Consciousness, then nothing has gone anywhere. The festival is the correct response, not because death should be denied, but because, seen truly, nothing has been subtracted.

## The Heart of the Whole Thing

One more note, and the chapter gives it the weight of a final instruction. Everywhere in this ritual — in the *śrāddha* and all that surrounds it — the single most important element is the *mūrti-yāga*, the worship of the form, the consecration of the embodied vessel through which the dead is held and offered. The chapter attributes this judgment to the venerable *Siddhamata*, the tradition of the perfected ones, and it does not expound the *mūrti-yāga* here. It defers it, promising that the rite will be described where the occasional observances are treated. So the chapter points beyond itself, marking its own most important piece as something to be unfolded later, in its proper place.

And the closing words lift the entire discussion up off the level of technique altogether.

He whom Śiva, in his devotion to Grace, chooses to favor — that one becomes the Supreme Lord, and there is nothing strange or surprising in this at all. It is the most natural thing in the world for the Lord to recognize the Lord. The adoption of any means whatsoever — the funeral fire, the offered food, the trembling corpse, the smallest gesture or the greatest — is itself nothing but the Lord's own Dominance, his *aiśvarya*, his sovereign power expressing itself through a chosen instrument. Once you understand this, you stop worrying about whether a given means is large enough or worthy enough or powerful enough to do the work. A wise person, knowing that the power was never in the means but always in the Lord who wields it, does not doubt even the most limited of means.

That is where the chapter rests. It has described the most elaborate of rites in unsparing detail, and then quietly told you that the rites were never the point. The point is the one Consciousness, delivering itself, recognizing itself, and — when one of its knowers lays the body down — not grieving, but keeping festival.

Here ends the nineteenth chapter of the Tantrasara, on the rites for the dead.
