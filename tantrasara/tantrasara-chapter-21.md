# Chapter Twenty-One: The Authority of Scripture

We have walked, in the chapters behind us, through the whole architecture of practice. The regular rites and the occasional rites, the daily and the seasonal, the obligatory observances and the ones called for by circumstance — all of that has been laid out and examined. And now a question rises that the careful listener has perhaps been holding in reserve the whole time. By what right does any of it stand? On what authority does the teacher say "do this, and not that"? Where does scripture get its power to bind, to instruct, to be believed?

This chapter answers that question. Its name is *āgama-prāmāṇya* — the authoritativeness of revealed scripture. *Āgama* is what has "come down," the handed-on revelation; *prāmāṇya* is the quality of being a valid means of knowledge, a thing you may rightly rely on. And the answer the chapter gives is not the answer you might expect. It does not appeal to a list of credentials or a chain of custody. It goes underneath all of that, to the nature of awareness itself.

## Scripture as the Reflection of the Real

Begin where this whole tradition begins. The universe consists of nothing but Consciousness. There is no second stuff alongside it, no inert matter standing outside the Light to be illuminated from elsewhere. Everything that is, is Consciousness — and Consciousness is not a blank, passive glow. It has within it *vimarśa*, self-awareness, the active "speaking" of awareness to itself. Where pure light merely shines, *vimarśa* is the light's knowing that it shines, the turning-back of awareness upon its own radiance. It is the difference between a lamp and a lamp that knows it is lit.

Hold that, and now the chapter makes its decisive move. A scripture, it says, is the *vimarśana* — the reflective awareness — of the reality that resides in all the worlds, and of the entire variety of action-and-fruit relations that dwell within that reality. Read that slowly, because it inverts the usual picture. We tend to imagine scripture as a report about reality, a description composed by someone who looked at the world and wrote down what they saw, a text standing at a distance from its subject. This chapter says no. Scripture is reality's own reflective awareness of itself. It is the moment when the Consciousness that is everything turns and considers what it contains — every world, every soul, and every link between what one does and what one reaps. Scripture is not a map drawn from outside the territory. It is the territory becoming aware of its own shape.

And from this everything follows. Because scripture is the self-reflection of the Supreme Lord, it is not different from His essential nature. It is not a creation set apart from Him, the way a book is set apart from its author. It is Him, reflecting. And so the entire collection of scriptures — all of them, the whole library of revealed texts across every tradition — really leads to one fruit and aims at one office. The word for that office is *adhikāra*, which means both qualification and appointed task: the single competence, the single purpose, toward which all genuine scripture is bent. One fruit. One office. Beneath the staggering variety of revealed texts, a single aim.

## Why, Then, the Many Doctrines?

But here is the obvious objection, and the chapter meets it head-on. If all scripture is one reflection of one Lord aimed at one fruit, why does the world contain so many doctrines, so many systems, each insisting on its own account and quarreling with the others? Why is revelation not a single clear voice?

The answer is *niyati* — the power of restriction. *Niyati* is one of the Lord's own powers, the force by which the boundless becomes bound, by which the infinite consents to appear as the limited and particular. It is the principle of "this and not that," the constraint that gives each thing its definite place and keeps it there. And because of the greatness of this power, people become established each in his own portion. Not everyone stands where the whole can be seen at once. *Niyati* distributes seekers across the field, each one rooted in the measure of revelation his nature can presently hold.

So what looks like contradiction among the scriptures is really distribution along a ladder. The doctrines are not rivals scattered across a flat plain, each as good or as wrong as the next. They are rungs, arranged by how near they bring the seeker to the truth — and the chapter names them in ascending order, each one truer than the one below.

## The Ascending Ladder

At the lowest rung stand the **Vedas** and the systems akin to them. Their consciousness is dualistic — they take the knower and the known, the self and the Lord, the worshipper and the worshipped, to be really separate. Theirs is the awareness suited to *māyā*, to the realm of difference and limitation, the world as it appears once *niyati* has done its work of separation. This is not contempt. The Veda is genuine revelation; it is simply pitched at the level of the divided world, and it can carry a seeker only as far as that level reaches.

A rung higher stand the **Sāṅkhya** and **Vaiṣṇava** scriptures. These reach further toward liberation — they take the question of freedom seriously and aim the seeker at release. But their conception of liberation is, the chapter says, erroneous. The Sāṅkhya imagines freedom as the bare isolation of the spirit from matter, a pure spectator standing forever apart; the Vaiṣṇava reaches toward a Lord but still across a gap. They have turned toward the goal, but they have mistaken its nature. Closer than the Veda, and still short.

Higher again stand the scriptures of **Śaiva-Siddhānta** and its kin. Now we are within the Śaiva revelation proper, and the seeker is brought into close contact with the nature of Śiva. But it is Śiva conceived as the isolated, the transcendent — the Lord held apart above His creation, pure and supreme but withdrawn from the world He surveys. This is real and high attainment. The contact is genuine. Yet it rests on a Śiva who is only above, not also within.

So a rung higher still stand the **Matanga** and the scriptures of its family. Here the seeker comes into close contact with the Supreme Lordship as immanent — present in all things, the Lord not merely above creation but pervading it, indwelling, the very sap of every existing thing. Where Śaiva-Siddhānta gave the transcendent Śiva, this gives the immanent one. A truer rung, because it recovers the half that pure transcendence left out.

And then, the rarest of the rare — so rare the chapter marks it as the attainment of almost no one — the venerable course of the **Trika** scriptures. This is the summit, and it is this tradition's own home. Here the seeker comes into close contact with the nature of the Supreme Lord as Consciousness itself: the Highest Reality, Absolute Freedom, and Bliss, utterly devoid of all limitation. Not Śiva-above-only, as the Siddhānta had it. Not Śiva-within-only, as the Matanga had it. But the full Lord, who is transcendent and immanent at once, who is the absolute freedom that is beyond every world and the bliss that is the heart of every world, with no limitation left standing anywhere. The Trika does not add a rung to the ladder so much as it arrives at the top of it, where transcendence and immanence are seen to have been one thing all along.

That is the ladder: Veda, then Sāṅkhya and Vaiṣṇava, then Śaiva-Siddhānta, then Matanga, then Trika. Each rung is true. Each is truer than the one below. And — the chapter is careful to add — there are some who do not climb rung by rung at all, but by abandoning each prior system, or by leaping clean over them, accomplish the one fruit directly through the one revealed scripture. The ladder is the ordinary route. It is not the only one.

## The Authority Even of the Lower Rungs

Now comes a generosity that is easy to miss and important not to. Having ranked the doctrines, the chapter does not then strip authority from the lower ones. Even the dualistic doctrines, it insists, keep their authoritativeness — because they too are the effect of the one Lord. Nothing that flows from the single source is false at its root. A lower revelation is not a wrong revelation; it is a true revelation pitched to a particular capacity.

And so, where the scriptures agree on a point, there is straightforward harmony and identity — they are simply saying the one thing together. And where they oppose one another, the chapter shrugs: mutual opposition over differing topics affects nothing. If two scriptures contradict each other, look closely and you will usually find they are speaking to different topics, different conditions, different seekers — and then the contradiction is no contradiction at all, only two true statements about two different cases. The chapter reaches for a homely example. Take the rule that one may not kill. It seems absolute, and yet it admits exceptions — the killing sanctioned in particular ritual or particular circumstance. The rule and its exception do not refute each other. The difference between them rests on a difference of latent impressions and of context — on which seeker, in which situation, carrying which accumulated tendencies. Truth bends to the case without ceasing to be truth.

What, then, makes one scripture excellent and another less so, if all are valid? Excellence comes through the excellence of the fruit. A revelation is higher not because the lower is false but because it yields a higher result. The chapter gives the Veda's own internal example: within the Veda, it is the Upaniṣadic portion — the part that turns from rite toward the knowledge of the Self — that stands highest, because the fruit it offers surpasses the fruit of the ritual portions. The same measure that ranks the Upaniṣad within the Veda ranks the whole ladder. Not "which is true and which is false," but "which carries the seeker furthest."

## Established Conviction and the Refuge in the Supreme

Beneath all of this the chapter sets a quiet, radical foundation, and to see it you have to look at how anyone knows anything at all. We live, every one of us, inside *prasiddhi* — established conviction, the innate and settled cognition that we do not argue our way to but simply already stand upon. The ordinary person crosses the street, trusts the ground, speaks to a friend, eats a meal, all on the strength of convictions so deeply established that they are never examined. *Prasiddhi* is the bedrock of all ordinary valid knowing. We do not prove the world before we live in it; we live in it on the authority of an established conviction that comes before proof.

And the world, the chapter observes, is full of many such convictions — a great crowd of *prasiddhis*, layered and competing. But here is the thing: whoever a person is, by the very force of his own nature, he wishes for the *prasiddhi* that culminates in the heart. Something in each of us reaches, underneath all the surface convictions, for the one settled certainty that rests at the center, the conviction that the heart itself is straining toward. And so the chapter gives its rule for how to choose among scriptures. One should regard as valid that established conviction — that revealed scripture — in which a superior result is taken as refuge. The scripture worth standing on is the one that takes the highest fruit as its shelter. No other test is needed. No other is needed at all.

And the chapter closes, as these chapters do, by lifting from argument into invocation. He — the Lord, the Self, Consciousness — is aware exactly as He shines. There is no gap in Him between the shining and the knowing of it; He knows Himself precisely as He radiates, in the form of the Highest Reality of the Light of Consciousness. Therefore, says the text to the one who ponders these things: take refuge in the scripture that is *Anuttara* — the Unsurpassed, that beyond which there is nothing higher — the scripture whose very essence is the awareness of the Splendor of Consciousness, the Consciousness that is made of all things and leaves nothing outside itself.

And the last word turns the whole inquiry back onto the listener's own life. Just as every ordinary person, free of doubt, goes about ordinary life firmly planted on his established convictions — never pausing to prove the ground he walks on, simply living on the strength of what he takes for settled — so, in exactly that same way, by establishing himself in a conviction that transcends ordinary men, a person becomes the Supreme Śiva. The whole logic of *prasiddhi* turns out to be a doorway. You already know how to live wholly on the authority of an unexamined certainty; you do it every waking hour. The teaching is only this: let the certainty you live on be the highest one. Stand on the conviction that culminates in the heart, the refuge that is *Anuttara*, with the same unshaken confidence with which you now stand on the ground — and the standing itself becomes the becoming. You do not reach the Supreme Śiva across a distance. You take refuge in Him, and the refuge is the recognition that you were never anywhere else.

Here ends the twenty-first chapter of the Tantrasāra, on the authority of revealed scripture.
