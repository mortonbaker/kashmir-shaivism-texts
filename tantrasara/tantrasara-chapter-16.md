# Chapter Sixteen: Initiation of the Absent — The Great Net

Every initiation we have walked through so far has had a body in the room. A living disciple, present, breathing, kneeling before the teacher, ready to be led up the ladder of the worlds. This chapter does something that, at first hearing, sounds impossible. It initiates someone who is not there.

That is the whole subject of this chapter, and it is worth saying plainly before we go a single step further, because it is easy to confuse with the chapter before it. The previous teaching concerned a disciple who is present but dying. This one concerns a disciple who is absent altogether. The Sanskrit name is parokṣa-dīkṣā — parokṣa meaning "out of sight," "beyond the eye," the one not before you. And the teaching divides cleanly into two cases. The absent one may be dead. Or the absent one may be alive but far away. Mṛta and jīvat. The dead and the living-distant. Hold those two branches in mind, because the whole chapter hangs from them.

## Who Can Be Initiated After Death

Start with the harder case, the one that makes people lean in. The dead.

Not just any dead person. The text is careful about who qualifies. Picture the cases it lists. A devoted student who died while still in service to his teacher, the work unfinished, the grace already descended but the body gone before the rite could be completed. One who was banished, driven out, and died apart from the community. One killed by hostile incantation — by abhicāra, the dark ritual aimed at another's life. One who died as a child, before there was time for any of this. One in whom the longing for liberation flared up at the very moment of dying, too late for the body to act on it. And, more broadly, anyone in whom the descent of Power, śaktipāta, had genuinely fallen, by whatever means.

What unites them is this: grace had touched them, but death came first. The readiness was real; the timing was cruel. These are the ones for whom this rite exists.

And one thing must be set aside immediately. For a dead person, the ordinary consecration — the abhiṣeka, the sprinkling and enthroning that crowns a living initiate — simply does not apply. You cannot anoint a body that is not there. So the whole machinery has to work differently. The first task is not to initiate the soul. The first task is to bring the soul near enough to be initiated at all.

## Drawing the Mantra Near

The tradition has a phrase for this: the "nearness" of the Mantra. Mantra-sānnidhya. Before anything can happen, the living power of the sacred sound has to be made present, dense, available in the space of the rite — and the soul has to be drawn into that nearness. This does not happen by accident. The text lists, in order, the things that produce it.

There are the many ritual acts, performed with care and in sequence. There are the finest materials — flowers and the rest, nothing shoddy, nothing improvised. There is the right place, the prepared seat. There is the sacred diagram, the maṇḍala, and here the text is specific: the trident crowned with lotus, triśūla-padma, the trident standing for the three powers of will, knowledge and action, the lotus for the seat of awareness that opens upon them. There is the form held in meditation — a luminous shape, the radiant Mantra itself, given as the object on which the mind rests. And there is the human factor, which the text will not let you skip: the presence of a yogin steeped in meditation and a jñānin saturated with absorbed devotion to That.

Each of these, the text says, is a cause of nearness in its own right. And if they are all present together — well, then, what more is there to say? So the Supreme Lord declared. Stack every cause in one place and the soul is as good as already drawn.

## The Great Net

Now comes the device at the center of this chapter, the one the old rendering left out entirely, the one this chapter is really about.

After worshiping God, the teacher places before himself a small figure of the dead one, woven of kuśa grass — the kuśa-puttalaka, the grass effigy. A body made of blades of sacred grass, standing in for the body that is gone. And then he does the thing he learned from his own teacher, by a method that is not described carelessly.

Listen to how it moves. The teacher's awakened energy rises from the base, from Mūlādhāra at the root of the body. Rising, it seizes by sheer power the central shaft — the great vertical channel through which the innumerable subtle channels, the nāḍīs beyond counting, spread out like the veins of a leaf. He takes hold of that shaft and rides it upward. When the shaft reaches the ether at the level of the nose — the fine open space where the breath turns — he is able to break through. And breaking through, he casts outward a net. A net of flames, beautiful, well-wrapped in smoke, flung out from himself into the spaces beyond, and that net finds the soul he is seeking and draws it near.

This is the device called the Great Net. Mahājāla. The image is exact and worth sitting with: the teacher does not climb up to some heaven to fetch the soul, and he does not wait passively for it to arrive. He throws. From the still center of his own raised energy, at the threshold where breath meets emptiness, he flings a burning, smoke-veiled net across whatever distance separates the living from the dead, and reels the soul in. Covered by that net, the soul appears in front of him, obedient, present at last.

This Yoga of attraction — and the text is clear it serves both branches, the drawing of a dead soul and the drawing of a living absent one — is called the Net. The teaching did not come from nowhere. It was learned from revealed scripture and from the venerable Śambhunātha, the teacher in the lineage whom this tradition names with reverence.

## Why Not Do This in Ordinary Life

A fair objection arises here, and the text raises it against itself. If a teacher can reach out and draw a soul near by contemplative power, why is this attraction not used out in ordinary life? Why not pull people toward the good, toward the Lord, all the time?

The answer cuts to the heart of why this rite works at all. Out in ordinary life, when such an inclination arises, absorption into the Lord can be blocked — blocked by attachment and aversion, by rāga and dveṣa, by the ordinary pulls of liking and disliking that keep a soul snagged on the world. And ordinary life runs under the rule of Niyati, the cosmic law of restriction that ties every result to its appropriate cause and makes everything depend on effort, on practice, on the slow grind of the path. Under that law, you cannot simply draw a person to liberation; they have to walk.

But here, inside this rite, accomplishment does not come through effort against Niyati's restriction. It comes through absorption into the Supreme Lordship — and that Lordship is made of nothing but Grace. Anugraha. The Supreme Lord Himself, presiding from within the body of the Guru, bestows grace on the one who is fit to receive it. The Guru's body becomes the seat from which the Lord acts. That is why what is impossible in ordinary life becomes natural here. The text gives this its proper name: an Inconceivable Greatness. Acintya-māhātmya. A power that does not submit to being thought through, only witnessed.

## The Soul in the Grass, and Its Dissolution

So the soul, drawn by the net, enters the body of kuśa grass — or sometimes a fruit, of jāti and the like, used in place of the effigy. But notice what the text observes honestly: the soul, once it has entered the grass body, does not stir. It does not vibrate. And the reason is plain — the full collection is missing. The mind, the vital breath, all the instruments of an embodied life, manas and prāṇa and the rest, are not gathered there. A soul without its instruments is present but inert.

And yet — by the force of the teacher's meditation, it can be made to vibrate even so. The contemplative power that drew it in can also quicken it.

Then the consecration proceeds as it would for the living: the sprinkling and the rest, moving step by step up to the yojanikā, the joining, the act that unites the soul with the Highest — sealed with the full oblation, the pūrṇāhuti, the complete pouring of the offering into the fire. And by that full oblation, the grass body itself is dissolved. It melts upward into the Highest Light. The effigy that held the soul is consumed, and the soul it held goes home.

Thus the one who was extracted becomes liberated — and the text widens the lens here to take in every direction the soul might have been drawn from. Whoever was pulled out of heaven, out of hell, out from among the wandering spirits, out from among the animals — drawn from any of these and dissolved into the Light, that one is liberated. But there is a special case. If the one extracted is a human being, he does not simply dissolve; he obtains Knowledge, or Yoga, or initiation, or the power of discernment. Because a human body is an entitled body — adhikārin, qualified, equipped to carry forward what a beast or a ghost cannot. The human alone can be drawn back into a path rather than only into release.

This, the text says, is the extraction of the dead.

## The Living Who Are Far Away

Now the second branch, and it is gentler.

Sometimes the descent of Power falls on someone who is alive but absent — far away, unreachable, but breathing. For this one there is no need of the Great Net and no need of the grass effigy. There is no soul to drag back from the realms of the dead. A living person still holds his own instruments, his own mind and breath. So the teacher brings him near by meditation alone — simply draws him into presence by the aiming of contemplative attention, and performs the rite upon the one so gathered.

And here the fruit is fuller. This initiation gives both enjoyment and Liberation. Bhoga and mukti, together. Because the living one still carries the force of his own vāsanās — the deep impressions, the unspent tendencies of a life still in motion — and those cannot simply be canceled while life continues. So the rite honors both: it grants the experience the impressions still demand, and it grants release. When such an initiation is done amid many, the impress of an elevated teaching runs strong, and the rest of the rite serves to purify what that strong impression stirs up. In the absent living one, initiated this way, Knowledge and all its companions duly manifest. The distance is no obstacle.

## The Marvel at the End

The chapter closes not with a procedure but with a kind of astonishment, and it is right to let it stand as astonishment.

What a wonder it is — by a firm, unwavering absorption into the Supreme Lordship, the Guru who enjoys absolute freedom, svātantrya, the freedom that belongs to the Lord himself, can initiate an absent one merely by aiming. Not by touching. Not by anointing. Not by any contact the eye could verify. Only by aiming the awakened attention across whatever gulf — of death, of distance — divides him from the soul, and letting Grace do the rest.

That is the teaching of this chapter. Not a deathbed mercy for someone present and fading, but something stranger and larger: the reach of a free awareness across the boundary that the whole world believes to be final. The teacher casts his burning net into the dark, and the absent one is drawn home.

Here ends the sixteenth chapter of the Tantrasara, the Initiation of the Absent, called the Great Net.
