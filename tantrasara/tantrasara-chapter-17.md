# Chapter Seventeen: Lifting Away the Old Mark

This short chapter handles a practical question that arises whenever a tradition welcomes people from elsewhere. What about someone who has already been initiated into another path — who already bears the marks and vows of some other teaching — and now comes to the door of this one? Can they enter? And if so, what is to be done about the allegiance they already carry? The answer is a rite the tradition calls Lingoddhara, the lifting away of the old mark.

## The Situation

Many seekers do not come to this path fresh and unmarked. They arrive having already followed other traditions, having taken other initiations, worn other emblems, sworn other commitments. The chapter is specific about who it has in mind: those who have lived in or taken vows in doctrines ranging all the way from the Vaishnavas through the right-hand Tantra — the whole spread of schools that fill the spiritual landscape. It also has in mind a subtler case: those who, though they have already come to the highest teaching, have been serving teachers of an inferior doctrine who were never authorized to guide them. These prior bonds are real. A person formed by another teaching carries its imprint, its vows, its way of holding the divine.

What turns such a person toward this door is not argument and not ambition. It is grace. The chapter is precise about the trigger: the rite is for one who has been made inclined through a descent of Power carried out by the Supreme Lord — shaktipata, the Lord's own energy moving in the seeker, turning him. Only when that has happened is there anything here to perform. And when it has, the old allegiance must first be addressed. Not in hostility — the chapter carries no contempt for other traditions — but as a matter of clearing the ground. A field already planted with one crop must be cleared before another can be sown. The old vow must be neutralized before the new initiation can take full hold.

## The Rite Itself

The chapter is, above everything else, a set of instructions — a liturgy, performed step by step. It is worth following the steps as the text gives them, because the meaning lives in the doing.

The aspirant fasts. Then, the next day, the teacher brings the fasting one before the Lord — the Lord worshiped here not yet with the secret, particular mantra of full initiation but with a generic mantra, the general form. The teacher has by now been informed of the seeker's prior conduct: which path he walked, which vows he swore. He needs to know what he is lifting away.

Then comes the first and most vivid gesture. The teacher takes hold of the old vow — grasps it, as if it were a tangible thing — and throws it into water. The vow is given to the water to be dissolved. The seeker then bathes, is sprinkled, and is purified further by the caru, the consecrated food, and by the tooth-stick, the dantakashtha, the small ritual cleaning of the mouth that the vow was spoken with. Body, food, and the very organ of the old oath are washed.

Now the seeker is led in blindfolded — eyes covered, brought forward sightless, the way one is brought to a threshold one cannot yet see across. Blind, he performs the worship of the Supreme Lord by the generic mantra.

Then the fire. The teacher establishes the sacred fire and, by the generic mantra, transforms it into Shiva himself — the fire is no longer fire but the body of the Lord, into which offering can be made. Into this Shiva-fire he performs the purification of the old vow. Naming a hemisphere of that mantra, he offers oblations one hundred times, each carrying the same intention — I purify the expiation — each ending in the word svaha, the cry that releases an offering into the fire. A hundred times the old vow is fed to Shiva and burned. And then, to seal it, a single full oblation, the purnahuti, the complete pouring-out, ending this time in the word vaushat. The hundred offerings cleanse; the full offering completes.

There remains one more figure to deal with — not the seeker, but the unseen presence behind the vow itself. Every vow has a lord, a presiding deity, the vrata-pati, the one in whose authority the oath was binding. The teacher invokes this lord of the vows, worships him, and then makes him hear a command. By Shiva's command, become in his case a doer of nothing. The deity who held this seeker is told, on the authority of Shiva himself, to have no further effect upon him — to do nothing, to bind nothing, to claim nothing. Having satisfied that deity and released him with honor rather than insult, the teacher leaves the fire. This is the removal of the mark.

## What Is Actually Lifted

It is worth being exact about what this rite removes, because it is easy to inflate it into something vaguer than it is. The chapter is not performing soul-surgery or erasing a personality. It is neutralizing a specific thing: a vow, and the deity who enforced it. The old oath is dissolved in water, burned in the Shiva-fire, and its presiding lord is formally commanded to stand down. That is the literal mechanism, and the spiritual meaning has to be read off of that, not floated free of it.

And read off of it, the meaning is real. A vow is a binding — a self-definition, freely taken, that gives the soul a particular shape and a particular allegiance. To take an oath is to say I am one who is bound to this. So when the rite unbinds the old vow and tells its deity to do nothing further, what is being loosened is precisely a way the seeker had defined and committed himself. In the non-dual vision of this tradition, that loosening is the same move the whole path makes everywhere else: the gentle release of one more I am this and not that, so that the boundless can come nearer. The removal of the old mark is not an insult to the other path. It is the undoing of one specific knot, so that the soul is free to be tied — or rather, recognized — anew.

## Performed With Respect

The chapter's spirit is worth dwelling on, because it reveals something fine about the vision behind it. The other traditions are not condemned. Notice how the rite ends: the lord of the vows is satisfied and released, not banished or cursed. The seeker who came from elsewhere is not scolded for having walked another road. The various paths — from the devotional schools through the dualist and right-hand systems — are treated as real approaches, real stages, each suited to souls at a certain point in their journey. The vow is dissolved with care, the deity dismissed with honor.

This tradition simply understands itself as the most encompassing, the one in which the others find their completion. So when a seeker arrives from elsewhere, the rite does not erase his past in shame. It neutralizes the one binding that would keep him from going further, and it does so respectfully. The old path was a real staircase; it carried the soul up to this door. Now, to pass through, the soul sets down even that staircase, because what lies beyond requires no support at all.

## And Then, Initiation

The rite is not the destination — it only clears the way. The chapter is explicit about what follows. Once the old mark has been lifted, the seeker's consecration proceeds exactly as it would for anyone else, and then his initiation is given as desired, in the form suited to him. Nothing about his foreign origin holds him back any longer; he is now, for ritual purposes, simply a candidate like any other.

The chapter closes on a rule that gathers the whole teaching into one sentence. Even one who has remained in an inferior doctrine — even one whose mind has been strongly impelled by the Power of the Lord, and who was, until now, devoted to a false guru — is to be initiated, once he has been purified. The purification is the condition; initiation is the promise. No one whom grace has genuinely turned is turned away. The door is open to anyone, provided the old oath is first set down.

## The Larger Lesson

There is a teaching here that reaches beyond the specific case of converts. The lifting away of the old mark is a vivid, miniature image of what the entire path is ultimately doing: loosening bindings, one after another, releasing the self-definitions and allegiances we have sworn ourselves to, until none holds us by force and only the unlimited Self remains.

We are all, in a sense, under vows — bound by our histories, our oaths, our accumulated sense of who we are and which lord we answer to. The path of recognition asks us, gently and in time, to bring each of these to the water and to the fire, and to tell its presiding deity, by the Lord's own command, to do nothing further. Not because the vow was worthless, but because every binding is a binding, and the Self we are seeking is bound by nothing.

So this small ceremonial chapter, about the practical matter of receiving seekers from other traditions, carries the whole spirit of the work in miniature: come as you are, bringing whatever vows your journey has bound you with — and then let even those be dissolved, their old lords released, so that nothing stands between you and the recognition that you were always the boundless Light, having worn, for a while, one more name.

Here ends the seventeenth chapter of the Tantrasara, called Lifting Away the Old Mark.
