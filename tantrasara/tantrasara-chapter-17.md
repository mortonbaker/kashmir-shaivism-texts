# Chapter Seventeen: Lifting Away the Old Mark

This short chapter handles a practical question that arises whenever a tradition welcomes people from elsewhere. What about someone who has already been initiated into another path — who already bears the marks and vows of some other teaching — and now comes to the door of this one? Can they enter? And if so, what is to be done about the allegiance they already carry? The answer is a rite the tradition calls the lifting away of the old mark.

## The Situation

Many seekers do not come to this path fresh and unmarked. They arrive having already followed other traditions, having taken other initiations, worn other emblems, sworn other commitments — from the various other schools and sects that fill the spiritual landscape. These prior bonds are real. A person formed by another teaching carries its imprint, its vows, its way of holding the divine.

When such a person, moved now by grace, comes seeking entry to this path, their previous allegiance must first be addressed. Not in hostility — the chapter carries no contempt for other traditions — but as a matter of clearing the ground. A field already planted with one crop must be cleared before another can be sown. The old mark must be lifted before the new initiation can take full hold.

## What Is Actually Lifted

It is important to understand what is being removed, because it is easy to picture this as mere sectarian bookkeeping — crossing out one membership to write in another. It is deeper than that.

What the rite lifts away is the binding force of the prior path's hold on the soul — the particular limitation, the specific contraction, that came with identifying oneself through that earlier teaching. Every path, every system, every set of vows, gives the soul a certain shape, a certain way of being defined. And any definition, however noble, is still a kind of boundary. The lifting away of the old mark dissolves that boundary, freeing the soul from the specific limitation its previous allegiance imposed, so that it can enter the new path unencumbered.

In the non-dual vision of this tradition, this makes perfect sense. The goal is the recognition of the unlimited Self, the Light that is bound by nothing. Any prior identification — even a religious one, even a holy one — is one more thing the soul has taken itself to be, one more disguise. So the removal of the old mark is not an insult to the other path. It is the same loosening this tradition performs on every limitation: the gentle removal of one more layer of "I am this and not that," so that the boundless can shine through.

## Performed With Respect

The chapter's spirit is worth dwelling on, because it reveals something fine about Abhinavagupta's vision. The other traditions are not condemned. The seekers who come from them are not scolded for having walked other roads. The various paths — from the devotional schools to the dualist systems and beyond — are understood as genuine stages, real approaches to the divine, each suited to souls at a certain point in their journey.

This tradition simply understands itself as the most encompassing, the one in which all the others find their completion. So when a seeker arrives from elsewhere, the rite does not erase their past in shame. It honors the journey that brought them here and then lifts away the boundary that would keep them from going further. The old path was a real staircase; it carried the soul up to this door. Now, to pass through, the soul sets down even that staircase, because what lies beyond requires no support at all.

## The Larger Lesson

There is a teaching here that reaches beyond the specific case of converts. The lifting away of the old mark is a vivid reminder of what the entire path is ultimately doing: removing identifications, one after another, until none is left and only the unlimited Self remains.

We are all, in a sense, marked — defined by our histories, our allegiances, our accumulated sense of who we are and which group we belong to. The path of recognition asks us, gently and in time, to lift every one of these away. Not because they were worthless, but because each was a boundary, and the Self we are seeking has no boundary. The soul that would be free must eventually set down even its most cherished definitions of itself.

So this small ceremonial chapter, about the practical matter of receiving seekers from other traditions, carries the whole spirit of the work in miniature: come as you are, bringing whatever marks your journey has given you — and then let even those be lifted away, so that nothing stands between you and the recognition that you were always the boundless Light, wearing one more name.

Here ends the seventeenth chapter of the Tantrasara, called Lifting Away the Old Mark.
