# Chapter Fifteen: The Initiation of Departure

There is one moment the teacher prepares for that no rehearsal can soften, and this chapter is about that moment. A person is dying. The body is loosening its grip, the breath is growing uncertain, and the soul stands at the threshold of leaving. And then, in that very person, grace arrives. The tradition calls it a descent of Power — śaktipāta, the falling of the divine energy into a soul — and it may come all on its own, rising up in the dying one unbidden, or it may be carried in through the prayer and the love of a relative standing at the bedside. Either way, when grace descends upon someone who is about to die, there is no time to lead them slowly along the years-long path of a living disciple. There is time only for one thing, and it must be done at once. The teacher performs what is called the initiation of departure — the rite that turns a death into an ascent.

So understand the setting clearly before we go any further. This is not the initiation of someone who will wake the next morning and go on living. This is the rite for the one who is leaving the body, the one for whom the journey out has already begun. The whole purpose of this chapter is to teach how the teacher takes a death that would otherwise be an ordinary fall back into bondage and turns it instead into a gateway — a departure that carries the soul home.

## Laying the Whole Course Upon the Dying One

The teacher begins as initiation always begins, by placing the entire course of the worlds upon the disciple. The tradition speaks of the whole of manifested reality as a path — an unfolding of levels and worlds and stages that the soul has descended through to become a bound and limited creature. In initiation, the teacher mentally installs this entire course, this whole spread of existence, upon the disciple, and then purifies it stage by stage, in order, working through the levels in succession. Each stage is touched, acknowledged, and released, so that nothing of the soul's involvement in the worlds is left clinging to it. By the end of this purification the disciple has been loosened, level by level, from the long ladder of existence that had bound it.

This is the same patient, ordered work the teacher does in any deep initiation. But here it is done against the clock of a departing breath, and it is done so that what follows can carry the soul cleanly out.

## The Installation of Kālarātrī, Who Cuts the Vital Joints

Now comes the central act of this whole rite, and it is fierce. The teacher installs within the dying disciple a particular form of the Goddess. Her name is Kālarātrī — the Dark Night, the Night of Time, the power who stands at the end of things. And her specific work, the work she is summoned for, is to cut the marmas.

The marmas are the vital joints — the secret junctures of the living body, the knots and seams where life is fastened in. In the ordinary understanding they are the tender, mortal points; in the language of this rite they are the very fastenings by which the soul is tied into the body and into bondage. Kālarātrī is the one who cuts them. The teacher installs her, and then, through her, works gradually at these vital joints, piercing the bonds that are knotted there. Not violently in the gross sense, but precisely and in order — the same successive, stage-by-stage discipline as before, now applied to the actual fastenings of embodied life. One by one the bonds lodged in the marmas are pierced, and one by one the soul is released from the points that held it.

There is something worth pausing on here. The figure who performs this releasing is not a gentle one. She is the Dark Night, the power of dissolution, the face the divine wears when it is taking a world apart. And that is exactly right, because what is being undone is the binding of a soul into a dying body. The same power that the timid mind fears as death is, in the teacher's hands and under the teacher's intention, the very instrument of liberation. The cutting is not a wound. It is an unfastening.

## Raising Consciousness to the Crown

As the bonds in the vital joints are pierced and released, the teacher draws the disciple's consciousness upward. There is a particular destination: the Brahmarandhra, the aperture at the crown of the head, the highest opening, the door at the very top of the subtle structure of the person. The teacher causes the consciousness of the dying disciple to come to rest there, gathered at that crown aperture, no longer dispersed through the body it is leaving.

Picture what has happened. The soul that was tied into the worlds has been loosened from them. The soul that was fastened into the body at its vital joints has had those fastenings cut by the Dark Night. And now that freed consciousness is lifted and held at the highest door, poised at the threshold, no longer pouring itself down into a dying frame but gathered up and waiting at the place of exit. Everything below has been released; everything is collected at the crown.

## The Full Oblation and Union With Supreme Śiva

Then the teacher offers the full oblation. In the fire-rite that runs through this whole tradition, the full oblation — the pūrṇāhuti — is the complete and final offering, the one in which everything is given at once into the fire, the consummation of the whole ceremony. Here it is offered for what is called the yojanikā, the joining, the conjunction. This is the oblation whose entire purpose is to unite — to join the freed and gathered soul to its source.

And at the end of that full oblation, the thing the whole rite was for comes to pass. The departed soul becomes one with the Supreme Śiva. Not joined to a heaven, not promoted to a higher station within the worlds, but united with the supreme reality itself — the ground of all consciousness, the highest. The soul that a few moments before was dying in a body now stands fully merged with the divine. The departure has become an arrival. This is the heart of the chapter, and everything before it — the purification, the cutting of the marmas, the raising to the crown — was the preparation that made this union possible.

## The Second Oblation, for the One Who Still Wants to Taste the Worlds

But not every soul, even at the threshold, wants only the supreme dissolution. Some still carry a desire for enjoyment — for experience, for the savor of the worlds, for a place where pleasures can still be had. The tradition does not shame this and does not refuse it. For such a one, the teacher offers a second full oblation, and this second offering joins the soul not to the supreme alone but to the place of enjoyment it longs for. The soul is conducted to the world its desire reaches toward.

And at that moment something definite happens: the dissolution of the individual soul. The separate, limited self — the little contracted "I" that wanted the enjoyment — is dissolved even as the desire is fulfilled. The longing is honored and the longing's owner is undone in the same act. And the tradition is plain about what follows: here there is nothing else to do. The rite is complete. The soul has been delivered where it was bound to go, the individual knot has been released, and the teacher's work is finished.

## Or: Speaking the Knowledge Into the Ear

There is another way to accomplish this, and it is startling in its simplicity. Instead of the full ceremonial mechanism, the teacher may simply recite the Knowledge of the Absolute directly into the dying one's ear. Not a long teaching, not an explanation to be studied and pondered — the supreme Knowledge itself, spoken into the ear at the threshold.

And why should mere words spoken into an ear do the work of the whole elaborate rite? Because this Knowledge is not really information. It is, in its very nature, Consciousness itself. To speak it is to put Consciousness directly into contact with the soul, and Consciousness touching consciousness does not need to be processed or believed or understood — it acts immediately. The moment it lands, it awakens Śakti. It rouses the I-consciousness, the living power of self-awareness at the heart of the person, the divine energy that had been sleeping under the contraction of a limited life. The Knowledge, being Consciousness, wakes the Consciousness that was always there. And that awakening does what the oblations do — it carries the soul home. A whisper at the right threshold can do the work of a fire.

## The Seedless State and the Body Left Light

There is a deeper layer beneath all of this, and the chapter names it. What truly binds a soul, even past the cutting of the body's joints, is the seed — the karma-creating power, the latent capacity to sow further lives and further bondage, together with the residual impressions, the saṃskāras, the deep grooves of habit and memory pressed into the person by all that they have lived. As long as that seed remains, the soul can sprout again into bound existence.

So the teacher, in this rite, casts in — as one plants a seed of the opposite kind — that which destroys the karma-creating power itself. And what comes about is called the seedless state, the nirbīja condition. In this state, two things are burned at once: the latent power hidden within the seed, the potency that would have grown into new bondage, and the residual impressions lying within the disciple, the saṃskāras that carry the shape of the old life. Both are destroyed. The soul is left not merely loosened but seedless — with nothing left in it that could grow into another round of bondage. This is why the union is final. There is no seed left to bring the soul back.

And the tradition tells us there is an outward token of this, something almost shockingly physical. As the seed-state is destroyed, the disciple, in the words of the text, becomes light and shakes violently, and comes to reside in the world of Air. The dense weight of bound existence — the karmic gravity, if you like, that pressed the soul down into earth and body — is consumed, and what remains gravitates toward the airy, the subtle, the unburdened. And here is the strange sign: on account of this, the disciple is seen to be light when weighed. The very body, set on a scale, weighs less than it should. The destruction of the seed shows itself in the flesh as literal lightness. The invisible burning of karmic potency leaves a visible, measurable trace — the dead weight is simply not there anymore.

## The Closing Word

The chapter ends by gathering all of this into a single recognition. When, in the ritual of cutting the marmas, the right Mantra brings about that light condition — the lightness that signals the seed-state has been destroyed — then it is the Supreme Lord himself who is acting. The teacher installs Kālarātrī, the teacher cuts the vital joints, the teacher offers the oblation or whispers the Knowledge into the ear; but prompted by a suitable path, called forth through the right channel, it is the Supreme Lord who swiftly accomplishes all of it, this result and that. The teacher provides the path. The divine does the work. And so the dying are not abandoned at the threshold but carried across it — death itself turned, by the right hands and the right power, into the last and cleanest initiation.

Here ends the fifteenth chapter of the Tantrasara, the Initiation of Departure.
