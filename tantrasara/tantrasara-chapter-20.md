# Chapter Twenty: The Conduct of the Remaining Life

The last chapters carried you through initiation — the rite that purifies, and the rite that frees with a seed still planted for those not yet ready for full Knowledge. But initiation is a door, not a destination. Once a soul has passed through it, a plain question waits on the far side: how should the rest of this life be lived? Not in the lofty sense of inner attitude, but in the concrete sense of what one actually *does*, day after day, festival after festival, year after year, until the body falls away. That is the subject of this chapter. The tradition calls it śeṣa-vartana — the conduct of what remains. And once initiation is complete, it is the Guru's task to teach it.

The teaching begins with a map. The conduct of the remaining life divides into three kinds. There is Nitya — the constant, the obligatory, the practice that simply does not stop. There is Naimittika — the occasional, the rite called forth by a particular occasion. And there is Kāmya — the desire-prompted rite, the one performed to obtain some specific thing one wants. The third belongs to the sādhaka, the seeker still working toward powers and results, and this chapter sets it aside. Our concern is with the first two: what must be done always, and what must be done when the occasion arrives.

## The Constant Practice and Its Occasions

Nitya is the practice that runs underneath everything. Its aim is not modest. The whole point of the constant rite is the steady, unbroken arrival at identity with That — at becoming, in recognition, what one already is. What feeds this aim? Worship at the sandhyās, the junctures of the day, the seams where one period of light hands off to the next. These observances are done daily, without fail, because the daily rhythm of the seams is itself the rhythm by which recognition is kept warm.

And then there are the great fixed days — the festivals, the Pavitraka, and the rest. Some teachers count these among the Nitya rites too, and their reasoning is worth hearing: though a festival comes only once in its season, it is *fixed* in that season, locked to its own time as surely as the sunrise is locked to morning. A thing can be obligatory and rare at once. So the line between constant and occasional is softer than it first looks.

The Naimittika rites, by contrast, are not regular at all. They are summoned by events. The text names them, and the list is a portrait of a life lived inside a living tradition. The Naimittika occasions are these: the attainment of Knowledge itself; the acquisition of the scriptures; the day the Guru and his circle come to visit one's house; the Guru's birthday; the day of his purification and his departure from this life; the common festivals shared by all; the explanation of the scriptures — at its beginning, its middle, and its end; the manifestation of the deity; the union of the Siddhas and the Yoginīs; and the junctures of the day. Each of these is a moment when the membrane between the ordinary and the sacred goes thin, and the rite marks it.

But here the chapter says something that reaches under all the machinery of observance and quietly reorders it. In every one of these rites, what matters is not the *sequence* in which the worships are performed. The order of operations is not the load-bearing thing. What matters is one thing only: identification with the deity who is Śiva. Get the inner identification right, and the choreography is secondary. Get the choreography flawless and miss the identification, and you have performed nothing.

The teachers press this even further, and it is among the most beautiful sentences in the chapter. Take a person who has become absorbed in identity with the highest Mantra — someone in whom the old stain of limited-being, the vāsanās, the deep grooves of contraction, have ceased; someone whose entire net of bonds has dissolved through devotion. Whatever resides in the heart of *that* person — that very thing is the supreme to be chosen. The rite does not authorize the heart; the liberated heart authorizes the rite. This, the chapter says, is the opinion of the Gurus.

## Worship Upon the Liṅga

From the constant rites the chapter turns to a concrete practice: worship upon the liṅga, the support that holds the divine presence. And it opens with a restriction. One should not *install* the liṅga through the secret Mantras — that is, one does not perform the full consecrating establishment with the esoteric formulas. Instead one worships upon a support that has already been installed, calling the presence into it by āvāhana, the invitation, and releasing it again by visarjana, the dismissal. The deity is invited to be present, honored, and then sent on its way. The support is borrowed, not seized.

And what may serve as that support? Here the chapter opens wide, and the breadth of it tells you something about how this tradition sees the sacred. One may worship, as a liṅga: the body of the Guru. One's own body. The body of the Śakti, the consort. A book of secret scripture. The drinking-vessel of the heroes. A rosary of Rudrākṣa seeds. A white stone from the Narmadā river, worshiped as the liṅga of Śiva. A pearl. Gold. Lovely materials — flowers, fragrances. Or a mirror. The list runs from a living human body to a river-stone to one's own reflection. Anything, rightly seen, can hold the Lord.

But the supports are not all equal, and the chapter is precise about why. Success with the Mantras comes by the *force of the support* — the power of the rite borrows from the vessel it works through. So each prior support in that list is superior to the next, and the Mantras themselves take on the quality of whatever support they inhabit. The Guru's body is a higher vessel than a river-stone, and the Mantra worked upon it carries more. Yet over all of this stands a single rule that levels the whole hierarchy from above: everywhere, the supreme purificatory rite — the highest thing any of this is reaching toward — is nothing but the conception of unity with the Supreme Lord. Every stone, every mirror, every body is only a doorway to that one recognition.

## The Sixfold Festival

Now the chapter turns to the festivals, and the structure becomes architectural. A festival, it says, is sixfold. There is the general; the general-general; the general-special; the special-general; the special; and the special-special. It is a lattice built by crossing two qualities against each other — the ordinary and the heightened — to generate every grade of sacred day between the most common and the most rare.

The general festival is the first fifth — every day. The baseline. Sacredness available at all times. The general-general festival falls on particular lunar days: the fourth, the eighth, the ninth, the fourteenth, and the fifteenth of each of the two fortnights, the waxing and the waning. These are the recurring nodes in the month, the days that come around reliably. The general-special arrives when those days become charged by conjunction — when they fall together with particular stars and planets, the asterisms and the celestial bodies aligning with the lunar day. Here the source opens into an elaborate calendrical table of months and asterisms and planets, the fine machinery of when exactly the heightened days occur. The dense astronomical reckoning matters in practice but need not be recited bead by bead to grasp the doctrine.

What the doctrine says is this. When stars and planets conjoin, the *hour* of the day is not the decisive thing. It is the tithi — the lunar day itself — that marks the difference, that determines which kind of festival you stand within. And yet, on a festival day, the governing concern is conformity with the time of the Anuyāga. The Anuyāga is the central sacrifice of the festival, and the festival's rites are arranged around it because the festival's offerings are predominant in an Anuyāga. Hold this name carefully, because the chapter now reveals that it has three: Anuyāga, Mūrti-yāga, and Cakra-yāga are one and the same rite under three words. The circle-worship, the assembly sacrifice — that is the heart of the festival, and that is where the chapter goes next.

## The Cakra-yāga: The Circle Worship

The Cakra-yāga is the great assembly rite, and it begins with the gathering of those to be worshiped — for in this rite, living persons are themselves the deities honored. They are worshiped both individually and as a single group. The chapter names them: the Guru; the Guru's circle together with their progeny; a knower of the Truth; a virgin girl; a woman of the lowest tribe; a courtesan; a menstruating woman; or a female knower of the Truth. The selection is deliberate and pointed. It reaches across every line of purity and rank that ordinary society polices — and worships precisely those whom that society would exclude. The rite is built to dissolve the conventional grid by honoring what the grid rejects.

The arrangement is concentric. The Guru sits in the center. Around him the others are placed — from the Guru outward to the samayis, the bound disciples — set in the order of Hero and Śakti, each adept paired with his consort-power, forming a circle, or arranged in rows. One worships them in succession, with fragrant incense, with flowers, with all the materials of honor.

Then comes the inner movement of the rite, and it is dense with meaning. One meditates upon the vessel — the ritual cup — as Sadāśiva himself, and fills it with liquor conceived as the Nectar of Śakti, the very fluid of the goddess's power. Within that vessel one worships the enjoying Śakti as Śiva, and through Her one satisfies the whole gathered group of deities. Then one meditates upon the union of the triad — the limited individual, the Śakti, and Śiva, the three brought into one — and by that union one satisfies the supremacy of both Liberation and enjoyment at once, outside and within. The coverings are laid aside in succession, and then taken up again in reverse order; this full circular movement, out and back, is what nourishes the cakra, the circle of the rite. The whole sacrifice turns like a wheel, and the turning is what feeds it.

The overflow is not wasted. By the falling drops, the vetālas and the guhyakas — the spirit-beings on the edges — are satisfied. By the stream itself, Bhairava is satisfied. Even the spillage feeds the divine.

And the rite is sealed. No one may be allowed to enter while it proceeds. The circle is closed. Should someone enter through negligence, breaking the seal, the remedy is not to abandon the rite but to redouble it: one performs a double Cakra-yāga, twice over, to restore what the intrusion disturbed. Then, as desired, one scatters pungent food and the rest. Or — in a secret room — one addresses everyone present by a deity-name, dropping the conventional names entirely, so that in that room there are no longer ordinary people but only gods speaking to gods. This is the sacrifice of the intermixture of heroes. At the end, one satisfies all with dakṣiṇā — the parting offering — with betel, with clothes, and the rest. This, the chapter says, is the most important Mūrti-yāga of all.

And then a promise, wide enough to reach the ones who cannot perform the full rite. Even a person who has never seen the maṇḍala — never been admitted to the inner diagram — attains the fruit called putraka within a year, if on the festival days he performs the Mūrti-yāga worship. This rite, the chapter says plainly, is for the old, for those who still seek enjoyment, and for women — and it must be taught by the Guru when a descent of Power, a śaktipāta, occurs in the one to be taught. The grace comes first; the teaching follows the grace.

## The Pavitraka: The Annual Thread

The chapter closes with the Pavitraka, the rite of the consecrated thread, and it carries great weight. The authorities are named — the Ratnamālā, the Triśiromata, the Siddhamata — and what they describe is the act of fulfilling the Supreme Lord's command according to the prescribed rules. The Tantrāloka states the stakes without softening them: without the Pavitraka, all is fruitless. The whole year's worship, every rite performed, hangs upon this annual sealing; leave it undone, and the rest does not hold.

The season is fixed. It is to be performed from the bright fortnight of Āṣāḍha — the waxing moon of the monsoon month — through to the full-moon day of Kula, though the different schools give variant dates within that window. According to one's wealth, after worshiping God and satisfying Him by oblation, one offers the pavitraka itself — a cord tied with knots. The material runs the full range of one's means: from gold and pearl at the richest, down through cloth, fiber, cotton, to humble Kuśa grass at the simplest. The rite scales to the means of whoever performs it; poverty does not bar it.

And the knots carry the cosmos. The cord is not arbitrary string. One reaching to the knees is tied with knots equal to the number of categories — and with knots equal to the number of the Padas, the Kalās, the Bhuvanas, the Varṇas, and the Mantras, the whole architecture of the manifest worlds counted out in tied knots along its length. Another cord reaches the navel, another the throat, another the head, each graded by length. Four such pavitrakas are offered: to God and to the Guru one offers cords imagined as full of all the adhvas — all the courses of manifestation, the whole emanated universe held in the thread — and one more is offered to the rest. The cosmos is tied into the cord, and the cord is given back to its source.

And afterward, a great festival is held. The year's most binding rite ends not in austerity but in celebration — the thread tied, the command fulfilled, the worship made fruitful, and the whole circle gathered to rejoice. So the conduct of the remaining life is not a waiting, but a calendar: the daily seams, the summoned occasions, the turning wheel of the assembly, and the annual thread that ties it all back into the One from whom it came.

Here ends the twentieth chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Conduct of the Remaining Life.
