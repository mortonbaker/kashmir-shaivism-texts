# Chapter Twenty: Living What Remains

The daily practice of the last chapter raises a question it does not fully answer. What is the texture of a whole life lived after awakening? Once a soul has been initiated and freed, and has taken up the daily worship, how does it actually *live* — among other people, in an ordinary world, through the years that remain to it? This chapter is about the conduct of the remaining life: how one who has been set free moves through the world while waiting out the natural span of the body.

## The Liberated Who Still Live

Begin with the situation the chapter addresses, because it is one of the most fascinating in the whole tradition. A soul can be genuinely liberated — the knot cut, the recognition firm — and yet still be alive, still walking around in a body, still having to eat and sleep and deal with other people. The freedom has been won inwardly, but the body has not yet fallen. There is a stretch of life still to be lived on the far side of awakening.

What is that life like? It is not a suspension, a holding of the breath until death finally releases the soul. It is a life — to be lived fully, but lived now from an entirely transformed standpoint. The liberated person is no longer driven by the old engine of wanting and fearing, no longer convinced they are a small self struggling against a hostile world. They know what they are. And so the remaining life becomes the free expression of that knowing, played out in ordinary days.

## Freedom From, Not Freedom To Misbehave

It would be easy to misread this freedom, and the chapter guards against the misreading. To be liberated from the rules that bind the ordinary soul is not a license to do harm or to indulge every impulse. The freedom of the awakened is not the freedom of a child left without supervision. It is something deeper and, in a sense, more disciplined.

The liberated person acts rightly not because a rule compels them from outside, but because their action now flows from the recognition of the one Self in all. When you know that every being is the same Light you are, cruelty becomes impossible — not forbidden, but simply unthinkable, the way you would not knowingly harm your own hand. The conduct of the liberated is governed from within, by the recognition itself, which is a far surer guide than any external rule. They have not been released into chaos. They have been released into a goodness that needs no enforcement because it springs from seeing truly.

## Holding the Recognition Through Ordinary Life

The real challenge of the remaining life is steadiness. The world is relentlessly persuasive in its illusion. Hunger argues that you are a body. Insult argues that you are a separate self with pride to defend. Loss argues that you are small and time is devouring you. The liberated soul must move through all of this while holding to the recognition that none of it touches what they truly are.

This is the work of the remaining life, and the daily practice of the last chapter is precisely what sustains it. Each return to worship re-anchors the recognition; each ordinary day becomes an occasion to see the Light again where the world insists on showing only separate things. Over time, the seeing stabilizes. What once required deliberate effort — remembering the truth against the world's constant argument — becomes effortless, the natural way the liberated person perceives. The world stops being able to fool them. They eat, and it is the Light tasting itself; they meet another person, and it is the Light recognizing itself; they suffer some loss, and the loss plays out on the surface while the depths remain untouched and serene.

## Equanimity

From this comes the great mark of the liberated life: an unshakable evenness. The one who knows they are the boundless Light is no longer at the mercy of fortune's swings. Pleasure and pain still come — the body still feels them — but they no longer reach the center. They are weather passing over a mountain that does not move. The applause of the world and its contempt, gain and loss, comfort and hardship: all of these are received with the same deep steadiness, because the one receiving them knows they are not finally affected by any of it.

This equanimity is not coldness or indifference. The liberated person can still love, still act, still engage fully with life — indeed they engage more freely than anyone, because they have nothing to protect and nothing to fear losing. Their evenness is the calm of someone who has woken inside the dream and can now play their part in it wholeheartedly, with delight, precisely because they know it is a play and they are its one author wearing every role.

## The Life That Teaches

There is one more dimension to the remaining life, and it is the most beautiful. The liberated person, simply by living from recognition, becomes a light for others. They need not preach. The mere presence of someone genuinely free — someone unshaken, unafraid, evenly kind, at home in the world without grasping at it — is itself a teaching, an invitation, a living demonstration that such freedom is possible.

So the remaining life is not merely the awakened soul waiting out its days. It is grace continuing to work *through* that soul, into the world, touching others. The one who has been set free becomes, in their ordinary living, a quiet source of the same grace that freed them — until the body finally falls and the soul returns fully to the Light, leaving behind the memory of a life that showed everyone who saw it what a human being looks like when they have remembered what they are.

Here ends the twentieth chapter of the Tantrasara, called Living What Remains.
