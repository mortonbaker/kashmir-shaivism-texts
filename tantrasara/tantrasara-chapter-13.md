# Chapter Thirteen: Entering the Door

The last chapter prepared us with the true bath — the inner cleansing that is really a recognition. Now the book reaches the threshold it has been approaching since the beginning: initiation. The Sanskrit word is *dīkṣā*, and it is the central act of the whole tradition, the moment a teacher takes a soul and joins it to the divine. But initiation is not a single event. It comes in stages, and this chapter describes the first one — the entry initiation, the *samayī-dīkṣā*, the ceremony by which a newcomer becomes one who is bound by the sacred pledges, brought across the threshold and made a member of the path.

## What Initiation Really Means

Before any candle is lit or any offering made, understand what the word itself carries. In this tradition, initiation has a double meaning folded into it. It is said to *give* something and to *remove* something at the same time. It gives knowledge — the living recognition of one's identity with the Lord — and it removes bondage, the accumulated weight of ignorance and limitation. To be initiated is to receive the one thing and be relieved of the other in a single act.

So this is not joining a club or taking on a label. It is the beginning of a real surgery on the soul, performed by one who has walked the path, upon one who is ready to. The descent of grace from the last chapters has already fallen; initiation is the human answer to that descent, the visible ceremony that meets the invisible event.

## Where Is the Sacred Site, Really?

The teacher goes, with a pleased and settled heart, to the place of sacrifice. And here the chapter says something you must not miss, because it overturns everything you might assume about holy ground. The real sacrificial site is not a spot on a map. It is the heart endowed with calmness — the mind grown quiet enough to be absorbed into the Supreme Lord. That, and only that, makes a place fit for the rite. The site has no other defining feature. Even when the scriptures seem to say a place causes something, what they mean is that the practitioner has become identified with the object of meditation, and even that happens only through inner calm. There is, the text says flatly, no other site.

Then why do the scriptures name particular places — sacred seats, mountain peaks, certain consecrated grounds? Because the powers of the Lord, his *śakti-s*, full of him and arranged by his own ordering, take up residence in those places and put on bodies there. It is like the difference between the lands of people who live by *dharma* and the lands of those who don't: the place takes on the character of what fills it. And a mountain peak helps for a plainer reason too — solitude. The distractions fall away, and concentration gathers. So the holy place is real, but it is downstream of the quiet mind, never a substitute for it.

## Installing the Divine Upon the Body

Outside, in front of the sacrificial house, the teacher performs the general placement of sacred sound — *nyāsa* — the contemplative act of installing the divine, syllable by syllable, first upon the hands and then upon the whole body. He does this with two great mantra-bodies of the alphabet. The first is **Mātṛkā**, the natural order of the letters — this stands for the Possessor of Power, the Lord himself. The second is **Mālinī**, the alphabet in its garland-shuffled order, where vowels and consonants are intertwined — and this stands for Power, the Goddess. And the direction of the installation carries meaning: if the candidate longs for liberation, the sound is laid from the tips of the feet up to the head, the ascending motion; if he seeks worldly enjoyment instead, the whole thing runs in reverse, from the head downward.

The chapter pauses here to praise Mālinī, and the praise is worth slowing for. She is the Goddess in her primary form, Power itself, and because in her the vowels and consonants are joined together, she yields whatever the heart desires. She wears the garlands of the powers of Rudra. She bears the flowers that ripen into fruit. She is the female bee of sound — the honeybee of pure vibration — whose humming melts away the cold numbness of *saṃsāra*, this round of birth and death. In her hands she holds both the supernatural powers and liberation itself. So great is this installation that a mantra performed by it becomes full and perfect even when the ordinary rules were not followed. Even a mantra aimed elsewhere — the mantra of Garuḍa, of Viṣṇu, of any other power — once purified by this nyāsa becomes a giver of liberation. Right after the body is sealed this way, the same installation is laid upon the ritual vessel.

## Why the Whole Apparatus Becomes the Lord

Now the chapter steps back to tell you what all this ritual machinery is actually *for*, and the answer is a piece of grammar turned into theology. In any action there are factors — the doer, the instrument, the object, the recipient, the place — what the grammarians call the *kārakas*. The whole rite of worship is staged as a demonstration that every one of these factors is, in truth, nothing but the Supreme Lord.

Watch how it maps. The worshiper is supported by the purifying of the site. The materials and the senses are consecrated by nyāsa. The vessel is made the Lord by its purification. The worshiper himself becomes the Lord by the installation laid upon his body. And the deity to be worshiped is established by the installation upon the altar. So by the ritual method, one by one, every grammatical factor of the act is transformed into the Supreme Lord. And here is the payoff: a person who comes to see all his activity from this standpoint — every doer and instrument and object revealed as the Lord — becomes the Supreme Lord himself, even without the highest reaches of Knowledge and Yoga. The ritual is a back door to the same summit.

## Crossing the Threshold, and the Fire That Faces North

With the vessel sealed and worshiped with flowers and incense, the teacher sprinkles the essence of the sacrifice, scattering those consecrated drops over the offerings. He worships the outer retinue arrayed in the circle of light, then the cluster of deities who stand guard at the door. Then comes a small, sharp act against obstruction: he takes a flower, whispers over it three times the weapon-mantra **Phaṭ** — the sound that strikes and clears — and flings it out, holding in his mind that the obstacles are now gone. Then he enters, and he sees the sacrificial house everywhere lit up, every surface kindled by the rays of the Supreme Lord pouring through his own gaze.

And then the orientation, which the chapter treats as load-bearing: one who seeks liberation should remain turned toward the **north**. Why north? So that the bonds of the soul may be instantly scorched in the fire of the lord **Aghora** — one of the Lord's faces, the fierce one whose burning consumes limitation. The direction is not arbitrary. To understand why, the chapter opens into its longest doctrinal passage.

## The Doctrine of the Directions

Here is a teaching most ritual manuals never bother to give, and it is the philosophical heart of this chapter. What *are* the directions — north, south, east, west, up, down? Where do they come from?

They come from nowhere but the absolute freedom of the Supreme Lord, who makes the very principle of direction appear by manifesting forms. Strip away the forms and there is no direction at all; direction is a feature of a manifested world, not a thing in itself. So begin at the center. The center is the Light of Consciousness — because every other division appears out of it, the way every ray needs a source. "Up" is what that Light claims as its own. "East" is what stands in front of the Light, where manifestation breaks out first. "South" is where the stream of that Light rises and ascends. And "north" is simply what is yet to be illumined — the direction the Light turns toward to shine upon.

Now place the faces. Right in the middle is the Lord himself. Above him is the face called **Īśāna**. Below him is the **Pātāla** face, the downward one. And in the four directions stand the four remaining faces of the Lord: **Tatpuruṣa, Aghora, Sadyojāta, and Vāma**. These are the five mantra-faces of Sadāśiva spread out across space, with the Lord at their center. And so the whole compass is nothing but the greatness of Consciousness displaying itself through forms.

From this comes a line you should hold onto, because it guards against a deep confusion: **a direction is not another *tattva*.** Directions are not additional building-blocks of reality stacked alongside earth and water and mind and the rest. They are not levels of being. They are only the appearance of orientation that Consciousness throws off when it manifests form. To mistake a direction for a fundamental principle would be to mistake the shadow of the display for the display itself.

The chapter gives a second image to seat the same point. Just as the Lord lays out the directions, so does the sun — and the sun here is named the Power of Knowledge of the Supreme Lord. For the sun, too, "east" is wherever its manifestation first breaks, and that depends on where *you* stand. East is not fixed in the sky; it is relative to the one who sees. And so, as the teachers of Abhinavagupta handed down, the directions are truly understood only by a single unified contemplation of a triad held together at once: **one's own Self, the sun, and the Supreme Lord.** Find the place where those three are seen as one seeing, and the whole grid of space resolves into Consciousness orienting itself.

## Burning the Body-Sense

So established, seated and turned toward the north, the teacher does the next inward thing: he burns away the state of having a body. He sets fire to the pride that says *I am this* — the pride attached to the gross physical body, to the subtle body behind it, even the pride that clings to the bodies of others as though they were finally real. He lets all of it go, because the Self has no body. The Self was never the thing that wears flesh.

Then, settled into a firm and waveless state — a stillness with no ripple in it — he performs the installation of the group of deities to be worshiped. Among these the *śaktis*, the Powers, are worshiped first and foremost, because they are the very seats of That, the thrones on which Reality sits. He worships the ninefold Self of the Lord: **Prakṛti, Puruṣa, Niyati, Kāla, Māyā, Sadvidyā, Īśvara, Sadāśiva, and Śiva** — nine levels of the one Self ascending from nature up to Śiva. And then there are the states of awareness: five of them — waking and the rest — and a sixth beyond them all, called **Anuttara**, "the Unsurpassed," the innate State that needs nothing above it. The five plus the innate sixth make the sixfold installation.

## Unity With Bhairava

Why this elaborate placement of principle upon principle? Because there are great Causes — beings whose forms are **Brahmā, Viṣṇu, Rudra, Īśa, Sadāśiva, and Śakti** — and these six preside, one by one, over the whole array of the thirty-six *tattvas*, the full ladder of manifest reality. When the installation is performed in unity with venerable **Bhairava**, who stands beyond all the ordinary principles, then the very State of Bhairava arises — and it arises not by addition but by *Fullness*, because nothing was ever missing.

The text anticipates an objection, and answers it. Someone asks: once a waveless state has been reached, what is the point of all this manifesting of principles, all this installation? And the answer is that the waveless form of Bhairava is *precisely* the one that holds millions of varieties of manifestation and dissolution alive within itself. The stillness is not empty. It is the calm that contains all motion. To rest in Bhairava is not to abolish the worlds but to hold them all at once, unruffled.

## The Trident and the Lotus

Having contemplated that the vital breath, the body, and the intellect have all become the Supreme Lord by mutual merging, the teacher worships, outwardly and inwardly, with flowers and incense and offerings of water and food as his means allow. And then comes one of the chapter's most vivid inner acts: the installation of the trident and the lotus upon his own body, breath, and intellect.

Picture it built upward as a great staff rising through the levels of reality. The shaft of the trident rises and ends at the category **Kāla**. Along it is a knot, and that knot is **Māyā**. Above the knot opens the lotus of Pure Knowledge, a fourfold blossom, and seated upon that lotus is venerable **Sadāśiva** — described here as a great spirit, because in him the body of objects has fallen silent, he is predominantly dissolved, almost wholly Consciousness, and awake to **Nāda**, the primal sound. From his navel, out through three openings in his head, rise the three prongs of the trident: **Śakti, Vyāpinī, and Samanā** — three subtler and subtler phases of vibration dwelling in Nāda, reaching up to the measure of twelve. And above even these, the three pure lotuses of **Unmanā**, the "mind-transcending," the highest reach where thought itself is left behind.

Then, imagining the chosen deity seated upon this whole universal spread, the teacher performs what the chapter now redefines for us, stripping each ritual word down to its inner meaning. *Worship* is the act of offering every positive thing that exists to That whose nature is one homogeneous whole — handing the entire furniture of reality back to its single ground. *Meditation* is generating identification with That — becoming what you contemplate. *Japa*, usually thought of as muttered repetition, is here something deeper: an investigation into Nāda, a listening into the real being of this inner resonance. And *homa*, the fire-offering, is making the whole universe one with the Self — by force, through the great fire that this very reflection awakens. Having done all this, he meditates on the surrounding retinue as sparks flying off the one mass of fire, and worships them too.

## The Inner Sacrifice and the Rising Trident

The chapter seals this section with two terse verses about the Sky-Goer. Remembering this central trident as it rises from the root of the body all the way to the *dvādaśānta* — the point twelve finger-widths above the crown — the one who has let go of all succession, all step-by-step sequence, attains the state of **Khecara**, the Sky-Goer who moves freely in the open space of Consciousness. This Khecarī, the Sky-Going Power, consumes the Nectar of *Kha* — the nectar of the Void — by abiding and moving in that Void, and fills up the whole inner channel from the *mūlādhāra* at the base to the top of the ether twelve finger-widths above.

And so, the text concludes, full accomplishment comes through the inner sacrifice alone. Then why perform the outer rite at all? For the one already absorbed in That, the external sacrifice is done only to wear away the last limitations. For the one not yet absorbed, the external sacrifice is the main vehicle — absorption comes to him through its practice, because even an incomplete reflection on the inner sacrifice is itself a giver of purity, working on him before he is fully established. The outer rite is a ladder; some climb it, and some, already on the roof, kick it for the sport of tidiness.

## The Adhivāsana — Settling the Ground the Night Before

Now the chapter turns to the concrete preparation for the initiation itself: the **adhivāsana**, the consecration performed the evening before, the settling of the space that ripens overnight. And here the text gets specific and practical, naming the steps in order. There is the acquisition and enclosing of the place, the worship of **Gaṇeśa**, the worship of a small pot and a big pot, the worship of the altar, and a *havana* — a fire oblation. Let us walk through it as the teacher does.

In the sacrificial house, now filled with all the instruments of the rite, he holds the goddess in mind — Mālinī or Mātṛkā, the garland-order or the natural order of the alphabet — and casts flowers from his hands, seeing everything his eye falls on as full of the massed light of those very letters. Then he scatters white mustard seed, coriander, grains, parched rice and the like, each first whispered over with the weapon-mantra, and gradually he gathers them toward the **northeast** corner. This sweeping-and-gathering is the acquisition and enclosing of the place — drawing a clean perimeter and sealing it.

Then he offers a seat that ascends up to **Śuddhavidyā**, Pure Knowledge, and on it worships **Gaṇapati**, the remover of obstacles. Next he worships the small pot, decorated and filled with wine. He sets a betel nut down near the deity, and then, holding the principal Mantra in mind as the one that presides over everything, he **charges that small vessel one hundred and eight times** — pouring the power of the mantra into it, repetition upon repetition, until the water itself is saturated with the divine. For the further cessation of obstacles he worships the weapon in a second pot, and then the protectors of the world — the guardians of the directions — each furnished with his weapon, stationed in his own quarter.

Then comes a touch of human choreography that tells you this is a real ceremony with real people in the room. He hands the big pot, the one bearing the weapon, to a disciple who has already been initiated, and keeps the small pot for himself. The initiated disciple walks ahead, pouring out a continuous stream of water to the far end of the house to drive out obstacles, and the teacher follows behind him, and as he goes he calls out to the regents of the directions, beginning with **Śakra** — Indra, the lord of the east: *"O Śakra, by Śiva's command, to hold off all obstacles in your own direction, stay alert and on guard until the end of the rite."* And he says the same, turning to each one, to all the other regents who hold the other directions. The whole compass is thus posted with sentries before a single candidate is brought across.

## Why the Outer Rite Matters

A skeptic might ask, as we asked of the bath: if grace has already descended and the real event is inward, why all this ceremony? Why fire and sound and pots and pledge?

The chapter has already given the answer, and it is not sentimental. The outer rite is not the cause of the inner change — the inner sacrifice alone is full accomplishment. But human beings are embodied. We live through gesture and repetition, and the soul takes hold of truth more firmly when the body enacts it. For the one not yet absorbed, the outer rite is the actual road by which absorption arrives, because even a partial reflection on the inner sacrifice purifies. And for the one already absorbed, the rite is done to scorch away the last limitation. Either way, the ceremony gives the invisible event a visible shape, a moment in time the person can point to and say: there, that is where I crossed over.

And the teacher who performs all of this is, in this tradition, not merely a guide but a living conduit of the very grace that awakened the candidate. Through the teacher's own absorption — that waveless state in which the whole apparatus has already been recognized as the Lord — the divine acts. The whole rite we have walked through, from the calm heart that is the only true site, through the directions resolving into Consciousness, through the trident rising to the Sky-Goer's nectar, to the pots and the mustard seed and the sentinels of the quarters, is the meeting place of two movements: grace descending from above through the teacher, and the soul rising up to receive it. Initiation is where they touch.

This is the door. The candidate has been settled, the ground enclosed, the directions posted, the space ripened overnight. What lies beyond — the full initiation that severs bondage and the soul's long journey home through the purified worlds — is the work of the chapters to come.

Here ends the thirteenth chapter of the Tantrasara, called Entering the Door.
