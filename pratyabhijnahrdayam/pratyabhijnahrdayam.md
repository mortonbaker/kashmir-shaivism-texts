# The Heart of Recognition

*The Pratyabhijnahrdayam of Kshemaraja — the whole tradition in twenty steps*

A plain-English rendering, written for narration. Faithful to the original twenty sutras, in everyday modern language.

---

## About this text

This little text is the perfect doorway. Around a thousand years ago, the sage Kshemaraja — the great disciple of Abhinavagupta — wanted to hand the heart of the whole tradition to ordinary seekers who could not wade through the enormous philosophical works. So he distilled the entire system of Recognition into twenty short sutras, each one a single step, the whole forming a staircase that climbs from the nature of reality, down into why we suffer, and back up into freedom.

The tradition that this text crowns is called Pratyabhijna, which means "recognition" — and that word holds the central secret. You are not trying to *become* divine. You are trying to *recognize* what you have always been, the way you might suddenly recognize an old friend whose face you had failed to place. Liberation, here, is not an achievement. It is a recognition.

What follows renders all twenty steps in order, in plain words.

---

## The Twenty Steps

**One.** The independent Power of Consciousness is the cause of the whole universe — its appearing, its lasting, and its dissolving. Everything begins here. There is a single Consciousness, utterly free, depending on nothing outside itself, and it is this that brings every world into being, sustains it, and draws it back. Not a god who makes a universe out of separate materials — the very awareness in which all things appear, freely producing them out of itself.

**Two.** By her own free will, she unfolds the universe upon her own canvas. There is no outside material, no separate stuff to work with. Consciousness paints the entire cosmos on the surface of itself, the way a dreamer projects a whole world using nothing but their own mind. The screen and the paint and the painter are one.

**Three.** That one reality becomes the many, dividing itself into perceivers and the things they perceive. The single Light fans out into countless subjects facing countless objects — into the basic split of "me in here" and "the world out there." This division is real as appearance, but it is the one Consciousness wearing both masks, the knower and the known.

**Four.** And the individual perceiver, whose very self has contracted, embodies the whole universe in miniature. When Consciousness narrows itself into a limited soul, that soul is not a fragment cut off from the whole. It is the entire cosmos folded small — the complete Light, contracted into the shape of a person, carrying the whole within it like a seed carries the tree.

**Five.** Consciousness itself, descending from its unbounded fullness, becomes the limited mind. Here is the great contraction named plainly. The same boundless awareness, by stepping down through its own powers, becomes the ordinary thinking mind — the inner instrument that feels itself small, separate, and bound. Your mind is not something other than the infinite. It is the infinite, throttled down.

**Six.** And so the bound soul appears, made of this contracted consciousness — living out the limited round of ordinary experience, taking itself to be a small self in a large and threatening world.

**Seven.** Yet even in such a one, divided though they seem across the changing states of waking, dreaming, and deep sleep, the one Consciousness shines unbroken. The single awareness is never actually fragmented. It runs continuously through all the states and all the experiences, the unbroken light behind the flickering film, present even when unrecognized.

**Eight.** The various philosophies are like roles this one Self plays — each system a partial standpoint, a costume the one Consciousness tries on. Every worldview that takes some limited level of reality as the ultimate is the Self mistaking one of its own masks for its whole face. The tradition does not condemn these views; it places them, each as a stage in the Self's self-exploration.

**Nine.** Through the power of her own freedom, Consciousness conceals her true, full nature from herself, and so becomes bound. This is the hinge of the whole text. Bondage is not imposed from outside. It is Consciousness, by its own freedom, choosing to forget itself — drawing a veil across its own infinity for the sake of the play, and then mistaking the contracted appearance for the whole truth. The very power that could free you is the power that bound you: your own freedom, turned to forgetting.

**Ten.** Yet even in that contracted condition, the soul still performs the five great divine acts — for every soul, in every moment, is still creating, sustaining, dissolving, concealing, and revealing its own world of experience. Even bound, you have never stopped being divine. Every act of perception is a small creation; every turning-away a small dissolution. You are doing exactly what the Lord does, all day long, without knowing it.

**Eleven.** One who knows this — who recognizes the five divine acts unfolding in their own ordinary experience — becomes liberated even while living. To see that your every perception is the Lord's act of creation, your every forgetting the Lord's act of concealment, is to wake up inside the dream. This recognition, and nothing more exotic, is liberation.

**Twelve.** Failing to recognize this, deceived by one's own powers, the soul remains in bondage — frightened by a world that is its own projection, exiled in a country it secretly owns. The cause of all bondage is just this one thing: not recognizing one's own powers as one's own.

**Thirteen.** But by turning the attention inward and seizing recognition, one regains the full sovereignty that was always there. The path is a kind of inner about-face — a turning back toward the awareness that was looking out, until it recognizes itself. And in that recognition, the lost kingship is not granted anew; it is found never to have been lost.

**Fourteen.** The fire of this awakened consciousness, even when it sinks back toward ordinary experience, either burns the world of objects into itself or makes them conform to its own light. Once the recognition has truly caught, it does not simply vanish when the seeker returns to daily life. Like a fire, it either consumes what is fed to it — dissolving objects back into awareness — or transfigures them, so that the world is increasingly seen as nothing but the Light.

**Fifteen.** By strengthening this inner power, one masters the field of one's own experience — the whole world of appearances comes to be devoured and held within awareness as one's own. The practice is precisely the steady building of this recognition until it is strong enough to take in the entire field of experience as itself.

**Sixteen.** And when one becomes firmly established in the bliss of pure awareness, the dissolving and re-arising of the world no longer disturb — for both are now seen as the play of one's own Self. Stabilized in the recognition, the seeker is no longer rattled by the coming and going of experiences, pleasant or terrible. Worlds rise and fall, and the awakened one watches it all as their own delight, the way an author enjoys the rise and fall of fortunes in a story they are telling.

**Seventeen.** By this means the inner bliss unfolds, and one comes to dwell in the state of universal joy. As recognition deepens, a happiness wells up that does not depend on any object or circumstance — the native joy of Consciousness simply being itself, full and free.

**Eighteen.** The means to this is the steady tending of the inner pulse of awareness — a continuous, gentle returning to the living center of one's own consciousness. Recognition is kept alive by practice: again and again coming back to the awareness behind all experience, until the returning becomes effortless and constant.

**Nineteen.** Then, in the midst of all activity — moving, speaking, perceiving, living — the awareness of one's true Self is never interrupted. The fully awakened do not have to retreat from life to keep their recognition. It holds steady through every action, so that ordinary living and the deepest absorption become a single seamless thing.

**Twenty.** And so one attains the supreme nectar — the state in which the entire universe is experienced as the blissful expansion of one's own Consciousness, the very Self of Shiva. This is the summit and the whole point. The seeker comes to live in what the tradition calls world-bliss: the recognition that everything — every being, every object, every world — is the joyful overflowing of one's own awareness, which is none other than Shiva. Not "I am a small self watching the divine." But simply: *I am That. This whole universe is my own self, delighting in its own being.*

## Fidelity note

This rendering follows all twenty sutras of the Pratyabhijnahrdayam in their original order, from the opening declaration that the free Power of Consciousness is the cause of the universe, through the analysis of how Consciousness contracts itself into a bound soul, to the culminating recognition of one's identity with Shiva and the state of world-bliss. Each sutra is rendered as a single distinct step, faithful to its meaning, opened just enough in plain language to be heard and understood. The interpretive expansions stay within what Kshemaraja's own commentary draws out and add no teaching foreign to the text; nothing of the twenty steps has been omitted. This is an original plain-English rendering for audio, not a reproduction of any modern translation or commentary.

Here ends the Heart of Recognition.
