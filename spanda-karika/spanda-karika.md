# The Spanda Karika

*The Verses on Vibration — the pulse of consciousness*

A plain-English rendering, written for narration. Faithful to the original verses, in everyday modern language.

---

## About this text

If the Shiva Sutra is the seed of the tradition, the Spanda Karika is its first flowering — a set of fifty-two verses, traditionally received through Vasugupta and brought into the world by his disciple Kallata, written as a running meditation on a single word: *spanda*.

Spanda means a throb, a quiver, a pulse. But it points to something paradoxical that the tradition calls "vibrationless vibration" — a movement that does not actually move. It is the living dynamism at the very heart of Consciousness, the subtle stir by which the changeless Light gives rise to the whole moving world without ever itself changing. Think of the way a perfectly still pond, with no wind on it at all, still holds a kind of trembling aliveness in its surface tension — present, ready, vibrant, yet unmoving. That readiness, raised to the level of the absolute, is spanda.

The text unfolds in four movements: the nature of the pulse, the natural knowledge it opens, the powers it brings, and a brief closing.

---

## First Movement: The Nature of the Pulse

We bow first to the source — the divine awareness whose mere opening and closing of the eyes brings whole universes into being and dissolves them again. When that awareness opens its eyes, a world appears; when it closes them, the world withdraws into it. Creation and dissolution are nothing more dramatic than the blink of the one Consciousness. And that Consciousness is not somewhere far away; it is your own Self, the ground in which all of this rising and setting happens.

This pulsing awareness can never be suppressed or lost, because it is what does the suppressing and the losing. You cannot get behind it to deny it, because it is the very thing that would be doing the denying. Even when every object is stripped away — no thoughts, no sensations, no world — the pulse remains, for it is not any of those things. It is the awareness in which they came and went.

Do not mistake this for a blank or a void. When the tradition says that in the deepest state "there is nothing," an objector protests: then your spanda is just a dead rock, a nothing! But it is not dead and it is not nothing. What remains when all objects fall away is not emptiness but pure, awake freedom — the Self's own boundless spontaneity, which the tradition calls its absolute freedom. Where there are no objects to be found, the Self is most fully itself.

How do you find this pulse in your own experience? The text points inward, to the living machinery of your own awareness. Watch the cycle of your own faculties — the way perception rises, holds, and withdraws; the way the breath moves out and in; the way the senses reach and return. In all of that ceaseless inner activity there is a subtle, steady throb underneath, and by attending to it with care and reverence you can catch hold of the reality from which it all springs. That freedom is everywhere, native and uncontrived, running through everything you do.

Here the text gives one of its most quoted instructions, an answer to anyone who thinks meditation only happens with eyes closed on a cushion. Wherever the mind goes, there — right there — is the state of deep absorption, if you know how to look. As long as the mind does not contract and get lost in its object, every place the mind lands is a doorway to the Supreme. You do not have to still the mind into a corner. You have to recognize, in the very movement of the mind toward anything at all, the pulse of consciousness that is carrying it.

This same pulse is what makes the difference between the bound soul and the awakened one. The ordinary person, swept along by the rising and falling of inner states, is carried helplessly from mood to mood, thought to thought, never noticing the awareness in which it all occurs — and so is drained, scattered, diminished. But the one who stays anchored in the pulse is never robbed of their fullness. The states still rise and fall; the difference is that the awakened one watches them rise and fall from the unmoving ground, and so loses nothing.

Watch how this works at the edges of experience. At the moment a strong emotion seizes you, at the instant of intense fear or sudden joy or the first gasp of wonder, at the edge of sneezing, in the breathless second before sleep takes you — in all these moments the ordinary mind drops away for a flicker, and the pulse of pure awareness is laid bare. The tradition says: catch it there. These cracks in the ordinary surface are where the Light shines through most plainly. Where the mind is suspended, the Self stands revealed.

---

## Second Movement: The Natural Knowing

From recognizing the pulse, a new kind of knowledge dawns — not learned, but natural; not acquired from outside, but rising spontaneously from within. This is the knowledge of one's own identity with Shiva, the recognition that the awareness pulsing in you is the very awareness pulsing as the whole cosmos.

The bound soul lives under a kind of spell. Because it does not recognize the pulse as its own, it is acted upon by its own powers rather than wielding them — the energies of consciousness, which are meant to be the soul's own radiant capacities, instead rise up as if foreign, veiling the soul from itself and binding it in limitation. The very powers that could free you, unrecognized, are what bind you. Words and concepts, which should be tools in your hand, become the bars of the cage, because you have forgotten that you are the one speaking them.

But when the natural knowing dawns, the spell breaks. The same powers that bound the soul now turn and serve it. Recognized, they become a radiant circle of energies playing for their master rather than imprisoning a stranger. The one who awakens to this never falls back into the old forgetting, because they have seen, directly, that the doer and the deed and the world were always one single pulse of their own awareness.

---

## Third Movement: The Powers of the Pulse

The third movement describes what unfolds for one established in the pulse — the natural masteries that come, not as the goal, but as the overflow of a consciousness awake to itself.

Such a one, even as they drift toward sleep, can stay awake at the threshold and enter the higher states deliberately, riding the pulse down into dream and dreamless depth without losing the thread of awareness. The states that swallow ordinary people whole become, for the awakened, transparent regions they can move through consciously.

Because they are anchored in the source from which all the energies pour, they find those energies answering to them. Whatever they sincerely turn toward tends to manifest; the body and senses become responsive instruments rather than unruly masters; the obstacles that scatter an ordinary mind lose their grip. The text is careful, though: these are described so the seeker will recognize them as signs along the way, not so they will be chased. To hunt the powers for their own sake is to fall back asleep at the very threshold of waking.

Above all, the awakened one comes to live in an unbroken fullness. Hunger, weariness, the buffeting of circumstance — these still touch the body, but they no longer reach the center, because the center has been found to be the inexhaustible pulse itself. Just as a seed holds the whole tree, the pulse holds the whole of one's being, and the one who rests in it draws on a source that never runs dry. They become, in the tradition's image, like one who has remembered they are the author of the dream they are walking through — able to act freely within it, because they know what it is made of.

---

## Conclusion

The text closes by honoring the treasure it has handed on. This knowledge is hard to come by and easily lost, a jewel that must be carefully kept in the cave of the heart. As gaining it and guarding it brought the highest good to Vasugupta, through whom it came, so it will bring that same good to anyone, in any time, who attains it and holds it safe within. The pulse was never the private possession of one sage. It is the birthright of everyone, the living throb of the one Consciousness, waiting in the heart of each person to be recognized as their own.

## Fidelity note

This rendering follows the fifty-two verses of the Spanda Karika in their four traditional movements — the nature of the vibration (verses 1–25), the natural knowledge of one's identity with Shiva (26–32), the powers that arise from the vibration (33–52 of the running count), and Vasugupta's two-verse conclusion. The famous teaching verses are rendered closely and in sequence: the opening on the divine blink that creates and dissolves worlds, the instruction that wherever the mind goes the absorbed state is found, and the catching of the pulse at the edges of strong emotion, sneezing, and the brink of sleep. The more technical material on the yogic powers is conveyed faithfully in measured form rather than itemized, matching how the text itself frames these as signs rather than goals. Nothing has been added beyond what the verses teach, and no movement of the text has been omitted. This is an original plain-English rendering for audio, not a reproduction of any modern translation or commentary.

Here ends the Spanda Karika.
