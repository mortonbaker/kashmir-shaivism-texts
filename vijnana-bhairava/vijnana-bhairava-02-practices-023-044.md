# The Vijnana Bhairava Tantra

## Practices 23–44: Sound, Mantra, and the Doorways of the Senses

*The second family: entering through hearing, sacred sound, and the living edge of sensation.*

---

**23. The unstruck sound.** Turn your attention to the continuous sound that goes on inside you on its own — the subtle inner hum that no one makes and that never stops. Follow it inward, from its grosser to its finer reaches, and as you ride it toward its source, it carries you up into the soundless awareness from which it rises.

*The Tantrasara teaches this same unstruck sound in its fifth chapter, the sound struck by nothing that sounds in every living being. There it is one rung of the inner ladder; here you simply listen for it and let it draw you home.*

**24. Sound trailing into silence.** Sound a sacred syllable, or any resonant tone, aloud — and then follow it as it fades, growing fainter and finer, until it dissolves into total silence. Ride the sound down into the silence at its end, and rest in that silence. The vanishing point of the sound is a doorway into the soundless supreme.

**25. The flowing thread of a chanted sound.** Dwell on a sacred sound chanted slowly and continuously, without the breaks and pauses of ordinary speech — an unbroken stream of tone. As the mind rides that continuous flow, it is carried past its own chatter into the open void at the heart of the sound.

**26. Tracing a written or imagined letter into emptiness.** Take a single sacred letter or syllable and contemplate it dissolving — its form thinning, emptying, opening into the void at its core. As the letter melts into emptiness, the mind melts with it into the supreme.

**27. The hidden meaning behind the words of a hymn.** When absorbed in a sacred recitation or a beautiful hymn, let go of the surface meaning and sink into the wordless devotion beneath it — the silent fullness the words were only pointing at. Resting there, the supreme reveals itself.

**28. Sustained attention until the object dissolves.** Fix the mind on a single thing — any object will do — with complete, unwavering attention. Hold it so steadily that the usual mental commentary falls silent. As the gripping stillness deepens, the particular object opens out into the boundless, and supportless awareness dawns.

**29. The space inside the body filled with bliss.** Contemplate the whole inner space of the body as flooded with subtle energy and bliss — not the physical organs, but the living field of awareness within, brimming and radiant. Saturating the body-space with this felt fullness, the limited self dissolves into delight.

**30. Energy rising and filling the crown.** Feel the subtle power rising up through you and flooding the space at the crown, filling the whole head with a fine, bright energy, and let your awareness dissolve into that radiance. As it fills and overflows, the boundless dawns.

**31. The body as the whole universe.** Contemplate your own body as containing the entire cosmos — every world, every level of being, present within this single form. Holding the whole universe within the body in this way, the small bounded self gives way to the all-containing Self.

*This is the Tantrasara's seventh chapter made into a practice: the body is the universe written small, and to know it truly is to find the cosmos within your own form.*

**32. Awareness in the spine like a luminous stalk.** Meditate on the central channel of the spine as a slender, upright stalk of light, and concentrate within it. As awareness gathers into that luminous core, the divine shines forth from within.

**33. The senses withdrawn into the heart.** Gather the outward-streaming senses — sight, hearing, all of them — and draw them inward, dissolving each into the open space of the heart. As the scattered faculties are gathered back to their source, the heart-space opens into the supreme.

**34. The mind held wherever it wants to go.** Wherever the mind wanders, whatever it runs toward, let it go there — but stay aware. Do not fight the mind's movement; instead, ride it with attention, and recognize the awareness present in the very place the mind lands. Held this way, every wandering becomes a doorway, and absorption is found wherever the mind goes.

*This is the Spanda Karika's teaching exactly — wherever the mind goes, there is the absorbed state — turned into a deliberate practice. It is one of the most liberating instructions in the whole text: you need not still the mind by force; you need only stay awake within its movement.*

**35. Awareness in the upward and downward currents.** Attend to the two great currents within — the rising and the descending energies of the breath and body — and rest in the awareness that holds them both. Between and within these currents, the centered state dawns.

**36. The whole body as consciousness alone.** Contemplate the entire body, inside and out, as made of nothing but awareness — no flesh, no bone, only consciousness taking this shape. As the felt sense of a material body dissolves into pure awareness, the supreme is recognized as one's very substance.

**37. Joy in the body remembered or felt.** When a wave of intense joy or pleasure arises in the body — recalled or present — do not grasp at its object. Instead, plunge into the felt bliss itself, sink your whole attention into the delight, and let it carry you. The bliss, entered fully, opens into the boundless bliss of the Self.

*Here the text begins its great teaching that pleasure itself is a doorway. The Tantrasara would frame it this way: every joy is a small taste of the Self's own native bliss, and to follow the joy to its root is to find the Light delighting in itself.*

**38. The bliss of union.** In the height of intimate union, when delight floods the whole being, do not let the mind fixate on the other person or the act. Instead, enter the surging bliss itself and rest in it, recognizing it as the bliss of your own awakened awareness. The delight, taken inward, reveals the supreme joy that was its source all along.

*The tradition is unembarrassed here, and it is important to hear it rightly. The point is never mere indulgence; it is that the most intense human delight, entered with awareness rather than grasping, lays bare the same bliss the yogi seeks in deepest meditation. The Tantrasara's whole vision rests on this: there is nothing outside the Light, so even the most ordinary or intimate joy is the Light, waiting to be recognized.*

**39. The remembered echo of delight.** Even without the experience itself, simply remember a past joy or pleasure vividly, and as the felt glow of it returns to the body, sink into that glow. The remembered delight, entered as pure feeling, becomes a doorway to the bliss within.

**40. The delight of fine food and drink.** In the moment of savoring delicious food or drink, when the body fills with the pleasure of the taste, rest your attention fully in that satisfaction. Sink into the contentment itself, and the small pleasure opens into the great fullness of the Self.

**41. The lingering joy of music and beauty.** When delighted by beautiful music, or any sense-pleasure that fills you with sustained joy, let the mind become one with the delight, steady and absorbed. As awareness merges with the unbroken pleasure, it dissolves into the bliss that underlies all delight.

*Practices 37 through 41 form a striking sequence, and together they make the text's boldest claim: that the senses are not enemies of the spirit but unguarded doorways into it. The Tantrasara's path of the individual works exactly here, using the whole embodied life — taste, sound, sensation — as legitimate means, because the same Consciousness that is sought within is also the one tasting the food and hearing the music.*

**42. Contentment at the satisfaction of any desire.** Whenever a desire is satisfied and a wave of contentment arises, turn from the thing that satisfied it and rest in the contentment itself. That settled fullness, entered directly, is a taste of the Self's own completeness, in which nothing is lacking and nothing is wanted.

**43. The threshold of sleep.** At the moment sleep is about to take you — when waking has loosened but dream has not yet begun, in that soft borderland between the two — turn your attention to the spreading openness, and rest there awake. In that threshold, the supreme reality quietly reveals itself.

*Again the Spanda Karika's teaching: at the brink of sleep the ordinary mind lets go, and the pulse of pure awareness is laid bare. Most people fall asleep through this doorway every night without ever noticing it stood open.*

**44. Gazing at a vast open expanse.** Rest your gaze on a wide, uninterrupted expanse — open landscape, a great plain, a distant horizon, empty sky — something with no boundary for the eye to catch on. As the gaze and the mind spread out into that openness without an edge to land on, awareness loses its own boundaries and rests in the limitless.
