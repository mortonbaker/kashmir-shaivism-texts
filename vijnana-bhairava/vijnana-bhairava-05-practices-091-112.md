# The Vijnana Bhairava Tantra

## Practices 91–112: The Self, the Supreme Lord, and the Closing

*The final family: resting directly in one's own divine nature — and the end of the dialogue.*

---

**91. The Self as pure "I" before all attributes.** Turn attention to the bare sense of "I am," stripped of every description — not "I am this body," not "I am this name or role," but the simple, attributeless awareness of being itself. Rest in that pure I-ness before any quality attaches to it. There, the true Self shines, identical with the supreme.

**92. Wherever the mind settles, the Self.** Let the mind come to rest wherever it naturally settles — on any object, in any place — and recognize, right there, that what it has settled into is awareness, the Self. Since the Self is everywhere, every resting place is the supreme; the mind cannot land anywhere it is not.

**93. The Self pervading the whole body at once.** Spread your awareness evenly through the entire body at the same moment — not focused in the head or anywhere in particular, but filling the whole form equally with presence. As awareness pervades the body without a center, the contracted sense of self gives way to the all-pervading Self.

**94. The mind dissolved into the heart in a single instant.** In one swift movement, drop the whole mind into the heart-space and let it dissolve there completely, all at once. In that sudden, total release into the heart, the boundless dawns.

**95. Steady in the conviction "I am Shiva."** Hold, with full and unwavering conviction, the recognition that your essential nature is Shiva — the one Consciousness, free and boundless, the source of all. Not as a wish or a hope, but as a settled certainty. Established in this conviction, you become what you recognize.

*This is the liberating thought of the Tantrasara's path of knowledge in its most direct form: the counter-thought "I am Shiva" that shatters the binding thought "I am small," held until it ripens into direct recognition.*

**96. Awareness aware only of itself.** Rest in awareness that has turned fully back upon itself, knowing nothing but its own knowing — no object, no other, only consciousness conscious of being consciousness. In that pure self-awareness, the supreme is not approached but simply recognized as what was always already the case.

**97. The certainty that pervades all states.** Contemplate the single awareness, full and self-evident, that underlies and pervades every state and every experience without exception — the one constant in which all the changing contents appear. Resting in that all-pervading certainty, the supreme is found everywhere and always.

**98. Joy welling up from the recognition of the Self.** As the recognition of your true nature deepens, a joy arises that depends on nothing outside — the native delight of Consciousness simply being itself. Rest in that self-arising bliss, and let it carry you fully into the supreme.

**99. The world as a wave on the ocean of awareness.** Contemplate the whole of existence as waves rising and falling on a single ocean of Consciousness — every form a momentary swell, never separate from the water, always the one sea taking shape. Resting as the ocean rather than the wave, you rest in the Self of all.

**100. The space of awareness between any two breaths or moments.** In the gap between any two of anything — breaths, heartbeats, instants, perceptions — find the open space of awareness that is always there, holding the two apart and together. Rest in that ever-present interval. In the gap, the supreme is continuously available.

**101. The energy of a fierce experience taken inward.** When a powerful experience seizes the whole being — a great fear, a great delight, a great shock that floods the system — do not let it scatter outward. Gather its intensity inward, into the core of awareness, and ride the sheer force of it into the depths. The most intense experiences, turned inward, are the swiftest doorways.

*The Spanda Karika taught this at the edges of fear and joy; the intensity that ordinarily sweeps us away, caught and turned inward, lays the pure pulse bare.*

**102. The whole of perception as a single field of awareness.** Take in the entire field of what is present — all sights, sounds, sensations at once — not as many separate things but as one unbroken field of awareness, a single seamless presence. Resting as the whole field rather than picking out parts, the undivided Self is recognized.

**103. The Self as the light in which all knowing happens.** Contemplate that every act of knowing, in yourself and in all beings, happens by one light — the light of Consciousness that makes all knowing possible, as the sun makes all seeing possible. Resting as that light rather than as any particular knower, the supreme is found.

**104. Awareness untouched, like space, by all it contains.** Contemplate awareness as being like space itself — containing everything, touched and stained by nothing, allowing all things to appear within it while remaining perfectly clear and free. Resting as that space-like awareness, you are untouched by all that passes through.

**105. The knower of the body, not the body.** Turn attention firmly to the one who knows the body — the awareness for which the body is an object — and recognize that you are this knower, never the known. As identification shifts from the body to its knower, the deathless Self is recognized.

**106. The supreme reached by releasing every effort.** Let go, finally, of all effort, all technique, all striving to reach the supreme — and simply rest, effortless, in what is already the case. For the supreme was never far, never to be attained; it is your own nature, found the moment all reaching ceases.

*Here the practices circle back to where Bhairava began — the pathless path of the Tantrasara's second chapter, where there is nothing to do because you have never been anywhere but home.*

**107. The Self recognized in deep, contented rest.** In moments of deep, easeful rest — when the body is at peace and the mind has stopped its striving — turn gently toward the contentment itself and recognize in it a taste of the Self's own fullness. That natural ease is a quiet doorway to the boundless.

**108. The mind absorbed wherever it finds delight.** Wherever the mind finds genuine delight, let it rest there fully and become absorbed — and in the depth of that absorption, recognize the bliss of the Self shining through. Since the Self is the source of all delight, every true joy leads home if followed to its root.

**109. Devotion melting the self into the Lord.** Let the heart pour out in love toward the supreme, in wholehearted devotion — and as the sense of a separate self melts in that love, only the Lord remains, recognized now as one's own deepest being. Devotion, carried to its end, dissolves the very separation it began in.

**110. The Lord as the Self of all that exists.** Contemplate the supreme Lord as the single Self present in everything that exists — the one awareness that is the innermost reality of every being and every thing. Recognizing the Lord as the Self of all, the whole world becomes the body of the beloved, and division ends.

**111. Resting in the natural state, free of all method.** Simply abide in your own natural condition, just as it is, without adopting any technique or straining toward any state — the effortless, ever-present awareness that needs no method because it is already complete. This natural resting is itself the highest practice and the end of all practice.

**112. The Self as boundless Consciousness, ever free.** Rest in the recognition of your true nature as boundless Consciousness — unborn, undying, ever free, the one Light that was never bound and never needs releasing. This final recognition is not a technique among techniques but the destination of all of them: simply being, knowingly, what you have always been.

## The Closing of the Dialogue

When Bhairava had given the hundred and twelve doorways, the Goddess was satisfied at last. Her doubt, which the whole of the sacred knowledge had not dispelled, was dispelled now — not by one more doctrine, but by being handed the keys to direct experience.

Bhairava makes his great promise once more, and it is worth hearing as the text's final word on its own purpose. One does not need to master all hundred and twelve. Established firmly in even a single one of these practices, a person attains the very state of Bhairava — becomes, in their own being, the awakened Consciousness these techniques were always pointing toward. Such a one is liberated while still living, free in the midst of all activity, untouched by the world even while fully engaged in it. They have found the deathless, and the fear that drives ordinary life loosens its grip.

And the Goddess, hearing this, recognizes its truth — for she is herself that Consciousness, and the whole dialogue has been, in a sense, the one Awareness teaching itself, Shakti and Shiva in loving conversation that was never really between two. The teaching ends as the tradition always ends: not with a god instructing a separate devotee, but with Consciousness recognizing itself, delighting in its own boundless nature, and overflowing in love.

So take these hundred and twelve not as a burden but as a gift freely given. Try them. Find the one that quietly opens for you. And when it opens — when some ordinary moment, a breath or a taste or a silence between thoughts, suddenly reveals the vast awake spaciousness that was always there — rest in it, and know that you have found what the Goddess was asking for, which was only ever your own true face.

## Fidelity note

This rendering presents all hundred and twelve centering techniques (*dharanas*) of the Vijnana Bhairava Tantra in their traditional sequence, framed by the opening dialogue between the Goddess and Bhairava and closed by Bhairava's promise and the Goddess's satisfaction. Each practice is rendered as a doable technique faithful to the meaning of its source verse, in plain modern language, never reproducing any modern translator's wording. The famous practices — the turning points of the breath, the gap between two thoughts, the center between any two things, the meditations on the void and on sensory delight, the holding of emotion without its object, the world seen as a magic show, and the direct conviction "I am Shiva" — are all rendered individually and in place. Where a practice illuminates or is illuminated by the Tantrasara (the breath-and-time work, the dissolution of the elements, the path of will, the liberating thought, the recognition of the world as one's own awareness), the connection is drawn. Nothing essential has been omitted, and the only additions are genuine explanations of what each technique asks you to do. The verse-numbering of the underlying Sanskrit varies slightly across editions; this rendering follows the standard count of one hundred and twelve practices. This is an original plain-English rendering for audio, not a reproduction of any modern translation or commentary.
