# The Vijnana Bhairava Tantra

## Practices 67–90: Emotion, Knowing, and the Play of Appearances

*The fourth family: using strong feeling, the structure of perception, and the dreamlike nature of the world.*

---

**67. The body trembling on a high place.** Stand, in body or in vivid imagination, at the edge of a great height — a cliff, a precipice — where the ordinary footing falls away and the body thrills with the vastness below. In that suspended, exhilarated moment when the usual ground is gone, the mind drops its habits and the boundless opens.

**68. The opening flood of awe.** When wonder or awe seizes you — at vastness, at beauty, at something that stops the mind in its tracks — do not rush past it. Rest in the wide-open astonishment itself, before the mind closes it down with explanation. The Shiva Sutra called the stages of yoga "full of wonder"; here that very wonder is the doorway.

**69. The lull after a great desire.** When a long-held longing is at last fulfilled and the wanting suddenly ceases, there is a quiet that opens in its wake — the mind, having nothing left to chase, falls still. Catch that stillness and rest in it. In the hush after desire, the Self stands revealed.

**70. The flash at the end of knowledge.** At the moment some piece of knowledge is fully grasped — the instant of "I see it" when understanding completes itself — there is a brief flash before the mind moves on. Rest in that flash. The completion of knowing opens, for an instant, into the knower behind all knowing.

**71. Equal-minded toward friend and stranger.** Cultivate, even briefly, a state in which the mind holds neither attraction toward a friend nor aversion toward a stranger — perfectly even, leaning toward neither. In that balanced evenness, free of grasping and pushing away, the centered awareness shines.

*This is the equanimity the Tantrasara describes as the mark of the liberated life — the unshaken evenness of one who sees the one Self in all, no longer swung between liking and disliking.*

**72. The mood held without its object.** When a strong feeling rises — anger, desire, joy — toward someone or something, do not pour it onto its object. Hold the raw energy of the emotion itself, detached from the person or thing that triggered it, and look directly into it. Entered this way, the feeling reveals the pure power of awareness at its core.

*This is one of the text's subtlest and most practical teachings: the emotion is not the enemy; the problem is only that we fling it outward at a target. Held inwardly as bare energy, the very same feeling becomes fuel for awakening.*

**73. Knowledge and the known seen as one awareness.** Contemplate that the act of knowing, the thing known, and the knower are not three separate things but a single movement of one Consciousness. As the three collapse into one, the division that structures all ordinary experience dissolves, and the undivided Light remains.

**74. Awareness pervading all beings equally.** Contemplate the one Consciousness equally present in yourself and in every other being — the same awareness looking out through every pair of eyes. As the sense of a private, separate self gives way to the one awareness shared by all, the supreme is recognized everywhere.

**75. The mind freed of "this" and "that."** Let go, in contemplation, of all the mind's sorting into this and that, here and there, mine and not-mine. Release the whole apparatus of division and rest in the undivided awareness that was there before the sorting began. In that freedom from distinction, the boundless dawns.

**76. Awareness as the empty sky in which all appears.** Contemplate your own awareness as a clear, empty sky, and all experiences — thoughts, sensations, the whole world — as clouds passing through it, never staining it, never disturbing its openness. Resting as the sky rather than the clouds, you rest in the changeless Self.

**77. Desire, knowledge, and action as one power.** See that wanting, knowing, and doing — the three great movements of your inner life — are all expressions of a single underlying power, the one energy of consciousness taking three forms. Tracing all three back to their single source, awareness rests in its own undivided power.

*These three are the will, knowledge, and action that the Tantrasara organizes its whole path around — the three faces of the one Consciousness, here recognized as a single energy.*

**78. The perceiving awareness in every perception.** In any act of perception whatsoever, turn from the thing perceived to the awareness that is perceiving it — the constant light present in every single perception, however different the objects. Resting in that ever-present perceiver, the supreme is found shining in all experience.

**79. The world recognized as one's own awareness.** Contemplate the entire perceived world as nothing other than your own awareness appearing as form — not a foreign reality out there, but the Light of your own consciousness taking the shape of a world. As the world is recognized as Self, the division between inner and outer dissolves.

*This is the Pratyabhijnahrdayam's culminating recognition turned into a practice: the universe is the blissful expansion of one's own Consciousness, and to see it so is world-bliss.*

**80. The knower unchanged through every changing object.** Notice that objects endlessly change — they come, they go, they shift — but the awareness that knows them does not change; it is the same constant light through every passing thing. Rest your attention on that unchanging knower. In it, the deathless Self is recognized.

**81. Consciousness untouched by pleasure and pain.** Contemplate that pleasure and pain pass across awareness like weather across the sky, but the awareness itself is never wounded by the pain nor inflated by the pleasure — it remains, in its own nature, untouched. Resting as that untouched awareness, one is free in the midst of all experience.

**82. The whole world seen as a magic show.** Contemplate the entire world as a magician's display — vivid, convincing, and yet not solid, not ultimately real, a shimmering appearance with no substance behind it but the one Consciousness conjuring it. Seeing the world this way, the mind stops clinging, and freedom dawns.

*This is the Tantrasara's screen and the dreamer's city: the world is real as appearance and empty as a separate thing, a play of light projected by the one awareness that is you.*

**83. Everything as the play of consciousness.** See all that happens — every event, every form, every change — as the spontaneous play of the one Consciousness delighting in its own creativity, a free and joyful display with no other purpose than the joy of the playing. Resting in this, life becomes a celebration rather than a burden.

**84. The unreality of bondage itself.** Contemplate deeply that bondage was never truly real — that the soul was never actually bound, only dreaming it was bound, and that there is therefore nothing real to escape from. As the very idea of bondage dissolves, what remains is the freedom that was always the case.

*Pure Tantrasara: the chain was only ever a misunderstanding, and recognition does not break a real chain but reveals there was none.*

**85. Steady in the knowledge "I am everywhere."** Hold the firm conviction that your true Self is not confined to the body but pervades everything, present in all places, the awareness in which the whole world appears. Steadied in this knowing, the cramped sense of being trapped in one small body dissolves into boundlessness.

**86. The same consciousness in waking, dream, and sleep.** Contemplate the single awareness that is present through all three states — awake, dreaming, deep asleep — the unbroken light that does not vanish when waking ends or dreaming stops. Resting in that continuous awareness, you find the Fourth, which underlies and exceeds them all.

*The Shiva Sutra's teaching of the Fourth state, made into a contemplation: find what does not come and go with the states, and you have found what you are.*

**87. The Self as the source of all the powers.** Contemplate your own awareness as the single source from which all your faculties pour — seeing, hearing, thinking, moving — the one root behind every branch. Tracing the many powers back to their one source, awareness rests in its own undivided sovereignty.

**88. Awareness filling a chosen object until it overflows.** Take any object and fill it, in contemplation, with your own awareness — pour attention into it so completely that the boundary between you and it dissolves, and it is known from within as consciousness. As the object is flooded with awareness, the division between self and world gives way.

**89. The thread of awareness through a sequence of objects.** As perception moves from one thing to the next to the next, attend not to the changing things but to the continuous thread of awareness running through the whole sequence, unbroken from object to object. Resting on that thread rather than the beads, the constant Self is found.

**90. The mind dissolved at the thought of the supreme.** Simply turn the mind, with devotion and steadiness, toward the supreme reality itself — and as the mind dwells on that boundless truth, it loses its own boundaries and dissolves into what it contemplates. Thinking of the limitless, the mind becomes limitless.
