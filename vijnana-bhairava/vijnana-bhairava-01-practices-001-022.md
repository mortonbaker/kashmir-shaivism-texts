# The Vijnana Bhairava Tantra

## Practices 1–22: The Breath and the Center

*The first family of techniques: finding the gap in the breath, and resting in the middle.*

---

**1. The two turning points of the breath.** Watch your breathing without changing it. The out-breath rises and flows up and out; the in-breath sinks and flows down and in. At the top of the out-breath there is a turning point, a place where the breath has gone out and not yet come back. At the bottom of the in-breath there is another. Rest your whole attention on these two turning points. In those two places the breath, and the mind with it, comes to a natural stop — and in that stop, Bhairava shines.

*This is the foundational practice of the whole text, and it is the same insight the Tantrasara builds its sixth chapter on: the breath carries the pulse of consciousness, and at the turn between out and in, time itself pauses. Where the Tantrasara contemplates the whole of time folded into the breath, here you simply rest at the seam.*

**2. The center of the inward and outward flow.** As the breath moves out and as it moves in, follow it to the central channel — the subtle core running up through you. Let the attention settle into that center where both movements meet. Resting there, you touch the place from which both the outward and the inward arise.

**3. Holding the breath at the full pause.** Let the breath rise to its fullest and stay there, suspended, neither breathing in nor out — the held stillness at the top. In that retention, when the breath is poised and motionless, the peaceful Power reveals herself. Do not strain; let the pause be natural, and rest in its quiet.

**4. The rising energy like lightning.** Picture the subtle energy rising up the central channel from its root, ascending through the centers of the body, thin as a lotus fiber, brightening as it climbs. As it rises and dissolves at the crown, the great awakening dawns. This is the kundalini practice in seed form — the upward journey of the body's own power into the open space above.

*The Tantrasara's fifth chapter maps these same centers and this same upward current; here it is given as a single vivid visualization to ride.*

**5. Piercing the centers one by one.** Move your attention up through the energy-centers in sequence, each in turn, releasing each as you pass beyond it — letting go of the grosser to rise into the subtler, center by center, until at the journey's end the power dissolves into the supreme and Bhairava's form appears.

**6. The energy in the twelve stations.** Carry the awareness up through the successive stations of the inner ascent, understanding each as you pass, becoming free of the gross and then the subtle one after another, until at the very end the rising power merges into the absolute.

**7. The fivefold dissolution.** Contemplate the levels of your own being — the grosser elements dissolving into the subtler, the subtler into the powers of mind, mind into pure awareness — each dissolving upward into the next, until nothing gross remains and only Consciousness is left. As each layer melts into the one above it, the supreme dawns.

*This is precisely the Tantrasara's reverse-ascent through the categories — the staircase of the cosmos climbed back upward, here turned into a contemplation you run within your own body.*

**8. Awareness on the crown.** Fix the attention steadily at the crown, the opening at the top, while the senses are stilled — and in that fixed, undistracted attention, the boundless dawns.

**9. The space within the central channel.** Meditate on the central channel as a thread of pure light running up the core of the body, and let your awareness rest within that luminous line. Resting in it, the divine shines forth from within.

**10. The five voids of the senses.** Place the attention at the gateways of perception — and instead of letting it flow out to objects, let it sink into the emptiness behind each sense. With the doors of perception turned back toward their own source, the void opens, and in that void the supreme is found.

**11. Resting in the heart-space.** Let the mind sink into the space of the heart — the inner cave of awareness at the center of the chest — and stay there, in that open, empty room. As the mind dissolves into the heart-space, the highest reality is revealed.

*The Tantrasara invokes this same Heart in its opening verses as the source from which all things pour. Here it becomes a place you can actually go: drop the mind into the chest and rest in the spaciousness there.*

**12. The body as empty space.** Contemplate your own body, and the whole world with it, as nothing but empty space — vast, open, with no solidity anywhere. Hold this steadily until the sense of a dense, bounded body dissolves into boundless openness, and awareness stands free of all support.

*The void contemplations are among the most characteristic of this text. The Tantrasara would say: you are recognizing that the densest disguise, the solid body, was always made of the same open Light as everything else.*

**13. The void above and the void below.** Meditate on emptiness in the body — the void of the core, the void of the base, the void of the heart — all at once, simultaneously empty. When all the inner spaces open together, thought falls away and the thought-free state arises.

**14. The skin as an empty wall.** Imagine the surface of the body, the skin, as a thin wall enclosing nothing but emptiness — pure space within, pure space without, the wall itself dissolving. Held this way, the bounded self opens into the unbounded.

**15. Awareness suspended between above and below.** Let the attention hover in the space between the crown above and the base below, holding neither, resting in the suspended middle. In that balanced betweenness, the centered state shines.

**16. The world dissolving in fire and flood.** Picture the entire universe consumed — burnt to nothing in a great fire, or dissolved away in a vast flood — until not a single object remains anywhere, only open emptiness in every direction. When the whole world of objects has been dissolved in contemplation, awareness rests, objectless and free.

*This is the dissolution-contemplation the Tantrasara describes in the path of the individual — rehearsing the cosmic withdrawal within your own meditation, so that with every object gone, what remains is recognized as the Light that was holding them.*

**17. Resting where one object is released and the next not yet grasped.** In ordinary perception the mind leaps from object to object. Catch the instant when it has let go of one and not yet seized the next — the bare gap between two perceptions. Rest in that gap. There, in the space where the mind holds nothing, reality shines forth in the center.

*Here is the master theme of centering in its purest form, and the direct echo of the Spanda Karika: in the seam between two graspings, the pulse of pure awareness is laid bare.*

**18. The single thought held until it opens.** Take one object, one idea, and hold the mind to it alone, refusing every other thought that tries to enter. Keep the mind one-pointed on that single support, and as all other movement falls away, the awareness behind the single thought opens into boundless space.

**19. Resting in the space between two thoughts.** Watch the stream of thoughts. Between any two of them there is a silent interval, brief and easily missed. Turn your attention to that interval and rest in it. As you learn to dwell in the gap between thoughts, the gap widens into the open sky of consciousness.

*This is perhaps the most universally taught of all the practices, and the Tantrasara's path of knowledge explains exactly why it works: thought itself is the binding, and in the space where thought is absent, the bondage simply isn't there.*

**20. The mind turned back on itself.** Instead of letting awareness flow outward to its objects, turn it back to look at itself — let the knower seek the knower. When attention curves back upon its own source and finds the awareness that was doing the looking, the supreme is recognized as one's own self.

**21. Dwelling on the spaciousness of the sky.** Gaze, with soft and steady attention, into clear open sky — empty, cloudless, boundless — and let the mind take on the quality of what it rests on. As the awareness merges with that limitless openness, it discovers its own limitless nature.

**22. The supportless awareness.** Let the mind release every support — every object, every image, every prop it usually leans on — and remain, for a moment, holding nothing at all. In that supportless condition, where awareness rests on nothing, it rests at last on itself, and the boundless dawns.
