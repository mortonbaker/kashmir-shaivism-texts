# The Vijnana Bhairava Tantra

## Practices 45–66: The Void, the Elements, and the Dissolving of Distinctions

*The third family: emptying the field of awareness until only the Light remains.*

---

**45. The threefold void of the body.** Contemplate emptiness simultaneously in three places — the void of the central channel along the spine, the void of the base below, and the void of the heart — all empty at once. When these inner spaces open together, thought subsides and the thought-free state arises.

**46. The void spreading through the whole body.** Let the sense of emptiness, begun in one place, spread outward until it fills the entire body — every part open, weightless, without solidity, made of space. When the whole form is felt as void, awareness rests, free of its bodily anchor.

**47. Emptiness in all directions at once.** Contemplate the empty space stretching out in every direction simultaneously — above, below, before, behind, to every side — boundless openness with no center and no edge. Dissolving the mind into that all-directional void, the supreme is revealed.

**48. The body emptied from within.** Picture the body as a hollow form, its inner substance dissolved away, nothing inside but pure open space. As the felt density empties out, the bounded self gives way to the boundless within.

**49. The empty heart.** Rest the mind in the open emptiness of the heart-space, the inner room of awareness, and let it remain there, holding nothing. As the mind dissolves into that empty fullness, the highest reality shines.

**50. Awareness with no object, no thought, no support.** Let the mind release everything — every object, every thought, every prop — and rest in pure awareness alone, without form or content. This supportless, objectless awareness is itself the supreme; rest in it and you rest in Bhairava.

*This bare, formless resting is the closest the practices come to the pathless path of the Tantrasara's second chapter — no technique at all, only awareness recognizing itself when everything else is set down.*

**51. The fire of awareness consuming the body.** Imagine a subtle fire rising and burning through the body from the base upward, consuming it entirely, until nothing of the physical form remains — only the awareness that watched it burn. With the body dissolved in the fire of contemplation, the unburnable Self stands revealed.

**52. The universe consumed by the fire of time.** Meditate on the entire cosmos being burnt to ash in the great fire at the end of time, every world consumed, until nothing whatsoever is left but empty space — and then rest in the awareness that remains when all has been consumed. In that final emptiness, the supreme dawns.

*This is the cosmic-dissolution contemplation the Tantrasara describes — running the universe's withdrawal in reverse within your own meditation, so that with every world gone, only the Light that held them is left.*

**53. The world dissolved, awareness remaining.** Having dissolved the whole world in contemplation — every object, every being, every level — rest in the tranquil awareness that remains when there is nothing left to perceive. In that objectless peace, one becomes established in the supreme.

**54. The qualities of things dissolved into bare existence.** Strip away, in contemplation, all the particular qualities of things — their colors, shapes, names, differences — until only bare existence remains, undifferentiated being with no distinctions. Resting in that featureless ground, awareness merges with the supreme.

**55. Awareness riding the held breath.** Let the breath be gently suspended, and in that pause turn the mind to the subtle energy poised within — neither rising nor falling, simply present. In the stillness of the held breath, the centered awareness dawns.

**56. Concentration at the point between the eyebrows.** Fix the attention at the subtle center between the eyebrows, with the mind one-pointed and the breath quiet, and let awareness gather there into a single point. As the concentration deepens, the inner light dawns and the supreme is revealed.

**57. The mind freed of its contents in an instant.** In a sudden movement, empty the mind of all its contents at once — drop every thought together, not one by one but all in a single release — and rest in the bare awareness that flashes open. In that abrupt emptying, the supreme shines.

**58. Steady gaze without blinking.** Rest the eyes on some point and hold the gaze steady, without blinking, without moving, until the usual flicker of seeing settles — and as the outer gaze grows still, turn the same stillness inward. In that motionless seeing, awareness rests in itself.

**59. The breath drawn in and held with attention upward.** Draw the breath in fully and hold it gently, directing the attention upward through the body toward the crown, riding the held breath into the open space above. In that suspended ascent, the boundless dawns.

**60. Resting between waking and the world's appearing.** In the first moment of waking, before the world and the day's identity have fully reassembled — in that bare instant of simply being awake with no story yet attached — rest in the open awareness. There, before the mind rebuilds its world, the supreme is briefly naked.

**61. Resting in the center between two things.** Take any two things — two breaths, two thoughts, two perceptions, two states — and let go of both at once, resting in the center between them where neither is held. Abandoning both simultaneously, reality shines forth in the middle.

*This is the master practice of centering stated in its most universal form, the verse the whole tradition returns to. The Tantrasara's path of will lives here: in the gap where both supports are released, the will leans into the open and thought drops away.*

**62. The mind restrained to a single awareness.** Restrain the mind to one object of awareness, casting all others aside, allowing no movement toward anything else — and then, in that gathered stillness, even let the single object fall away. In the one-pointedness that opens into no-pointedness, the centered state dawns.

**63. Awareness on the rising of any perception.** Watch closely the very first instant any perception arises — the bare moment a sound or sight or sensation first dawns, before the mind has named or judged it. Rest your attention right at that first flash of arising. There, at the birthplace of perception, the pure awareness is laid bare.

**64. The breath dissolving, the self dissolving.** As the breath grows fine and quiet and seems almost to dissolve, let the sense of a separate self dissolve along with it, until neither the breath nor the "I" can be found. In that double dissolving, what remains is the supreme, which was never the breath or the self to begin with.

**65. The energy of will at the first stir of desire.** Catch the very first stirring of will or desire, the bare impulse before it becomes a particular wish aimed at a particular thing — the pure energy of wanting before it has an object. Rest your attention in that first stir. There, the radiant power of will reveals itself as the divine.

*The Shiva Sutra named this: the power of will is the radiant Virgin, pure and untouched before it hardens into a particular wish. Here it becomes a practice — catch the will at its source, before it contracts into grasping.*

**66. The state before knower and known divide.** Contemplate the instant before awareness has split into a knower here and a thing known over there — the seamless condition before the great division into subject and object. Rest in that undivided ground. There, in the unity that precedes all separation, the supreme is recognized as one's own Self.

*This reaches into the very structure the Pratyabhijnahrdayam describes — the one Consciousness dividing itself into perceiver and perceived. Here you turn attention back to the moment before that split, and find the undivided Light that was always prior to it.*
