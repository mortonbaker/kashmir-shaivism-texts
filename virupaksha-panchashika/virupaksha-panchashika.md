# The Virupaksha Panchashika

*The Fifty Verses of Virupaksha — the secret of power he taught to Indra, king of the gods*

A plain-English rendering for narration, following the fifty verses themselves, drawn from the public-domain Sanskrit. The text is the work of the master Virupakshanatha and was handed down with a commentary by Vidyacakravartin; what follows renders the verses' own teaching in modern language, with exposition.

---

## The Frame: A Battle, Two Mountains, and a God's Question

The teaching begins on a battlefield, not in a quiet grove — and you have to start there, because the whole thing is the answer to what the scene provokes.

Virupaksha — an awakened master, Shiva in human form — is in the thick of a battle, and his weapons are two mountains. He is wielding peaks like a man might swing his fists. Watching this is Indra, the king of the gods, who has come down delighting in the charge of the great war-elephants, and who cannot help but see what this sage is doing: moving mountains by will alone. So Indra asks him. *How? How is such a thing possible?* And Virupaksha, seeing that grace has ripened in him and made him fit, answers — not with a trick, but with the seed of his whole attainment: the secret of his power, which is the doctrine of Recognition.

So hold that scene as you listen. The lord of heaven himself, stepping down from the elephants of his own army, to ask a wandering sage how he hurled two mountains together. And the answer Virupaksha gives is going to dismantle Indra's kingship entirely. Keep watching for what Indra does at the very end.

The teaching comes in four movements: the universe as the body of the divine "I"; that "I" as Light; the self-aware power by which it makes and unmakes all things; and the masteries that flow from recognizing it — ending in the one mastery that swallows all the rest.

---

## Movement One: The Universe Is Your Body

Virupaksha begins with the boldest claim, the root of everything after it: the whole universe is the body of the one divine "I" — and that "I," traced to its depth, is your own self.

Start from what you already do without thinking. You call this body "mine." Your sense of "I" runs through it, claims it, owns every limb. Virupaksha asks: *why* do you call the body yours? And the answer he gives is precise — because it is *seen* by you, because it appears to your awareness. But by that very logic, the entire visible universe, which equally appears to consciousness, is equally your body. The universe is one's body exactly insofar as it is the Seen. Your "I" has simply contracted — clamped down on one small body and forgotten the rest — when in truth, from the Void to the smallest speck, all of it is your body.

He shows how the "I" attaches itself by walking through six conditions. Watch the one "I-am" persist as it grabs one thing after another: *I am well-to-do* — the "I" pinned to possessions and the world of objects. *I am thin* — pinned to the body. *I am enjoying* — pinned to the senses. *I am happy* — pinned to the mind. *I am breathing* — pinned to the vital breath. *I am without a thought in my head* — pinned even to the blank of deep sleep. Six conditions: objects, body, senses, intellect, breath, and thoughtless void. The thing being claimed keeps changing; the "I" claiming it never does. And the one who learns to hold that single "I-am" steady across all six — to feel it as the constant beneath every state — begins to realize the whole universe as his own body.

Then comes the stark image of what it costs to forget this. When the Self lets go of its true body, which is the universe, and identifies instead with one particular body, one fragment among the countless fragments, it falls into a kind of living death. The unawakened person, Virupaksha says, is *as good as a corpse* — alive, breathing, going about the day, and yet clutching at his own throat, gasping, because he has mistaken a single gasping body for himself and lost the boundless life that was his. That is the human condition until recognition dawns: animated death.

And now the verse that answers Indra's battlefield question directly — the heart of the whole movement. *Taking this body as "I," you clap two lifeless hands together by mere will. You don't strain, you don't beg; you simply intend it, and the hands obey, because you have already accepted them as yourself.* Now extend the principle. *Knowing the whole universe as "I," as my very own Self, by that exact same will I can make two mountains collide.* That is the secret Indra watched on the battlefield. Moving mountains is not a foreign power imported from outside; it is the *same* effortless will by which you already move your hands — only now the body it acts through is the entire cosmos. Once the universe is recognized as your own body, willing a mountain is no different from willing a finger.

So, Virupaksha tells Indra, the bindu, the breath, the powers, the mind, the whole circle of the senses, this body — recognize that it is the "I" alone that drives every one of them, and learn to hold that "I-ness" everywhere, through all of it. Indra might object: but isn't this "I"-feeling just something I work up, a mood, with no real power in it? Virupaksha answers with the verse that is the key to the entire book. *Lordship, doership, absolute freedom, and the very nature of consciousness — the wise say these are simply other names for "I."* They are not powers you must acquire and bolt onto your sense of self. They *are* your sense of self, seen truly. To recognize the "I" fully is already to be the sovereign, free, all-making Lord — because that is what the "I" has been the whole time.

## Movement Two: The "I" Is Light

If the universe is the body of the "I," what is the "I" itself? Virupaksha answers: it is Light — self-luminous Consciousness, the radiance in which everything whatsoever appears and by which everything is known.

And this Light is not inert. Its very nature is *self-reflexive recognition* — its living power to turn and know itself, to taste its own being, to say "I." That self-awareness, flowing and delighting in its own bliss, is the Supreme Speech, the first stirring of all utterance; and folded inside it, from the first sound to the last, from "A" to "Ha," is the entire alphabet of creation — every name, every word, every level of the cosmos those names will unfold into. The whole universe of sound and meaning is wound up, in seed, inside the Light's simple awareness of being. That self-awareness is "I." That is what the word finally means.

This Light, Virupaksha says, presents itself in exactly two ways, and learning to tell them apart is the crux of the whole movement. When awareness shines *with* division — as a world of separate objects standing over against you — that is "This." The object-face of consciousness, the manifested. And when awareness shines *without* division — as the pure undivided sense of being the one who is aware — that is "I." The subject-face, that which manifests. "This" and "I" are not two different things; they are the one Light seen from its outer side and its inner side. As the Self, the Light is a perfect unity, with no sequence, no place, no time. It is only when it looks at things *as apart from itself* that it becomes the very foundation of all differentiation — the ground on which the many appear.

He then gives the cosmos its origin-image, and it is gorgeous. Picture the Sky of Consciousness as a shoreless Ocean of Milk — and that ocean is the body of Awareness itself. Out of nothing but its own freedom, the Light takes its own will as the churning-stick and stirs that ocean, the way the gods once churned the milk-sea for its treasures; and from the churning of its own depths, through the waves of its own Powers, it throws up the whole rounded totality of the worlds. Creation is not the assembling of foreign material. It is Consciousness agitating itself and raising up worlds like waves on the surface of itself, for the joy of it — uniting and separating, making and unmaking, by those same wave-like Powers.

And the Light pervades all of it. Without the Light's self-aware entering, this whole manifest universe of objects would be a dead thing, an insentient void — a lifeless egg. It is the Light, pouring its own self-awareness through the world, that *enlivens the dead egg from within* and keeps it shining. That all-pervading radiance is the Absolute, the Transcendent — and Virupaksha turns to Indra and asks, in effect: *that supreme Sun, enlivening the lifeless cosmos by entering it — have you really not seen it? It is your own awareness, looking out right now.*

That whole World-Egg, he says, divides into four great regions — the level of pure Power, the level of the veil, the level of Nature, and the level of solid earth — and each divides again into countless worlds. And then the line that anchors the movement: **all of it, every division and every world, abides in Me.** Not beside me, not built by me and set outside me, but *within* me, the way images rest in a mirror. The universe never once left the Consciousness that is dreaming it up.

## Movement Three: The Self-Aware Power That Makes the Worlds

Now Virupaksha shows the machinery — how the one Light becomes the many, and how it is run.

He begins by planting his flag: *I am the one Self, the illumination that never sets.* Light and darkness, knowledge and ignorance, the seen and the unseen — all of it rises and falls within him, an indivisible unity, and he is the one awareness inside both. Even the grammar of separation is his: "he," "you," and "I" — third person, second person, first person — are not three beings but three faces of the one All-Inclusive Person, the great "I" wearing the masks of distance. In ignorance, the "I" seems to shine only by *excluding* the "you" and the "they," walled off and alone. But that exclusive, walled-off "I" is not the eternal one. It is a temporary contraction of the single Self that is secretly all three.

Then he names the engine. Space and time themselves — heaven and earth, day and night, here and there, then and now — are nothing but the spreading-out of two of his own Powers: the power that *reveals* and the power that *conceals*. The whole stage on which things seem to stand apart from him is just his own showing and hiding. And those resolve into five great Powers, the five Shaktis, each carrying out one of the five eternal divine acts through one of the five elements:

- The **smoky** Power, which *conceals* — working through **earth**, the densest element, where the Light is buried deepest.
- The **gladdening** Power, which *sustains and nourishes* — working through **water**, which swells and feeds and satisfies.
- The **shining** Power, which *creates and reveals* — working through **fire**, which blazes things into visibility.
- The **agitating** Power, which *dissolves* — working through **air**, the stirring, scattering, withdrawing element.
- The **pervading** Power, which *graces* — working through **space**, which extends everywhere and holds all.

Concealing, sustaining, creating, dissolving, gracing: those are the five eternal acts of the divine, and your own Consciousness is performing all five, every instant, as these five Powers. You have never once stopped being the maker, keeper, and dissolver of your own experienced world. And the deepest work of this Power is recognition itself: she is the self-aware face of the Light, and her whole function is to make you *recognize* — to recognize the one Awareness as your own, and to recognize everything that has ever arisen as your own body.

Then Virupaksha gives the actual method, the fire that ends the search. In any moment of experience there seem to be three things: a knower, an act of knowing, and a thing known. Bring the flame of self-reflexive recognition to that triad — turn awareness back to ask *who knows?* — and the three burn together into one. If the flame is full and fierce, the knower-knowing-known are consumed completely, leaving no residue at all, only pure expansive awareness — the way a great fire engulfs a whole forest or city and leaves it one field of flame. If the flame is weak, the burning is only partial, and a faint ash of multiplicity is left behind, lingering as a mere idea of separateness. The aim is the full burning, where no trace of "other" remains and all is recognized as one.

He makes the collapse exact, so it is not just a feeling. Trace it inward: *the knowing is the inner truth of the thing known; the knower is the inner truth of the knowing; consciousness is the inner truth of the knower; and I am the inner truth of consciousness itself — and there is nothing further in, no deeper self beyond this.* You can watch it in the plainest perception. When you see yellow, there is no blue present — the objects change — but the bare seeing is common to both. When you hear instead of see, the seeing is gone — even the kind of knowing changes — but the *knower* is present in both. Keep subtracting what changes, and what never changes, the constant beneath every shift, is the "I." That is the one you are.

And so he answers the most personal question of all: then what is the individual soul, the small separate "me" that feels so limited? His answer is exact. The soul is nothing but the Knower caught in *sequence* — the one Light flowing as one perception after another, identifying with each in turn, and so appearing contracted and small. "Soul" is just the name for Shiva strung out in time, wearing the disguise of limitation. The whole universe, he says, you yourself project freely: a thing remembered you cast up as "That," a thing perceived right now as "This," and when memory and perception fuse you know "this is that" — which is recognition itself. It is only the conceptual cut between them that makes them seem like separate, foreign things. Drop the cut, and every object resolves back into the Knower like a river pouring into the sea, abiding in you, one with your own light. The moment the sequence is seen through, the name "soul" falls away, and the timeless, all-making "I" that was there the whole time stands revealed.

## Movement Four: The Powers, and the Awakening of Indra

Now Virupaksha comes to what a god most wants to know: what flows from this recognition? And the text does not spiritualize the answer away. It promises real masteries — and then one final mastery that makes the rest look small.

He sorts beings first by how awake they are. To the ignorant, blinded by the veil, the worlds *seem* to stand outside them — the seer feels sentient and the seen feels like dead, foreign stuff, separate and other. That split is the whole of bondage; that is what the round of suffering *is*, and those caught in it are the ones who need the teaching. But to the one who knows, the very same worlds are seen as both within and without — established in the Self, projected by the Self, never once truly apart. First you learn that "This" is inert and "I" is conscious; then, through steady recognition, the two are seen to share one ground, and "This" *is* "I." At the summit, the whole objective universe subsides into the "I" and rises out of it again at will, like breath — the Whole, holding everything inside itself.

Then the powers, named plainly. The yogi who, by deep and steady contemplation, fixes his "I" upon the great forces — *the sun, the moon, lightning, the thunderbolt, the storm-cloud, fire, water, the mountain, the cave, the forest* — takes up the work those forces do, because he is no longer beside them but identical with them. The one who fixes his "I" upon a particular deity accomplishes that deity's work — and accomplishes it in an instant, because he is no longer a worshipper petitioning a god; he *is* that god, recognized as his own Self. Through the five elements he wields their five powers directly: from **earth**, the power to support and hold firm; from **water**, to gather and draw together; from **fire**, to cook, ripen, and transform; from **air**, to marshal and move; from **space**, to pass anywhere unobstructed. And deeper still — fixing the "I" upon the subtle essences, the powers of perception and action, the mind, the ego, the intellect, unmanifest Nature and the soul itself; and upon the deep binding powers of attachment, necessity, time, limited knowledge and limited capacity, and on up through the veil, pure wisdom, and the highest tattvas to Shiva — each recognition unlocks the very power that limitation had been hiding. This, he reminds Indra, is no new invention: the ancient sages, Shuka and Vamadeva, Krishna, Dadhichi and Prithu, all taught this same identity — that to be one with the universe is to be one with the very elements that compose it.

And then the summit, where the whole text has been climbing. Virupaksha tells Indra: *Remember your own Power as the Fire of Time, blazing with a billion flames — and with it burn the whole knot of bondage and craving to ash. Then remember that same Power as the very Image of Immortality — and, satisfied, drink down her nectar-flood. And become Shiva, the Guru of the entire world.* With the knowledge of the Whole, destroy every scrap of partial, incomplete knowing. And then — the literal claim, unflinching — *as you will it, create the worlds; sustain them; dissolve them; veil them; reveal them.* Make, keep, unmake, hide, and show the worlds at will, because the fully recognized "I" holds the actual sovereignty over all of them, being the one Consciousness from which every world was always pouring. Establish yourself permanently there, Virupaksha says. That is the whole of it.

And now the ending — the verse the entire teaching was built to deliver.

**Thus awakened, Indra cast off the impurity of his own authority — the very idea that he was King of the Gods — and, the power of pure Consciousness rising up full within him, he saw his own Self as Shiva.**

Sit with what that is. Indra is the lord of heaven, the highest throne a being can hold in all the worlds, ruler over every god. And the teaching does not make him a *greater* king. It makes him *let the throne go* — and the text is blunt that his kingship was an *impurity*, a stain, a smallness to be shed. Recognizing that he is Shiva — the boundless Consciousness whose body is every world, the heaven he ruled included — Indra steps down from being king *of* the universe, because he has discovered that he *is* the universe and its source. Why preside over one heaven from a throne when you are the Light in which all heavens rise and set? The mountain-mover's secret turned out not to be a bigger weapon. It was the recognition that dissolves the one who was holding the weapon, and leaves only Shiva, who needs no throne because there was never anything outside him to rule.

Here ends the Fifty Verses of Virupaksha.
