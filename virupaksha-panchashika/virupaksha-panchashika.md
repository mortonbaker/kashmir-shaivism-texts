# The Virupaksha Panchashika

*The Fifty Verses of Virupaksha — the secret of power he taught to Indra, king of the gods*

A plain-English telling for narration. The shape of it, and every turn of the argument, follows the old commentary on these verses by Vidyacakravartin (the Trivandrum edition of 1910, public domain). His reasoning is all here — just put into ordinary, spoken language. Nothing has been added that the verses and that commentary don't say.

---

## The Battle and the Question

It starts on a battlefield, and you have to start there, because everything that follows is the answer to what happens on it.

Shiva — the Great Lord himself, whose one joy is to wake the world up — had taken on a human body and was walking the earth under the name Virupaksha. One day he came on Indra, the king of the gods, at the great battle of the war-elephants, and found him swollen with pride at being lord of heaven. So, right in front of him, with nothing but a flick of will, Virupaksha set two whole mountains fighting in the air. And at the sight of it, Indra's pride just collapsed into wonder. He asked the only question worth asking: *how?*

That question, the commentary says, is the tell. You only ask like that when grace has already touched you — the asking is the proof he's ready. And what Virupaksha gives him in answer isn't a lecture. It's an initiation, done through words. It comes in four movements: the whole world is your body; that "I" is light; the light is run by an awareness that makes and unmakes everything; and the powers that come of waking up to it — ending with the two greatest powers of all, and with Indra himself waking up. Watch, all the way through, for what Indra does at the very end.

---

## One: The Whole World Is Your Body

Start with the boldest claim, because the rest hangs on it: the whole universe is your body. Not a metaphor. Your body.

Here's the reasoning. Why do you call *this* body yours in the first place? For one reason — it shows up in your awareness. You see it, you feel it, it appears to you. Fine. But by that exact logic the whole world, which also shows up in your awareness, is just as much your body. So why does your sense of "I" feel so small, crammed into these few pounds of flesh? Because of a trick of attention. When you stare at one thing — this one patch of blue — your awareness seems to shrink down to the size of it. But that smallness isn't yours. It's borrowed from the thing you're looking at. Look at everything at once and you'll find your awareness has no edge at all — and how could it? The thing that lights up all of space and all of time can't itself be boxed inside space and time. So for the boundless light you actually are, the whole world, just by being seen, is your body. And notice the catch: if being-seen weren't enough to make something your body, then your own flesh wouldn't count either, because it's also just something you see. The only thing that ever made this body feel like *you* was that you claimed it. Claim the rest, and the rest is you too.

Now the obvious pushback: it isn't *seeing* that makes a thing mine — it's the feeling of "I." I feel the body as me; the world I feel as *that*, out there. True enough. So watch where the word "I" actually lands when you talk. *I'm doing well* — there's "I" landing on your stuff, your money, your house. *I'm thin* — landing on the body. *My eyes sting, I'm fond of her* — landing on a sense. *I'm happy* — landing on the mind. *I'm breathing* — landing on the breath. *I was out cold, blank* — landing even on the empty nothing of deep sleep, which is just Maya, the veil, the not-seeing. The "I" slides onto every one of them. Wherever it lands, that's "me"; and the moment it lets go of a thing — even your own hand — that thing stops feeling like you. So the lopsided picture, me in here and stuff out there, is nothing but a swindle worked by not paying attention.

But, you say, I only feel "I" in those few things — not in the mountains, not in the stars. Right — because that "I" was never the property of the small self in the first place. The small self blanks out every night in deep sleep; it can't be the source of anything. The "I" belongs to awareness itself, and awareness has no edge, so its "I" isn't stuck to a handful of things. It holds everywhere. Take it and hold it steady across the whole range, top to bottom, with a clear, fresh recognition — and you start to actually recognize the whole world as your body.

Then the hardest question, the one anybody honest would raise: if that were true, everyone would already be walking around as the cosmic body — and they obviously aren't. The "I" stays glued to the one body, and we feel it that way, plainly, directly. How do you argue with what a person plainly feels? Here's how. By not noticing yourself, you "leave" the cosmic body — not really leave it, just stop reflecting — and you plant your whole "I" in one small body, human or animal or god. And the result is a kind of living death. A person locked into one body like that is, in the verse's own words, as good as a corpse — breathing, walking around, going to work, and yet already a dead man with the rattle in his throat, because he's traded a boundless life for one small dying one. That living-death is real; the verse doesn't pretend otherwise. And the cause, when you actually look for it, is only Maya — which here means nothing fancier than *not reflecting on yourself*. Start reflecting, and everyone turns out to be the boundless Shiva, and always was.

And now the answer to what Indra saw on that field. When you clap your two hands, what actually makes it happen? Two things, and only two: you're certain the hands are yours, and you want them to move. That's the whole mechanism. No hidden machinery, no secret engine — just *these are mine* plus *now*. Well, says Virupaksha, I'm every bit as certain that the whole world is mine. So when I want two mountains to come together, they come together — by the very same move that brings your palms together. The only reason you can't do it is that your certainty stops at your skin. Your *that's me* is rock-solid for your hand and goes slack for the world. Tighten it all the way out to the edges of everything, and the same plain power that claps your hands moves mountains. The mountains were never a bigger muscle. They were a wider sense of "I."

One more objection, a fair one: hands don't move by wanting alone — there are nerves, there's breath, there's effort. Fine, says the answer — so do the same thing through all of that too. There's a quiet hum of "I" running underneath everything, before any particular thought rises. There's the breath that stirs the mind awake. There's the whole apparatus of intellect and ego, the part of you that judges and claims. The mind, the senses, the body. Run the "I" through every piece of it, the way you already run it through your hand, and the power is simply there.

And the doubt that closes it out: this "I"-feeling seems like something I just work up by imagining — it doesn't *feel* like lordship, like real power. The answer cuts the doubt at the root. Lordship, doership, total freedom, being-aware — these aren't extra powers you have to go get and bolt on. They're just other names for "I." Every greatness the scriptures ever praised is only this same "I"-ness wearing a grander word. So "I don't feel any power in my I" misses the point completely. The firmer the recognition gets, the more those powers — which were never missing for a second — simply show their faces. They weren't absent. They were unrecognized.

## Two: That "I" Is Light

So what is this "I," really? One self-shining light — the light everything appears in, and the light by which anything is ever known.

And it isn't a dead glow. Its whole nature is to know itself, to taste its own being, to say "I." It flows quietly, never dammed, always flashing. It's the deepest kind of speech there is — not spoken words, but the first inward ring of awareness being aware of itself. Take the first sound of the alphabet and the last, fold the whole of it in between, and you've spelled "I." Which is the point: this "I" is the entire alphabet of awareness rolled into a single note — not the little hiccup of "me" you usually notice.

What does "I" finally name, then? The light that can show both itself and everything else — the life inside a world of living minds and dead matter alike. Nothing can cut it, because the things that do the cutting, space and time, are themselves just more stuff it's showing. It's the one knower of everything knowable — so it can't be filed under "me" or "other," because those are things it knows. One, with no second thing anywhere around to draw a line on it.

Still, we seem to meet it in two modes, and telling them apart is the whole knack. Sometimes it shows up chopped into pieces — *this, and this, and this* — separate things walled off from each other. That's its outward face: the world. Sometimes it runs unbroken underneath — *I know this, and I also know that* — one continuous knower threading through. That's its inward face. Two faces, one light: the outside and the inside of the very same shining. And the chopping only ever happens in what's known; the light itself never actually breaks. Any sense that it breaks is on loan from the broken-up stuff it's carrying.

Here's the image to hold onto, because it comes back: the **screen**. On its own surface — call it the screen, the open sky of awareness with no edge — the light paints the world into being and wipes it away again, through the joining and parting of the waves of its own powers. Exactly like the sea. The sea humps itself up into waves and bubbles and then pulls them back down, and through the whole performance there was only ever the sea. Before the light reaches out to grab the world, there's just the blank open sky of awareness. When it wants to show something, its powers lean outward toward one thing — and that leaning-out is creation. When it draws the thing back into itself, those same powers fold inward — and that folding-in is dissolution. And the entire reason for the sea picture is to nail down one conviction: the waves, and the world they are, do not exist anywhere outside the light. No more than a wave exists outside water. ("Screen" is the old teacher's own word, by the way — not a modern dressing-up. It'll keep returning.)

Put it to work like this. Stare hard at the world and it's only ever three things: something enjoyed, the sense that enjoys it, and the one who enjoys — the seen, the seeing, the seer. Sit with awareness *as* exactly that, throwing off endless wheels of its own power. Doing that quietly kills two fears at once: the fear that there's far too much out there to ever hold, and the fear that any of it is separate from awareness in the first place. Watch closely enough and the thing seen, the sense, and the seer keep turning into one another in every single act of noticing.

Squeeze those three down to one. Call the enjoyed the moon, the senses the sun, the enjoyer the fire — then strip away their differences, and what's left is the bare shining they all have in common: the Point, the **bindu**, which is just awareness aware of itself. (Think of *bindu* as a single concentrated drop of awareness — the seed the whole spreading world of sound and form is only a swelling-up of.) That Point is the life of all of it. Every layer of word and world is only the Point, rippling.

Some people worship that Point as if it were many — sixteenfold, ninefold, and so on — each split chasing some particular power. But the manyness lives only in the props they use to worship; the Point itself can't be cut. And the ones who go at it in pieces like that are the beginners, the ones who haven't yet recognized their own nature. The ones who've actually arrived worship by sheer oneness — because they're already living free.

Now the Sun. A single spark of this self-aware light *is* the whole ladder of word and world — sound in three grades, from bare letter to charged syllable to full sentence, and world in three grades, from phase to level to realm. And this supreme Sun is the thing that lights up even the ordinary sun. Why call it a Sun? Because without it turning toward the world, the world is a dead egg — shut, dark, an eye that won't open. The Sun's turning-toward is what cracks the egg alive. And this Sun isn't hanging in the sky. It's in the space of your own heart. *Have you really never seen it?* — and that question is actually a denial: you have always seen it, you're seeing it this instant, because if you weren't, you couldn't see anything at all. That boundless light, looking for all the world as if it's been squeezed down inside one heart — that is you. That's the whole recognition, right there.

And the one who recognizes it is a world-maker. By its own freedom, the light churns the milky sea of awareness with the churning-stick of its own wanting, and up come the worlds on the waves of its powers — raised, the commentary is careful to say, on its own screen: a universe that was never for one moment anything but itself. That world-egg falls into four great bands — pure power at the top, then the veil, then nature, then solid earth — each one branching into countless worlds. And all of it rests in me. Not *rested*, past tense, as if it were done and over — *rests*, always, in the one light.

## Three: Burning Off the Last Trace of Separateness

One hairline crack is left in that last line. "All of it rests in *me*" still faintly sounds like a box and the things inside the box — a last whiff of two. This movement burns that off.

I'm the one light that never goes down, and I stand inside both the bright things and the dark — inside the intellect and the sun, which are bright, and inside the seed-point and Maya, which are dark — and every one of them stands inside me. Not light over here and things over there: the bright shines as bright, the dark shines as dark, and all of it shines inside the one light, so there's simply no room left for a box-and-contents split to hide in.

Even grammar folds in. *He, you, I* — three persons that exist by shutting each other out — and not one of them is outside me, the single All-Person. They rise and fall in me; they play out on my screen; all of them are me. Which means even that small walled-off "I" — the one that only manages to shine by shutting out "you" and "him" — is just one more cut-out figure, no more special than the other two. My real nature is the one boundless light. (Don't make the easy mistake of taking the little fenced-in "I" for the goal. It's just another figure on the screen. You are the screen.)

Space and time are the same story. High and low, day and night, here and there — they're nothing but the spreading-out of two of my powers: showing and hiding. The shining of everything and the hiding of everything are my two hands, and space and time are only the field those two hands play across. They don't exist outside me. Those two open into five. A smoky power that hides, working through earth. A gladdening power that holds things up, working through water. A shining power that creates, working through fire. A stirring power that dissolves, working through air. A pervading power that blesses, working through space. Hiding, holding, making, unmaking, blessing — five things your awareness is doing every single instant, as these five powers. You have never once stopped making, keeping, and dissolving your own world.

Now the fire, which is the working heart of this whole movement. The power that is self-awareness turns the whole world into your body by showing you that the light — the screen everything rises and sets on — is the one real thing left standing when all the knowing is done. So picture self-awareness as a flame. Turn it full force on the three — the knower, the knowing, and the known — and they all burn together into one, and what's left is no ash of separateness at all, just clean white light: the leftover that is pure being, untouched by any trouble, one bright ocean. That's the complete burning.

But — and the commentary really leans on this — if the flame is weak, the burning only goes halfway. When "I've realized it" still keeps a little residue stuck to the "I" side, the flame not yet hot enough to take it, then that leftover "I"-feeling glows on like an ember under a layer of ash: the separateness is *covered* but not dead — a little live coal of "me-versus-the-world" still breathing under there. That's the lesser arrival. Real, but with one spark left unburnt. Get the flame fierce, though, and then — watch. The whole world, completely taken by you, like a forest or a whole city swallowed in a single fire, throws off all its little differences, drowns them in itself, and goes to one thing: one dense field of light. And "watch" is meant literally — this isn't a theory, it shows up in your own experience if you look.

Here's the trace, done step by careful step so it isn't just a nice feeling. The real inside of any thing-known is the knowing of it. The real inside of the knowing is the knower. The inside of the knower is awareness. And the inside of awareness is simply "I" — and there is nothing further in than that. Take a pot. Before the thought "that's a pot" has even formed, you can't call the thing a pot or anything else at all — so the pot's own inside turns out to be just the knowing of it. The knowing only ever rises and sets on the knower, so the knowing's inside is the knower. The knower's inside is awareness. And awareness, once its very last ripple settles, is simply you. No other thing, anywhere up that whole line.

So what runs in sequence and what doesn't? I have no sequence. The knower-knowing-known runs in sequence. And awareness is both at once — in sequence when it faces the three, out of sequence when it faces me. Here's how a thing can be different and not-different at the same time. Say "that's a forest." Against the whole landscape, the forest is one thing; and against the forest, the trees are one thing too — all oneness. Now say "a mango and a magnolia." Against the forest, those two are still one, no difference there — but set the two against *each other* and they shut each other out, and right there is your difference. Everything works that way. Against the light, the whole and its parts have no difference at all; only set side by side do they take up their separate places. Difference is real between one figure and another. It is never real between any figure and the screen it's painted on.

You can catch it in plain seeing. When you're looking at yellow, there's no blue — the seen things take turns, one at a time. But the *seeing* is there for both yellow and blue (just not for hearing). And the one who *knows* runs straight through seeing and hearing both, never once breaking — that one is "like me," the part of you that never takes turns.

And now the pith of the whole thing — the commentary says so outright — and it turns on one tiny thing. Contemplate, without dressing it up in any thought: *I am That. The knowing is the Power. The known is the threesome.* And here's the hinge: it never says "I *become* That." There's no "become" anywhere in it. It just states it flat, as something already the case. Which means this oneness is not something you achieve. It's already finished. It always was. All the practice ever does is *recognize* it. That's the entire stance of this path in a single line. You are not building a union. You are dropping the not-noticing that hid a union which was never once broken. Recognition — not manufacture.

Then the last loose thread: so what is that little separate "me," the soul? Just the knower caught in the stream — sliding from one thought to the next, lighting up only one at a time — and right in that sliding it picks up the name "soul." But it's Shiva the entire time. "Soul" is a label that lasts exactly as long as the passing moment does. The sequence in it is borrowed from the objects streaming by; trace it back up and it's the sequenceless light, like me, even in the middle of a glance at a patch of yellow. The known sinks back into the knower the way a bubble slides back into the sea through its own wave — and that gives the line this whole movement was built for: *this very self-awareness is being Shiva.* Bright, unbroken awareness is what being awake means. The lack of it is what being a small bound creature means. The entire difference between free and bound is just that — awareness, or the lack of it. Nothing else at all.

So I throw the world up freely — as *that*, out of memory; as *this*, out of seeing; as *this is that*, in recognition — and I throw it up both ways at once, broken-apart and all-one: broken when the awareness goes slack, all-one when the awareness is full. And to anyone who wants to keep the brokenness as the final word, here's the catch: memory is the shine of a past thing, seeing is the shine of a present thing, recognition holds the two of them together — and that whole sequence can't even stand up without one unmoving awareness threading through it. Pull that thread out and even the "this and that" you wanted to keep falls apart into a painting with no canvas under it. There's the screen again, named from the other side: no screen, no painting. And so the things of the world, having flashed up as if they were separate, sink back when they're withdrawn and rest in me, one with my own shining — like a current resting in the sea.

## Four: The Powers, and How Indra Woke Up

So what comes of all this? The text doesn't wave the powers away as if they were beneath it. It lists them, ranks them, and saves two for last.

First it sorts people by how awake they are — dead asleep, nearly stirring, awake, nearly fully awake, fully awake. The worlds, even though they're stuck fast to my screen, look like they're *outside* to the ones who only see separateness — and that's Maya, the power that makes the seamless look split. To the ones further along, those same worlds look like they're *inside*, sorted out within — and that's clear knowing doing the sorting. (The screen again — and now it's the very thing the half-asleep are standing on without noticing.) And it pins down exactly what a bound soul is: the one who takes the seer to be alive and the seen to be some separate dead thing out there. That split is Maya itself, and the person fooled by it — his own nature unrecognized — is the one who needs teaching.

The text adds a hard, sober note here, and it shouldn't be hidden: this isn't for everyone. Hand it to someone whose whole way of thinking keeps awareness tangled up with its objects, and you don't help them — the teaching has to be kept back from the unready. It reads less like secrecy for its own sake and more like good sense: you don't hand a person a blade they'll only cut themselves on.

Then it climbs the stages, and names them. When you can hold the dead "this" and the living "I" together in one breath — point at the world and say "that's me" and actually mean it, sinking the difference down under the sameness, seeing the world as your own body — that's the stage the tradition calls **Ishvara**, the Lord. When the "this" sinks all the way down into the inner "I," held by self-awareness alone, that's the stage called **Sadashiva**. And when, in the full light, the world rises and sinks both at once — like waves winking out and in on a sea that is, in every moment, only ever sea — that's the very top, where the mind itself falls away. "Where the mind isn't" is all it means: simply resting in your own nature.

Now the powers themselves — the lesser ones first. The one who fixes his "I" firmly on the great forces, taking each as his own self — the sun, the moon, lightning, the thunderbolt, the storm-cloud, the fire under the sea, the ocean, a mountain, a cave, a forest — takes up the work that force does, because, being the world's own self, he already owns all the powers; recognition just switches them on. Fix the "I" on a god who does a certain job, and the job gets done — in a moment. Through the five elements you wield their five powers directly: from earth, the power to hold firm; from water, to gather; from fire, to cook and ripen; from air, to marshal and move; from space, to pass through anything unblocked. And it runs all the way up the ladder — the subtle senses, the mind, the ego, the intellect, unmanifest nature, the soul; then attachment, necessity, time, narrowed knowing and narrowed power, the veil, clear knowing, on up to Shiva — every rung of it, held as "I," handing over its work. The old sages did exactly this, it notes in passing — Shuka, Vamadeva, Krishna, Dadhichi, Prithu, all of them. And of this whole catalogue, the powers up through Maya are the lower set; the handful above them are higher.

And then the two that tower over everything else. The first: *Remember your own Power as the Fire at the End of Time, blazing with a billion flames — and burn the whole knot of your bondage clean to ash. Then remember that same Power as the very face of the deathless, raining nectar — and stand in the downpour until you're soaked through. And become the teacher of the world.* The burning is simply resting in the light, and it eats the entire chain of bondage. The drenching is the sheer wonder-taste of awareness, the bliss welling up and overflowing. And "teacher of the world" means just that — the *whole* world — because a person who has become the world's own body doesn't clean up one little private self; the whole universe comes clean through him. His teacherhood isn't running a parish. It's the universe washing itself through one human being.

The second is the one the commentary calls the heart of the entire teaching: *Lean your whole weight on the full knowing, and let it wipe out the half-knowing. Then make worlds as you please — keep them, dissolve them, hide them, show them.* There are only two kinds of knowing, it says — the full and the partial — and nothing else; throw your whole weight on the full one and the partial one gets pulled up by the roots, moment after moment. And here is the turn the whole book has been climbing toward: the very thoughts that used to wall you off from yourself, once you see straight through them, turn into the powers that run the worlds. The veils, seen through, *are* the throne. You can even feel where the teacher's own heart is — he taught the sun-and-moon powers almost with a shrug, and these last two with fire. So don't get stuck collecting the small powers. This is the thing.

And then the seal of it. *So, awakened at last, Indra threw off the impurity of his own crown — the standing thought "I am the king of the gods" — and, the full power of awareness rising up in him, saw his own self as Shiva.*

Don't slide past "the impurity of his crown." Indra's crown was his whole identity — ruler of heaven, the highest seat any being can hold — and the teaching calls that very identity the impurity, the shrinkage, the thing to be dropped. Waking up doesn't hand Indra a bigger throne. It has him let the throne fall. And "saw his own self as Shiva" is meant plainly and tenderly at the same time: Shiva was standing right there in front of him the whole time, as his teacher, as Virupaksha himself — because once you wake up like this there is no other Shiva to go find than the teacher in front of you, and not a hair's gap left between student and teacher. Indra knew it without a flicker of doubt: *I am the Lord himself, the teacher of this world.*

And that's where the Fifty end — not with a god made bigger, but with a god who found there had never been anything outside himself to rule.

---

*The commentary signs off calling itself a short companion to the verses, written by Vidyacakravartin for anyone who wants to recognize their own nature without a struggle — and ends, the way these always do, with a bow: to Shiva, to the teacher, to the one whose very being is joy.*
