# The Virupaksha Panchashika

*The Fifty Verses of Virupaksha — the secret of power he taught to Indra, king of the gods*

A plain-English rendering for narration. The exposition here follows the one classical commentary on this text — the *Vivriti* of Vidyacakravartin (edited by T. Ganapati Shastri, Trivandrum, 1910; public domain) — and is built only from the verses themselves and what that commentary actually argues. Where the commentary's reasoning is technical, it has been put into ordinary language; nothing has been added that the commentary does not say.

---

## The Frame: A Battle, Two Mountains, and a God's Question

The commentary opens by setting the scene, and it is worth telling exactly as Vidyacakravartin tells it, because the whole work is the answer it provokes.

Parama-Shiva, he says — the Great Lord, whose one delight is grace to the universe — had taken on a playful body and was wandering under the name Virupakshanatha. He came upon Indra, lord of the gods, at the great battle of the rutting war-elephants, and found him swollen with pride in his own supreme lordship. So, in front of Indra, by *mere will*, Virupaksha produced a battle of two great mountains — set two peaks fighting in the air. At the sight of that, the commentary says, Indra's conceit about his own power was pacified; and once it was pacified, for the sake of his awakening, Virupaksha brought down this teaching — the very essence, distilled, of all the doctrines of Consciousness.

Vidyacakravartin pauses on one word of the first verse: *asked*. Indra *asks* how it was done. The commentary says the question itself is the sign — the inferential mark — of a descent of grace already fallen on him; it is what shows he is now fit to receive the teaching of truth. And the word *tells* (Virupaksha "tells" him the secret) marks the beginning of what the tradition calls the Verbal Initiation — the initiation that happens through speech. So this is not a lecture. The commentary frames the entire poem as an initiation, given to a man whose pride has just been broken open by wonder.

The teaching has four movements, which the commentary calls the four *skandhas*: that the universe is one's own body; that this "I" is the one Light; the modes of self-aware reflection by which the leftover sense of separateness is dissolved; and the powers that follow from the recognition — ending in the two supreme attainments, and in Indra's awakening. The first verse and the last are the opening and the seal; one verse near the end, about the old sages, the commentary marks as a side-remark. The remaining verses number fifty — which is why the work is called the Fifty.

---

## Skandha One: The Universe Is Your Body

The commentary treats the second verse as a formal proof — a five-membered argument of the old logical kind — and it is worth following, because the boldness of the claim rests on it.

The thesis: this whole disputed thing — everything from the unmanifest Void down to solid earth — is my body. The reason: *because it is seen.* The example: just as your lump of a body is called "yours." Watch how it works. You, identifying yourself as a limited knower, call this body yours for one reason — it appears to your awareness, it is seen by you. The commentary presses on that reason. When does the knower seem small and bounded? Only when it faces a single particular object — this blue, this yellow. And in that moment, says Vidyacakravartin, the knower's own light *borrows* the limit of the object it is looking at; the limitation is on loan from the thing seen, not native to the seer. When awareness instead takes in everything at once, in general, it stands revealed as the unbounded Light of Consciousness — because that which lights up all space and time cannot itself be fenced in by space and time. And for that unbounded Light, the whole universe, being seen, is its body. The clincher: if being-seen weren't enough to make something your body, then your own lump of flesh wouldn't qualify either, since it too is merely something seen. So the moment the borrowed limit of "knowerhood" falls away, even for you the whole universe stands as your body.

An objection comes back: it isn't being-*seen* that makes a thing my body — it's being-felt-as-*I*. The lump is felt as "I"; the universe is felt as "this," out there. The third verse answers by walking through six everyday sayings, and the commentary names what each one secretly claims. *I am well-off* — the "I" reaching out and resting on objects, on property and field. *I am thin* — resting on the body. *I am tender-eyed*, fond — resting on a sense organ. *I am glad* — resting on the inner mind. *I am breathing* — resting on the vital air. *I have gone blank* — resting even on the empty nothing of deep sleep, which the commentary flatly identifies with Maya. In all six the "I" is found, by your own experience. So when "I" can reach out and land even on an object you'd call outside the body, then that object too, being seen, is body; and where the "I"-feeling withdraws, even body and senses stop being "yours." The lopsidedness you feared — "I" only in the lump, "this" everywhere else — is, in the commentary's word, a mere swindle worked by not paying attention. Failure to reflect is the only thing holding it in place.

But the "I," the objector says, only shows up in those six particular things, not everywhere. The fourth verse: that "I"-ness established in the six belongs to Consciousness, not to the small knower — for the small knower is itself sunk in the blank of the sleep-state. So if you take that Consciousness, whose "I"-ness is established in the six, and *hold it across the whole range* — from Shiva down to earth — with a firm, ever-pure recognition, then your own universe-bodiedness becomes something you actually recognize. Because the "I"-ness belongs to the unbounded Light, it cannot be confined to a few things; it holds everywhere. Recognize it everywhere, firmly, and the cosmos-as-your-body stands recognized.

Then the hard question, the one anybody would raise. If that's so, everyone would be walking around as the cosmic body — but they plainly aren't; the "I" stays nailed to the one lump, and that body-identification is something we *directly experience*. How can plain perception be talked out of? The fifth verse gives the stark image. Having "departed" from the universe-body — not really departed, but *as if* departed, through the single act of not reflecting on oneself — the Self plants a firm "I" in some one body, divine or human or animal, and the result is a world that is, in the verse's words, *as good as dead while still alive*, like a man with the breath rattling in his throat. The commentary does not soften it: this living-deadness is real, no denial. And the cause, when you actually examine it, is nothing but Maya — which the commentary here defines, precisely, as *not-reflecting-on-oneself*. Let reflection be regained, and everyone is the universe-bodied Shiva, and always was.

Now the verse that answers the battlefield. The sixth: just as, holding the body as "I," you bring your two hands together by *mere will* — so, holding the universe as "I," by that same mere will I bring two mountains together. The commentary makes the parallel exact. What is the cause of your hands clapping? Only two things — the firm conviction "I" in the body, and the wish. No third thing, no hidden machinery. And in the same way the only cause of the mountains colliding is the firm conviction "I" in the world, plus the wish. Therefore — and this is the whole diagnosis — it is *only from the slackness of your "I"-conviction in the world* that you lack this lordship. Tighten that conviction to the firmness you already have toward your hand, and the ever-accomplished lordship will surface in you too. The mountains were never a bigger muscle. They were a wider sense of "I."

A last objection in this movement: we never see hands move by will *alone* — there's always the apparatus, the nerves and breath and effort. The seventh verse answers by naming that apparatus and telling you to do the same thing through all of it: enter and animate the subtle pulse of awareness, the breath, the powers of intellect and ego, the mind, the circle of the senses, and the body — and hold the "I" through every one of them. The commentary glosses each term: the subtle pulse is the faint, self-flowing "I"-cognition that runs underneath before any particular knowing rises; the breath is what stirs the inner organ; the "powers" are intellect and ego, since their business is judging and self-claiming. Run the "I" through the whole set, the way you already run it through the body, and such powers stand ever-accomplished in you.

The movement closes by quieting a doubt: this "I"-feeling seems like something you merely work up by imagining, not something that actually carries lordship and doership in it. The eighth verse: lordship, doership, absolute freedom, being-Consciousness — the wise say these are simply *other names* for "I." Whatever excellence the scriptures call divine power, the commentary says, all of it is just "I"-ness; the grand words are synonyms, labels for the one thing. So the distrust — "but I don't *feel* any lordship in my I" — is answered: as the recognition firms, exactly so do the ever-accomplished powers of lordship and doership come into the open. They were never absent. They were unrecognized.

## Skandha Two: The "I" Is the One Light

The second movement asks what this "I" actually is, and answers: the one self-luminous Light — and it spends its strength showing that nothing, not the knower and not the known, stands outside that Light.

The ninth verse: this Consciousness, whose nature is reflective self-awareness, flowing of its own sweetness, is the Supreme Speech — and gathering its sounds from the first letter to the last, it is "I." The commentary explains the odd phrase. Consciousness here means the conscious being resting in its own nature with the stain of the object washed off; "flowing of its own sweetness" means ceaselessly flashing, because nothing dams it. It is called Speech because it is made of reflective awareness — and not the lower grades of speech but the Supreme, the inmost light-of-one's-own-being. And taking the first sound and the last — *a* and *ha* — and folding the whole alphabet between them, you get *aham*, "I." The point the commentary is careful about: this "I" is the entire alphabet of awareness gathered into one, not merely the little felt jolt of "I" that you ordinarily notice.

The tenth verse answers, then, what "I" really names: the Light able to manifest both itself and everything other than itself — the self of a world of conscious and unconscious things — and since the dividers of space and time cannot get a foothold in it, it is *one, without a second*, and the scriptures call it "I." The commentary's sharp move: the Light that manifests every knowable thing cannot itself be classed as "self" or "other," because those are knowables — and it is the knower of them. So it is simply one, with no second thing available to mark a boundary on it.

But, the eleventh verse grants, we do seem to meet two displays. The Light shows up "cut-off" — as *this*, one object walled off from the next — and "uncut" — as *I*, running continuously underneath. The commentary: the cut-off display ("this is blue, this is yellow") is the object-face, and without it the to-be-shown-ness of blue could not appear at all; the uncut display ("I know blue *and* yellow") is the subject-face, and that very continuity is the whole basis of the Light's power to show. Two faces, one Light — the outer and the inner side of the same shining.

The twelfth verse settles it: the Light is one by nature, not two — not Light-plus-a-separate-knower. The objection that "right now I am a knower, with a here and a now, in sequence" is answered: there is no sequence by space and time *in the Light*. Difference made by space and time lives only in the *known*. The Light is merely the ground on which that difference is displayed, and so the look of difference in it is borrowed from what it carries, not its own.

Then the great image, the thirteenth verse, and the word to hold onto is *screen*. On its own body — which the commentary calls the *screen*, the sky of Consciousness, unbounded — this Light creates and destroys the world through the joining and parting of the waves of its own Powers, exactly as the ocean throws up and pulls back its waves and foam. The commentary spells out the mechanics: before it reaches out to grasp the world, there is only the sky-like Consciousness; when it would manifest, its Powers face outward toward a single object — that joining is creation; when it reflects the object back in, those same Powers face inward and dissolve toward their source — that parting is withdrawal. And it tells you plainly why the ocean picture is used: to drive home the conviction that the waves, and the world they are, simply *do not exist outside the Light* — no more than a wave exists outside water. This is the same screen, the same wall on which the play of appearances is thrown, that runs through the deepest Trika texts; it is the author's own term, not a modern gloss, and it will return.

The fourteenth verse turns it into practice: the world, examined, is just the enjoyed, the instrument of enjoying, and the enjoyer — the five sense-fields and nothing more — so meditate on Consciousness as this, spreading endless wheels of its own Power. The commentary notes what that meditation cures: the fear that things are too many to hold, and the fear that they are something other than Consciousness. Both dissolve when you see the enjoyed object, the sense that takes it, and the enjoyer all turning into one another in every act of perception.

The fifteenth verse compresses the world to three and then to one: the enjoyed is "moon," the senses "sun," the enjoyer "fire" — and the bare common shining underneath all three, stripped of their differences, is the Point, the *bindu*, which is reflective awareness itself. The commentary adds that this Point is the very life of the sixfold path — the whole architecture of sound and world (which the next verses name) is only a modification of it. The sixteenth verse confirms that the world, gross or subtle, is always stuck to that Point and sinks back into it — nothing of it stands outside reflective Consciousness, like waves outside water again.

The seventeenth verse grants that some worship this Point as manifold — sixteenfold, ninefold, sixfold, threefold — for various powers; but, the commentary insists, the differences live in the *supports* used in worship, not in the Point itself, where no difference is possible. And it reads the verse's little word *will-accomplish* as a quiet verdict: it is the *limited* practitioners, who have not yet attained the recognition of their own nature, who worship by these divisions; the accomplished ones, whose single savor is living liberation, worship Consciousness by sheer unity alone.

The eighteenth and nineteenth verses are the Sun. A mere atom of the reflective awareness of this Light *is* the sixfold path of word and object — the threefold word of letter, mantra, and sentence, the threefold object of phase, principle, and world. And this supreme Sun, says the verse, enlivens the dead egg of the universe by its own entering — *have you not seen it?* The commentary unfolds the image: without the Sun's turning-toward, the cosmic egg is *dead*, merely shut, like an unblinking eye; the Sun enlivens it by the power of its own facing-toward; and this supreme Sun is found not in the outer sky but in the *heart-space*, the inner sky. The verse's "have you not seen it?" the commentary reads as a denial, across all three times, of any not-seeing: that Light is always seen by you in the heart, for if *it* weren't seen, nothing else could be seen at all. The unbounded Light, appearing as though hemmed into the heart — *that is you*. That is the recognition the whole movement was driving at.

The twentieth verse shows the recognized one as world-maker: churning the milk-ocean of the sky of Consciousness with the churning-stick of its own Will, by its own freedom, it raises up the egg of the worlds on the waves of its Powers — and, the commentary stresses, raises it *on its own screen*, manifesting a universe not different from itself. The twenty-first verse maps the egg's four great divisions — the level of pure Power, of Maya, of Nature, of Earth — each branching endlessly, and seals the movement: *all of it abides in Me.* The commentary catches the verb: it does not say "abode" in the past, because this abiding is of all three times — it always rests in the supreme Light.

## Skandha Three: The Reflection That Dissolves the Last Seam

The third movement exists, the commentary says, to make sure the recognized oneness isn't spoiled by a lingering doubt or a contrary habit of thought. One faint seam is left over from "all abides in Me" — it still sounds like a container and a thing contained, a last trace of two. This movement burns that off.

The twenty-second verse: I am the one never-setting Light, standing within both the lights and the darknesses — within the bright things (intellect, the senses, the sun) and the dark things (the subtle Point, Maya) alike — and they abide within me, the one. The commentary calls this an *interpenetration*: not Light over here and things over there, but the bright shining as bright and the dark shining as dark, all inside the single Light, so the leftover container-and-contained difference has nowhere to stand.

The twenty-third verse takes even grammar back: the three persons — he, you, I — though they mutually exclude one another, are not outside Me, the All-Person whose nature is reflective awareness; their making and unmaking depend on Me, and they appear *on My screen*, as one with Me. The screen again. The twenty-fourth verse adds the necessary correction: then the exclusive "I" — the one that shines only by shutting out "you" and "he" — is itself just a limited portion of Me, adventitious, like "you" or "he"; My real, eternal nature is the one unbounded mass of Light. The commentary is careful here, because it would be easy to mistake the small walled-off "I" for the goal. It isn't. It is one more cut-out figure; the Light is the field.

The twenty-fifth verse turns to space and time: heaven-and-earth, day-and-night — these are the spreading-out of My two Powers, the power that manifests and the power that conceals. The commentary ties it straight back to the ocean of verse thirteen: the shining of all things and their hiding are My two powers, and space and time are nothing but the field across which those two play. They do not exist outside Me. The twenty-sixth and twenty-seventh verses name the five Powers, their five acts, and their five elements: the smoky power that conceals, in earth; the gladdening power that sustains, in water; the shining power that creates, in fire; the stirring power that dissolves, in air; the pervading power that graces, in space. The commentary's note: by this, the ever-doing of the five cosmic acts — creating, sustaining, dissolving, concealing, gracing — by Consciousness is shown. You have never for an instant stopped performing all five.

The next four verses are the fire, and they are the practical heart of the movement. The twenty-eighth: the Power that is reflective awareness makes the whole into My body by showing that the Light — the screen on which everything rises and sets — is the real, residual nature of every knowable thing, the thing that is left, unsublatable, when all knowing is done. The twenty-ninth states the full burning: My reflective awareness is a flame that, burning the entire knower-knowing-known, leaves no residue but the white ash of pure Light. The commentary: the flame instantly consumes the *particular* portions — the knowerhood, the knownness — and what remains is the general Light, white because it is a mass of uncreated bliss, unstained by any affliction; the world becomes a single ocean of it. This is the complete burning, the kind the tradition associates with the highest, immediate path.

But — the thirtieth verse, and this is a distinction the commentary is keen on — if the flame is *not* fierce, the burning is only partial. When the "I have reflected" still leaves a residue clinging to the "I"-side, the flame's full burning-power not yet roused, then the knower's leftover "I"-cognition glows on like *ash over a live coal*: the difference is covered but not gone, a heap of cooling ash with a subtle ember still alive inside. That is the weaker attainment — real, but with a last spark of "I-versus-world" unburnt. The thirty-first verse gives the fierce case: then *see* — the whole world, utterly pervaded by Me, like a forest or a city wholly swallowed by fire, shakes off its variegated differences, sinks them in the Self, and takes on oneness, one dense mass of Light. The commentary notes the verse's "indeed": this is not theory, it is available in everyone's own experience.

The thirty-second verse traces the collapse precisely, so it isn't just a feeling: the inmost nature of the known is the knowing; the inmost nature of the knowing is the knower; the inmost nature of the knower is Consciousness; and the inmost of Consciousness is I, and there is nothing further in. The commentary works the example of a pot: before the thought "this is a pot" even arises, the pot cannot be pinned down as pot or as anything else — so the pot's own inward nature is just the knowing of it; the knowing, whose whole rising and setting hang on the knower, has the knower as its inmost; the knower's inmost is reflective Consciousness; and that, when its last reflective ripple sinks, is simply I. No other thing, anywhere along the line.

The thirty-third verse sorts what has sequence from what doesn't: I am sequenceless; the knower-knowing-known runs in sequence; Consciousness is both — sequenced toward the triad, sequenceless toward Me. And the commentary gives a homely picture for how difference is real and unreal at once. Say "this is a forest": against the whole, the forest is one continuous thing, and against the forest the trees are continuous too — so at that level, only oneness. But say "a mango and a *champaka* tree": against the forest they are still continuous, no difference there — yet set against each other they exclude one another, and *there* is difference. Just so: against the Light, the whole and its parts have no difference at all; only set side against side do they take their fitting separate places. Difference is real only between figure and figure — never between any figure and the screen.

The thirty-fourth verse illustrates it in bare perception: when you see yellow there is no blue — knowns exclude each other, so they run in sequence; but the *seeing* is common to yellow and blue, though not to hearing — so knowing is partly shared; and the *knower* runs continuously through seeing and hearing alike — so the knower is, in the verse's words, "like Me," the one that never breaks.

The thirty-fifth verse is, the commentary says outright, the pith — and it turns on a point of grammar that changes everything. Contemplate, *without fabrication*: I am He; the knowing is Power; the known is the triad. And the commentary notices that the verse supplies no verb — it does *not* say "I *become* He." It states identity as a standing fact, not a result to be produced. Therefore, Vidyacakravartin writes, this oneness is *not something to be achieved*; it is ever-accomplished, and the practice merely *recognizes* it. That is the entire stance of this path in one stroke. You are not building union. You are removing the inattention that hid a union already complete. Recognition, not manufacture.

The remaining verses of the movement clear away the natural follow-up: then what is the little separate soul? The thirty-sixth verse: the knower, flowing from one stream of thought to the next, shining exclusively, runs in sequence — and *then*, only then, gets the name "soul"; in truth, being Shiva, the name "soul" lasts no longer than that passing moment. The sequence in him is on loan from the object; trace him up and he is the sequenceless Light, like Me, even in the act of grasping a patch of yellow. The thirty-seventh verse watches the known dissolve into the knower the way a bubble sinks back into the sea through its wave — and then states the conclusion the whole movement was for: *this very reflective awareness is Shiva-hood; undimmed reflection is awakeness; the absence of reflection is the bound state.* The entire difference between the free and the bound is reflection versus its lack — nothing else.

The thirty-eighth verse: freely, I manifest the world as "That" out of memory, as "This" out of present experience, and as "This is That" in recognition — and I throw it up *both* as different and as non-different, the difference appearing when reflection slackens, the non-difference when reflection is done. The thirty-ninth verse turns it into an argument against anyone who would keep the differences ultimate: memory is the shining of a past thing, experience the shining of a present thing, recognition the two held together — and this whole sequence cannot stand without one sequenceless awareness that connects them all; deny that single connecting Light, and even the "this-and-that" you wanted to keep collapses into *a picture with no canvas to be painted on.* The screen, once more, named from the other side: take away the screen and the painting itself is impossible. The fortieth verse seals the movement: the set of objects, having shone as if separate, when withdrawn abides in Me, one with my shining, like a current in the ocean.

## Skandha Four: The Powers, and the Awakening of Indra

The last movement turns to what flows from the recognition — and it does not spiritualize the powers away; it lists them, ranks them, and then names two that stand above all the rest.

It begins by sorting beings into five, by how awake they are — the unawakened, the nearly-awake, the awakened, the nearly-fully-awake, and the fully awake. The forty-first verse: the host of worlds, *though stuck to My screen*, appears as if outside to those who perceive difference — the commentary names the difference-philosophers here — and this by the power of Maya, which makes the undivided look divided; while to those further along, the same worlds appear as inner, differentiated only within, by the power of pure wisdom. The screen is named yet again, and now as the very thing the unawake fail to notice they are standing on. The forty-second verse defines the bound soul exactly: the one who takes the grasper to be conscious and the grasped to be a separate, insentient *other* — that splitting is Maya, and the man deluded by it, his own nature unrecognized, is the soul who must be taught.

Here the commentary adds a hard, sober note that belongs to the text and shouldn't be hidden: this teaching is not for everyone. Those whose very framework keeps consciousness tangled up with its objects — it names certain rival schools — have, it says, *no qualification* to receive the doctrine of Consciousness, and it cites a scriptural warning of harm in teaching it wrongly or to the unready. The doctrine is to be kept concealed from the unprepared; that, it says, is the Lord's own command. I render it plainly because it is what the commentary says, though the spirit of it is less about secrecy for its own sake than about not handing a key to someone who would only cut themselves on it.

The next verses climb the stages by name. The forty-third: the awakened one knows "this is I" by holding the inert "this" and the conscious "I" in one breath, as co-referential, sinking the difference under an imposed sameness and seeing the world as if his own body — and the commentary names this the *Ishvara* stage. The forty-fourth: for the further-awakened, the "this" appears *submerged* in the inner "I" by the eye of reflective Power alone — the commentary names this the *Sadashiva* stage; and for the fully awake, in the full Light, the world appears *both* submerged and emergent at once, by the mind-transcending faculty, like waves winking out and in on an ocean that is, in every state, only ocean. That mind-transcending faculty, the commentary glosses with a scripture: it is "where mind is not" — the simple resting in one's own nature.

From there, the powers (the commentary calls them the *lower* attainments). The forty-fifth verse: the yogi who fixes his "I" firmly on the sun, the moon, lightning, the thunderbolt, the cloud, the submarine fire, the ocean, a mountain, a cave, a forest — and contemplates each as his own Self — carries out that thing's work, because, being the universe-self, all powers are ever-accomplished in him and only need firm recognition to become effective. The forty-sixth: fix the "I" on a deity who does a certain work, and that work comes to you *in a moment.* The forty-seventh names the five elements' five powers — earth supports, water gathers, fire cooks, air marshals, space lets pass unobstructed — all becoming the yogi's to wield once his "I" is their self. The forty-eighth and forty-ninth extend the same key up the whole ladder of principles — the subtle essences, the powers of sense and action, mind, ego, intellect, unmanifest Nature, the soul; and on up through attachment, necessity, time, limited knowledge and limited power, through Maya, pure wisdom, and the high principles to Shiva — each one, contemplated as "I," yielding its work. The fiftieth verse, which the commentary flags as a passing remark, points to the old sages — Shuka, Vamadeva, Krishna, Dadhichi, Prithu — who taught this same identity-with-the-universe through identity with the elements. The commentary adds a ranking: of all these, the powers up through Maya are the *lower* set; five higher ones, from pure wisdom upward, are the *higher* set.

And then the two that crown everything. The fifty-first verse: *Remember your Power as the Fire of Time, blazing with a billion flames, to burn the whole bondage; remember that same Power as the very Image of Immortality, raining nectar, and be drenched in the flood — and become the Guru of the world.* The commentary reads each image. The burning is resting in the Light, which consumes the entire sixfold path of bondage; the flooding is the wonder-savor of Consciousness, the welling of supreme bliss; and "become the Guru of the world" it reads with care — *of the world*, the whole of it, because the awakened one, being the universe-bodied, purifies not some private body but the entire cosmos. His guruhood is not lordship over a parish. It is the universe purifying itself through him.

The fifty-second verse is the one the commentary calls the essence of the entire teaching: *With firm absorption in the Full Knowing, destroy the incomplete knowing; and then, as you will — create the worlds, sustain, dissolve, conceal, and reveal them.* There are only two knowings, it says — the full and the incomplete — and nothing else; lean your whole weight on the full one and the incomplete one is uprooted moment by moment. And here is the turn that gathers up the whole book: the very mental constructs that *used* to veil your own nature, once recognized for what they are, become the powers that manifest supreme lordship — the worlds' making and keeping and dissolving done at will. The veils, seen through, are the throne. The commentary even notes a leaning in the master's tone: the earlier powers — sun, moon, the elements — were taught with a certain detachment, almost a shrug, while *these* last two are taught with ardor; from that, it says, you can tell where the teacher's own preference lies. Don't get attached to the lower powers; this is what matters.

And then the seal, the fifty-third verse, which the commentary unfolds slowly. *Thus awakened, Indra cast off the impurity of his own authority over the gods, and — the Power-principle risen full in him — saw his own Self as Shiva.* The commentary will not let the phrase "impurity of his authority" slip past. Indra's authority was his lordship over heaven, his standing conviction "I am the ruler of the gods" — and the commentary identifies that very conviction as *the contraction, the bound state*, the impurity to be shed. To become Shiva, Indra does not get a grander heaven. He lets the throne fall. And "saw his Self as Shiva" the commentary makes literal and tender: Shiva was standing right in front of him *as the teacher*, the venerable Virupakshanatha — for once a man is awakened this way, there is no other Shiva to be had than the guru before him, and no gap left between disciple and teacher, not the slightest degree. Indra realized, without a shadow of doubt: *I am the very Supreme Lord, the guru of this world.*

That is where the Fifty Verses end — not with a god made greater, but with a god who discovered there had never been anything outside himself to rule.

*Colophon. The commentary closes by naming itself: a brief Vivriti by the great devotee of the Great Lord, Vidyacakravartin — offered, it says, to those who would lay hold of the recognition of their own nature without strain. It ends, as such works do, with a bow: homage to Shiva, the guru, the God whose form is bliss, the Supreme.*
