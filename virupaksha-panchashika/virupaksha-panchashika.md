# The Virupaksha Panchashika

*The Fifty Verses of Virupaksha — how the sage awakened Indra, king of the gods*

A plain-English rendering for narration, following the original verses and the old commentary of Vidyacakravartin.

---

## The Frame: Virupaksha and Indra

This text is a teaching given to a god. The sage Virupaksha — an awakened master, a form taken by Shiva himself for the blessing of the world — was wandering one day along the shore of the Ganges. There he met Indra, the king of the gods, who had stepped down from his great elephant and approached him. And seeing that grace had ripened in Indra and made him fit to receive the highest truth, Virupaksha taught him the seed of his own attainment — the essence drawn from all the systems of knowledge, the doctrine of Recognition.

So as you listen, hold the scene in mind: the king of the gods himself, the lord of heaven, sitting at the feet of a wandering sage to be told who he really is. And at the very end of the teaching, you will see what Indra does with it.

The teaching has four parts: the universe as the body of the divine "I"; that "I" as Light; the powers by which it creates and dissolves all things; and the supernatural masteries — ending in the greatest mastery of all.

---

## Chapter One: The Universe Is Your Body

Virupaksha begins by establishing the boldest claim, the foundation of everything that follows: the entire universe is the body of the one divine "I" — and that "I," at its root, is your own self.

Think about what you already do without question. You call this body "mine." You say "I am" of it. Your sense of "I" pervades it, claims it, runs through every limb as its owner. Now Virupaksha asks Indra to see that the very same I-ness, traced to its true depth, pervades not just one body but the whole of existence — that the entire cosmos, seen rightly, is the body of the one Consciousness, exactly as your limbs are the body of your sense of self.

He proves it through how the "I" attaches itself. Look at all the things a person says "I am" of. **I am wealthy. I am thin. I have hands and feet. I am confused. I am breathing. I am empty.** Watch what is happening in those six statements. "I am wealthy" pins the "I" onto possessions and fortune. "I am thin" pins it onto the body. "I have hands and feet" pins it onto the senses and organs. "I am confused" pins it onto the mind. "I am breathing" pins it onto the vital breath. "I am empty" pins it onto the blank of deep sleep. In every case the one "I" reaches out and claims something as itself. The "I" is the constant; only the thing it grabs onto changes.

And here is the turn. If the "I" can claim the body as "mine" simply because the body appears to it, then by the very same logic the whole visible universe — which equally appears to consciousness — is equally "mine," equally the body of the "I." The ordinary person's "I" has simply contracted, clamping down on one small body and forgetting the rest. But the boundless Consciousness that you truly are has the whole of existence for its body.

Virupaksha drives the point home with a startling image. A person who has not recognized this is **like one who is alive but acts as though dead** — like someone with the breath still caught in their throat, present in a body but their true vast Self utterly forgotten. The whole world of unawakened beings, he says, lives in this living-death: animated, breathing, and yet asleep to what they are. What keeps them in it is nothing real — only *maya*, the power of self-veiling. The instant recognition dawns, the same person is seen for what they always were: Shiva, whose body is the universe.

So, he tells Indra, take that very same "I"-feeling you spread so naturally through your body, and learn to spread it everywhere — through all the levels of existence, through every world. Do that, and you will find that every power you could wish for was already yours, eternally, simply waiting to be recognized.

Indra might object: but this "I"-feeling is just a feeling I work up by imagining — it doesn't have real lordship and power in it. Virupaksha answers in the eighth verse, and the answer is the key to the whole book: **lordship, the power to act, absolute freedom, and the very nature of consciousness — these are all just other names for the "I."** They are not separate qualities you have to add to your sense of self. They *are* your sense of self, seen truly. To recognize the "I" fully is already to be the sovereign, free, all-creating Lord — because that is what the "I" has always been.

---

## Chapter Two: The "I" Is Light

If the universe is the body of the "I," what is that "I" made of? Virupaksha answers: it is Light — the self-luminous light of Consciousness, by which everything whatsoever is known and in which everything appears.

This is not the small "I" of the ego, the one defined against a "you" and a "they." That little "I" is just a contracted ripple. The true "I" is the pure Light that shines on its own, needing nothing else to reveal it — the awareness that illumines every object while itself being self-evident, the way the sun is seen by its own light and needs no second lamp held up to it. This Light is the single self of everything that exists, conscious and unconscious alike, the one radiance underlying the whole show.

And this Light is alive with its own self-awareness — its power to say "I." That self-awareness, Virupaksha says, is the whole alphabet of creation folded into a single point: it runs from the first sound, "A," to the last, "Ha," holding within it all the words, all the names, all the levels of the cosmos that those names unfold into. The entire universe of sound and meaning is wound up inside the Light's simple awareness of being.

He then gives the cosmos a vivid origin-image. The Light, out of nothing but its own free will, stirs itself — and from the churning of its own infinite ocean of consciousness it raises up the whole cosmic egg, the rounded totality of all the worlds, the way the gods once churned the milk-ocean to raise its treasures. Creation is not the assembling of foreign materials. It is Consciousness agitating its own depths and throwing up worlds like waves, on the surface of itself, for the joy of it.

And that whole cosmic egg, Virupaksha tells Indra, divides into four great regions — the level of pure Power, the level of illusion, the level of nature, and the level of solid earth — and the countless worlds within them. Then comes the line that holds the chapter together: **all of it, every division and every world, rests in Me.** Not "near me," not "made by me and set outside me," but *in* me, within the one Light, the way images rest in a mirror. The universe never left the Consciousness that is dreaming it.

---

## Chapter Three: The Five Powers That Make the Worlds

Now Virupaksha shows the machinery — how the one Light becomes the many worlds. The Light works through five named powers, five Shaktis, and they map onto the five elements of the world.

First he establishes that the whole of space and time is just the spreading-out of two of his own powers — the power that makes things appear and the power that conceals them. Day and night, the directions of space, the whole stage on which things seem to exist apart from him: these are nothing but his own showing and hiding, his own revealing and veiling. They have no independent reality.

Then he names the five powers directly:

- **Dhumavati**, the smoky one — the power of *concealment*, which works through **earth**, the densest, most opaque element, where the Light is most thoroughly hidden.
- **Hladini**, the gladdening one — the power of *nourishment* and refreshment, which works through **water**, that swells and feeds and satisfies.
- **Bhasvati**, the shining one — the power of *illumination*, which works through **fire**, that makes things visible and blazes them into appearance.
- **Spanda**, the throbbing one — the power of *vibration* and agitation, which works through **air**, the stirring, moving element.
- **Vibhvi**, the pervading one — the power of *pervasion*, which works through **space**, that extends everywhere and contains all.

By these five powers, he says, the whole world is pervaded and run. And notice what they amount to: appearing is creation, sustaining is maintenance, withdrawing is dissolution, concealing is the veiling, and pervading is grace. These are the five eternal acts of the divine — and the Consciousness that is your true self is performing all five, every moment, as these five powers. You have never stopped being the creator, sustainer, and destroyer of your own experienced world.

The rest of this chapter dissolves the last hiding place of separateness: the split between the knower, the knowing, and the known. Virupaksha shows that in any act of perception, the thing known, the knowing of it, and the knower are not three separate items but one Light wearing three faces — and traced inward, all three collapse into the single "I." When you see yellow, the yellow, the seeing, and the seer are one luminous event. The deepest, innermost truth of all of it is just "I am," the one Consciousness.

And he addresses the most personal question of all: then what is the individual soul, the *jiva*, the limited "me" that feels so separate and small? His answer is exact. The soul is Shiva — the very same boundless Light — only appearing in sequence, caught in the stream of one perception after another, identifying with each in turn and so seeming limited. "Soul" is just a temporary name for Shiva wearing the disguise of limitation. The moment the sequence is seen through, the name "soul" falls away, and what was always there — the timeless, all-creating "I" — stands revealed.

---

## Chapter Four: The Powers, and the Awakening of Indra

Now Virupaksha comes to what Indra, a god, would most want to know: what powers flow from this recognition? And the text does not flinch or spiritualize them away. It describes real, literal masteries.

He first sorts beings by how awake they are — from the wholly unawakened, who see the worlds as completely outside themselves; through the partly awakened; to the fully awakened, in whom the whole universe is known as nothing but the play of their own "I." The unawakened soul, still convinced the world is foreign and other, is the one who needs the teaching. The fully awakened has arrived.

Then the powers. The yogi who, through intense and steady identification, makes himself one with the great forces of nature — **the sun, the moon, lightning, the clouds, the submarine fire, the oceans, the mountains, the caves, the forests** — gains command over the work those forces do. By uniting his "I" with the sun, he wields what the sun wields; with the ocean, what the ocean wields. The yogi who merges his awareness with a particular deity accomplishes that deity's work — building up, dissolving, protecting, withdrawing — and does it, the text says, **in a single instant**, because he is no longer a petitioner asking the deity; he *is* the deity, recognized as his own Self.

Through mastery of the five elements he gains their five powers directly: from **earth**, the power to support and hold firm; from **water**, the power to dissolve and draw together; from **fire**, the power to cook, ripen, and transform; from **air**, the power to mass and marshal and move; from **space**, the power of unobstructed passage, to go anywhere unimpeded. The text names further masteries too, attained by identification with the subtle elements, the senses, the mind, and the deep powers of limited time, knowledge, and necessity — each recognition unlocking the power that was bound inside that limitation.

And then the greatest power of all, the summit of the whole text. Virupaksha tells Indra: become the **guru of the entire world**. Take your awareness, blazing now like the fire at the end of an age, and with it burn away the six-fold bondage that binds all beings; rain down the nectar-flood of grace that revives them. And — here is the literal claim — **create the worlds as you wish; sustain them; withdraw them.** Make, maintain, unmake, at will. The fully recognized "I" holds the sovereignty over all the worlds, the actual power to pour them out, hold them in being, and draw them back, because it has recognized itself as the one Consciousness from which every world was always arising. Establish yourself permanently in this, Virupaksha says, and this is the whole of the teaching.

And now the ending — the verse that gives the text its whole point.

**Thus instructed, Indra — the king of the gods — cast off his lordship over the heavens, and with all his powers awakened, saw himself as Shiva.**

Take in what that means. Indra is the lord of heaven, the highest seat a being can occupy in the cosmos, ruler of all the gods. And the teaching does not make him a *better* king. It makes him let the throne go entirely. Recognizing that he is Shiva — the boundless Consciousness whose body is every world, including the heaven he ruled — Indra steps down from being king *of* the universe, because he has discovered he *is* the universe and its source. The small sovereignty of a god over a realm is nothing beside the recognition that you are the Self of all realms. Why rule one heaven from a throne when you are the Light in which all heavens rise and fall?

And the old commentary adds the final, perfect note: once Indra was awakened, there was no longer any difference between the disciple and the teacher — between Indra and Virupaksha — for both now stood as the one Shiva, the sole guru of the world, looking out through what had seemed to be two. The teaching ends, as the whole tradition always ends, not with a god instructing a lesser being, but with the one Consciousness recognizing itself, and finding that the one who taught and the one who learned were never two at all.

Here ends the Fifty Verses of Virupaksha.
