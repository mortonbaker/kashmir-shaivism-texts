# Chapter Four (Supplementary): The Path of Knowledge — Complete

*This is the fuller, comprehensive version of Tantrasara Chapter Four, for reading rather than narration. It keeps everything in the audio version and restores the material that the audio version compresses out: the redefinition of the five ritual acts as inner contemplative acts, and the teaching of the four Powers and the twelve Kalis. Drawn from the public-domain Sanskrit of Abhinavagupta and the parallel Tantraloka material.*

---

## The core teaching (as in the audio version)

The path of knowledge — Shaktopaya, the way of Power — is the middle of the three methods. Its insight is that bondage is a thought. Living beings believe they are bound, and that very conviction is what binds them. So a counter-thought is raised — "I am Shiva, my true self is the whole Light of Consciousness" — and this liberating thought, cultivated steadily, shatters the binding one.

To the objection that this would make the Supreme itself merely a thought, the answer is precise: the liberating thought does only one job — it breaks the lingering residue of duality — and then it falls away on its own, leaving the self-luminous Reality that was never produced by thinking in the first place. The thought is a medicine that cures the disease and passes out of the body with it.

And the liberating thought only works when it is empowered by three things together: a genuine revealed tradition, a true teacher, and sound reasoning — *sadagama*, *sadguru*, and *sattarka*. Of these, right reasoning is itself the direct means; it is the very light of pure wisdom. This is why, on this path, the ordinary limbs of yoga — postures, breath-control, withdrawal of the senses, even meditation and absorption — are not the direct means. They work on the body, the breath, the objects of the mind. But the Self is not an object you reach by effort; there is nothing to add and nothing to remove. So reasoning, not technique, is the direct doorway here.

---

## The five acts, turned inward

Now the part the short version leaves out, and it is beautiful. Abhinavagupta takes the five great religious acts that an ordinary practitioner performs outwardly, and shows that on this path each becomes an inner act of recognition. The outer form is not the point; the inner contemplation is.

**Worship.** In ordinary religion, worship means offering flowers, incense, and gifts to a deity. Here, worship means offering *all things whatsoever* into the Lord — recognizing that every single thing already rests in the Supreme and has no separate existence apart from it. To worship is to lay the entire universe back into its source. (Pleasant outer offerings — flowers, scents — still have their place, the text notes, simply because beautiful things naturally help the mind enter awareness; but the real offering is the whole of existence, recognized as the Lord's.)

**Oblation.** In ordinary religion, oblation means casting an offering into the sacrificial fire. Here, the fire is Consciousness itself — the blazing awareness of the Supreme that delights in consuming all things — and the oblation is the dissolving of *every separate thing* into that fire, until nothing remains but the radiance of awareness. You burn the whole world of separateness in the fire of the Self, and what is left is pure light.

**Recitation.** In ordinary religion, recitation means repeating a sacred formula over and over. Here, recitation becomes the inward awareness, repeated and sustained, that this Supreme Reality is one's own essential nature — turning attention back, again and again, to the "I" that is the Light, independent of all the outer and inner objects that come and go.

**Observance.** In ordinary religion, an observance is a vow or discipline you keep. Here, the observance is to look upon everything — even the body, even a clay pot — as equal to the Lord, seeing the same Consciousness everywhere without exception. The scripture is quoted: *the supreme observance is seeing the sameness of all things.* That constant even vision, holding nothing as more or less divine than anything else, is the only vow that matters.

**Yoga.** The fifth act, yoga, is the linking contemplation itself — the sustained turning-toward and joining-with one's own real nature, the special thought that keeps the recognition steady until it becomes permanent.

So the entire apparatus of religious life is preserved, but transfigured: not abolished, not merely external, but turned into five forms of one inner recognition.

## The four Powers and the twelve Kalis

The fuller chapter also opens out the nature of the Supreme's Power, and this is dense, so take it slowly.

The Lord is the fullness of Consciousness, and that very fullness is his Power. The tradition gives this Power many names, each catching one of its aspects — Kula (the totality), strength, the Wave, the Heart, the Essence, Spanda (the throb), Glory, the Trident-Goddess, Kali, the Attractress, Chandi, Speech, Enjoyment, Vision, the Eternal. These are not many powers but many faces of the one Power, named differently so that contemplatives of different temperaments can each find a door.

This one Power is then seen as four Goddesses, by how she relates the world to Consciousness:

- **Para**, the Supreme — by whom the whole universe, from Shiva down to earth, is held, seen, and made to shine *as pure undivided Consciousness*, with no division at all.
- **Parapara**, the Supreme-yet-non-supreme — by whom the universe is held in *both* unity and difference at once, the way a reflection in a mirror is both one with the glass and distinct as an image.
- **Apara**, the non-supreme — by whom the universe is held *as difference*, things appearing separate from one another.
- And **Para again as the devouring Goddess** — Kalakarshini, the Attractress of Time, Matrisadbhava, the true being of all knowers — by whom all three of those modes are gathered back and swallowed into the Self, drawn home into pure awareness.

Each of these four acts in three phases — creation, maintenance, and dissolution — and so the one Power becomes **twelve**: the twelve Kalis, the twelve radiant energies of Consciousness. The text walks through them as twelve movements of a single act of perception: awareness first holds an object within, then projects it outward clearly, then takes delight in it, then withdraws it again, dissolving even the doubt that resists the withdrawal, until the knower recognizes *itself* as the one doing all of this — and finally rests, having drawn the whole cycle home into the Self. These twelve turn like a wheel, and that wheel is the inner life of the Goddess who is the Lord's own Consciousness. "Kalana," the root of the name Kali, means all at once: to move, to project, to know, to count, to take in, to express, and to dissolve into oneself — every one of these is what the Goddess does to the world at every instant.

This is offered, the text says, neither too openly nor too secretly — enough to point the contemplative toward it, with the rest to be found by deepening practice.

---

## The closing conviction

All of this returns to one point. The ordinary soul's certainty is: *I am inert, I am bound by my actions, I am impure, I am driven by forces outside me.* The whole of this path is the replacement of that certainty with its opposite, held just as firmly: *I am the Lord, my body is the universe, my self is Consciousness.* When that conviction becomes truly unshakable, one becomes, in that very instant, the Lord whose body is all that exists. And so the perfected yogi attends always to the strengthening of this true conviction, and is never shaken by the timid, partial vision of those who have not seen the real nature of things.

Here ends the supplementary reading of the fourth chapter of the Tantrasara, called The Path of Knowledge.
