# Chapter Five, Supplement: The Map of the Inner Ascent

*This expands the main chapter on the Path of the Individual. Read that one first. The main chapter gave the five methods — meditation, breath, inner sound, gesture, and external supports. This supplement opens up the detailed inner cartography that Abhinavagupta lays under those methods: what actually happens, stage by stage, as the breath and attention rise. It is precise and technical, and far more than the spoken version needs — but it is some of the most beautiful map-making in the whole book.*

## The Five Breaths

When the main chapter spoke of riding the upward current of the breath, it was pointing at a precise inner physiology. The vital energy is not one thing but moves in five named currents, and the ascent runs through them in order:

- **Prana**, the forward breath, by which the seeker first comes out of the silent void of the Heart toward the object.
- **Apana**, the downward, indrawing breath, imagined as a flooding moon, by which the seeker comes to see *everything* as filled with the Self, and so becomes free of craving for any particular "other."
- **Samana**, the equalizing breath, by which the seeker experiences a repose in the *union* of all objects, all of them held together and evened out.
- **Udana**, the upward breath, imagined as a rising fire, which *swallows* the whole machinery of knower-and-known — the entire restless counting and measuring of experience is consumed.
- **Vyana**, the pervasive breath, all-pervading and boundless, in which — once even the swallowing fire of udana grows quiet — the Self shines forth utterly free of every limitation.

So the breath is a ladder: out into the world (prana), drawing the world in (apana), unifying it (samana), consuming the very split between self and world (udana), and resting as the limitless (vyana).

## The Six Blisses, and the Seventh

These reposes along the breath are not dry technical stations. Each is a grade of *bliss* (ananda), and the tradition names six of them, rising from the void to the pervasive breath:

- **Nijananda** — the "innate bliss," the joy of resting in one's own self at the start, in the void of the Heart.
- **Nirananda** — the "bliss devoid," a spacious emptiness as attention opens outward.
- **Parananda** — the "higher bliss," tasted as the indrawing breath reveals the Self in all things.
- **Brahmananda** — the "vast bliss," the fullness of all objects held together.
- **Mahananda** — the "great bliss," as the fire consumes the knower-known divide.
- **Cidananda** — the "bliss of pure Consciousness," awareness resting in itself, free of limitation.

And then a seventh, which is not a rung above the others but their crown and unification — **Jagadananda**, the "bliss of the world." This is the one that has no rising and no setting, no coming and going. It is not a state you enter and leave; it is the unbroken inner repose that holds all six of the others together at once. In Jagadananda the whole world *is* the bliss — not bliss found by escaping the world, but the world itself recognized as the play of one's own blissful Consciousness. This is the true summit the breath-ladder was climbing toward.

## The Five States Within Each Repose

Abhinavagupta then adds another layer of fineness. Within each of these reposes, depending on how deeply the seeker enters, five conditions can arise in sequence — the body's own honest signals that the grip of separateness is loosening:

1. **Bliss** (ananda) — first contact with the touch of Fullness.
2. **Rising** (udbhava) — a leap or springing-up, as the seeker becomes established, for an instant, in bodilessness, no longer weighed to the flesh.
3. **Tremor** (kampa) — a trembling or quaking, as identification with the body relaxes under the rising Power.
4. **Sleep** (nidra) — a sleep-like drawing-in, as outward-facing attention dissolves.
5. **Whirling** (ghurni) — a whirling or reeling, the vertigo of the great pervasion dawning, as the false self melts into the not-self and the not-self in turn melts into the Self, because the Self is now found to be everything.

These five are worth understanding because a seeker who does not know the map can be frightened by them — the trembling, the sleep-like states, the reeling — and mistake the signs of dissolving limitation for something going wrong. They are, in fact, the threshold being crossed.

## Waking to Beyond-the-Fourth

These same stages can be cross-mapped onto the classical four states of consciousness and the state beyond them: waking, dreaming, deep sleep, the Fourth (turya), and the state beyond the Fourth (turyatita). The inner ascent is, from this angle, a journey from ordinary waking awareness all the way to that final condition beyond even the witnessing Fourth — the condition in which there is nothing left outside the Self to witness.

## The Rising Power and Her Centers

This entire ascent is, in the body's symbolic language, the rising of the coiled Power — Kundalini — upward through a series of centers. Abhinavagupta names them as the points where these stages occur: the triangle at the base, the bulb or *kanda*, the heart, the palate, and on upward. The breath-ladder, the blisses, and the five states are all happening *along* this upward axis as the Power uncoils and climbs toward its own source at the crown.

## The Three Marks, and the Heart of the Goddess

Finally, in the repose at the top of this upward movement, the tradition speaks of "three marks" or three *lingas* — three modes of the supreme Vibration, distinguished by what has happened to the world of objects:

- the Vibration in which all objects have **completely dissolved** away,
- the Vibration in which objects are just **beginning to appear**,
- and the Vibration in which objects **have fully appeared**.

These three are three faces of the one pulsing Reality, depending on whether the world is withdrawn, dawning, or unfolded. And above and within all three is what the tradition calls the supreme mark — the **Heart of the Yogini**: the pure throbbing of Consciousness itself, contracting and expanding, where Shiva and Shakti are united as a single Pair and the seeker comes to rest in the creative Emission from which all worlds pour. Here, Abhinavagupta says, the deepest penetration is something that cannot be set down in words at all. He marks the spot and falls silent — and so will we.

The essential gift of the chapter remains the simple one given in the main version: every limited handhold, rightly used, carries the limited self up to the unlimited. This supplement only shows how carefully the old masters had charted the climb.
