# Chapter Four, Supplement: The Five Acts Made Inner, and the Powers of Consciousness

*This expands the main chapter on the Path of Knowledge. Read that one first. What follows is the fuller material Abhinavagupta includes — vivid, but more than the spoken version needs.*

In the main chapter we saw the heart of the Path of Knowledge: bondage is a thought, a liberating thought breaks it, and that thought must be empowered by genuine tradition, a true teacher, and sound reasoning. Abhinavagupta then does something beautiful with the rest of the chapter. He takes the five great acts of religious life — the things people normally do with flowers and fire and muttered syllables and vows — and turns every one of them inward, into a movement of awareness. Nothing is thrown away; everything is relocated to where it actually does its work, inside consciousness.

## The Five Acts, Turned Inward

Here are the five, as he reinterprets them.

**Worship** — normally the offering of flowers, incense, water to a deity. Inwardly, worship means this: since every single thing rests in the Lord and nothing stands outside him, true worship is the offering of *all things whatsoever* into the Lord. You stop sorting the world into sacred objects to be offered and ordinary objects to be ignored, and you let the whole of experience be the offering. (Abhinavagupta adds a gentle, practical note: outward flowers and incense still have their place, because some pleasant things slip into awareness easily and naturally, and offering those is an easy way in. The outer act is a doorway, not the thing itself.)

**Oblation** — normally the pouring of offerings into a sacrificial fire. Inwardly, oblation means dissolving the whole world in the fire of Consciousness. You take the conviction "everything is made of the Lord's own radiance," and in that blaze of awareness — which delights in consuming all things — every separate thing is burned down until only the radiance is left. The fire is your own consciousness; the offering is the apparent separateness of everything; what remains when the burning is done is pure light.

**Recitation** — normally the repetition of a mantra. Inwardly, recitation means the continuous inner awareness that *this supreme Reality is my own very self* — turned over and over, not as outer sound but as the mind's steady return to its own nature, independent of any external object inner or outer. The repeated syllable becomes the repeated recognition.

**Observance** — normally a vow, a discipline, a religious restriction. Inwardly, the supreme observance is to look upon everything — even the body, even a clay pot — as equal to the Lord, with the settled conviction of the Lord's presence everywhere, until you can find the Lord effortlessly, in any place, with no technique at all. As an old scripture he quotes puts it: *seeing the sameness of all things is the highest vow.* The discipline is not abstaining from this or that; it is refusing to see anything as less than divine.

**Yoga** — the fifth, which links the others. Yoga here is the particular contemplation that joins you back to your own nature: the sustained inner act of re-cognizing the spontaneous, self-luminous Reality and settling permanently into the fact that it is simply, eternally, shining — that this is all it ever was.

Notice the pattern. In every case the outer act is not condemned; it is fulfilled by being understood for what it always pointed at. The flowers were always the world; the fire was always consciousness; the mantra was always self-recognition; the vow was always the vision of sameness. The Path of Knowledge doesn't abolish religious life — it reveals what religious life was secretly doing all along.

## A Word on Purity

Abhinavagupta pauses here to make a pointed argument, one that matters to the whole tantric outlook. Ritual systems obsess over purity and impurity — what may be eaten, what may be touched, what defiles. He says plainly: purity is not an intrinsic property of any object, the way blueness is a property of a blue thing. A thing is not pure or impure in itself. So the seeker on this path should not torment themselves with endless calculations of clean and unclean, which are products of mere convention and imagination, stripped of any real nature. What overrides all such rules is the single recognition that everything is the Lord. That recognition is the only purity, and beside it the rule-books — Vedic, tantric, every variety — have, as he says, essentially no force here at all.

## The Four Powers and the Twelve

Then the chapter turns to the powers of Consciousness itself, and this is the densest material — included here for completeness rather than for the audiobook.

The Lord is full Consciousness, and his fullness is his Power, called by many names in the scriptures — Heart, Essence, Vibration, Glory, and many more — each name catching one face of the single fullness, so that meditators of different temperaments can each find a way in. These powers are, strictly, countless: in the end, *everything that exists is one of his powers.* But the whole of them can be gathered into four.

- **The Supreme Power** (Para): by her the Lord holds, sees, and illumines the entire universe, from Shiva down to earth, as nothing but undivided Consciousness — pure unity.
- **The Supreme-and-non-Supreme Power** (Parapara): by her he holds the universe in unity-and-difference together, both at once, the way a reflection both is and isn't the mirror.
- **The non-Supreme Power** (Apara): by her he holds the universe as sheer difference, each thing distinct and cut off from each other thing.
- And the **Supreme Power again as the devouring one** (named Kali, Matrsadbhava, "she who draws in time"): by her he takes all three of the above and gathers them back into himself, withdrawing the whole display into his own awareness.

Each of these four, by the Lord's freedom, operates in three modes — creation, maintenance, and dissolution — and four times three gives **twelve** powers. Abhinavagupta walks through all twelve as twelve successive "graspings" by which Consciousness brings a thing inward, holds it forth, savors it, draws it back, and finally rests it in pure awareness — the twelve Kalis, the wheel of energies revolving in every single act of perception, whether you are watching the months and seasons turn outside or simply seeing a pot in front of you. The same twelve-fold pulse runs the cosmos and runs the glance of an eye.

This is as far inward as the chapter goes, and Abhinavagupta himself notes that such things should be neither fully broadcast nor fully hidden — pointed at, for those ready to follow. For the purposes of practice, the essential gift of the chapter remains the simpler one: the binding thought is broken by the liberating thought, and the whole of religious life, rightly understood, is that liberating recognition carried into every act.
