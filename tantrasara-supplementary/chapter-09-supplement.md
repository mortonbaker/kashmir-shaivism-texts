# Chapter Nine, Supplement: The Descent of the Thirty-Six

*The main chapter nine renders what Abhinavagupta actually argues there — that the "division of the categories" is really the division of the knowers, the seven grades of perceiver from the sleepwalking soul up to Shiva. But there is a companion question that every student of this system needs answered plainly: what ARE the thirty-six categories, walked through from top to bottom? This supplement gives that descent — the standard map of how boundless Consciousness step by step disguises itself as a small, limited person in a world. Read it as background to the whole Tantrasara, not as a rendering of chapter nine's specific argument.*

There are thirty-six of these categories. They fall into three great stretches: the pure heights, where the Light still knows itself as the Light; the middle band, where the disguise is put on; and the lower reaches, where the disguise has hardened into an ordinary world of minds and bodies and things. Let me walk down the whole staircase.

## The Pure Heights

At the very top is pure Consciousness, undivided, knowing itself perfectly — the Light, here called Shiva, with its own living self-awareness, its Power, inseparable from it. This is the summit, where there is no separation at all between the knower and the known, because there is as yet nothing else to know. Awareness rests in its own fullness.

Just below, the Light begins, ever so gently, to lean toward expression. It says, in effect, "I am this — I will be a universe." At first the emphasis is all on the "I," the universe still held inside as a barely-stirring intention. Then the emphasis shifts toward the "this," the universe leaning outward, becoming more vivid, though still entirely transparent to its source. Through these first steps the Light remains undeceived. It is expanding, taking on the colors of manifestation, but it never once forgets that all of it is itself. The knower and the known are still one being, looked at from two sides. These are the pure heights, and a soul established here is essentially free.

## The Great Veil

And then comes the decisive step, the hinge of the entire cosmos. The Light, out of its own absolute freedom and for the sheer play of it, chooses to forget. It draws a veil across its own infinity. The tradition calls this veil *maya*.

Do not picture *maya* as a cosmic mistake or an evil force. It is the Light's own power of self-concealment, freely exercised — the same power a great actor uses to disappear so completely into a role that, for the length of the play, even she forgets she is acting. *maya* is Consciousness pretending not to be Consciousness, and pretending so well that it convinces itself. Everything below this veil is the world as we ordinarily know it: a place of separate things and limited selves, all of them in truth the one Light, none of them remembering it.

## The Five Sheaths

The instant the veil falls, the boundless Self is wrapped in five tight sheaths, and these five are worth knowing by heart, because they are the exact shape of every limitation you have ever felt. Each one takes an infinite attribute of Consciousness and shrinks it to a sliver. The infinite is not destroyed; it is throttled down to a trickle, and the trickle is what we call a human capacity.

The first sheath takes the Light's infinite power to do anything and narrows it to the ability to do only some things. Boundless creative power becomes a pair of working hands. This is limited agency.

The second takes the Light's infinite knowing — which knows everything at once — and narrows it to knowing only a little, only this and not that. Omniscience becomes the small flashlight beam of ordinary attention. This is limited knowledge.

The third is the strangest and most poignant. The Light is perfectly full, lacking nothing, wanting for nothing. The third sheath takes that fullness and replaces it with desire — the gnawing sense that something is missing and must be pursued. Perfect contentment becomes endless wanting. This is attachment, and it is the engine of almost all human restlessness: the infinite, having forgotten it already holds everything, now chases scraps.

The fourth takes the Light's eternity, its timeless present, and slices it into past, present, and future — installs the clock, starts the countdown, introduces aging and deadline and the dread of the end. Eternity becomes time.

And the fifth takes the Light's absolute freedom, which is bound by nothing, and binds it to the iron rule of cause and consequence — this action must bring that result, this deed must be paid for by that fate. Boundless freedom becomes the law of necessity, the sense of being hemmed in by rules you did not choose. This is restriction.

Limited doing, limited knowing, restless wanting, ticking time, binding law: wrap the infinite in those five, and you have manufactured a soul that feels small. That bundled-up, contracted Self — the infinite Light now experiencing itself as a particular limited person — is the next category. It is the individual soul, the *purusha*: not a different being from Shiva, but Shiva with the five sheaths on, Shiva who has forgotten, the actor lost in the role.

## The World of the Soul

Facing that limited soul, the veil also produces its counterpart — undifferentiated nature, the raw stuff out of which all experienced objects and inner states will be churned. From this primal nature unfold the instruments by which the soul lives its narrow life.

First the inner instruments: the intellect, the mirror in which experience is judged and decided; the sense of being a separate "I," the ego that claims each experience as its own; and the mind that reaches out, sorts, and stitches impressions together. These three are the machinery of a private inner life — the apparatus that makes you feel like a single person looking out at a world.

Then the powers of perception — hearing, touch, sight, taste, smell — the five gateways through which the world comes in. Then the powers of action — speaking, handling, moving, and the rest — the channels through which the soul pushes back out into the world. Then the subtle essences behind what the senses grasp: sound itself, texture itself, color and form, flavor, scent — the pure qualities that perception drinks. And finally, at the very bottom of the staircase, the five great elements that build the visible world: space, air, fire, water, and at the lowest rung of all, solid earth — the densest, most thoroughly disguised form the Light can take, so opaque that almost no one looking at a stone would ever guess it is made of the same Consciousness that is reading these words.

## The Staircase Runs Both Ways

That is the whole descent: from pure self-knowing Light, through the veil of forgetting, through the five throttling sheaths, into a limited soul, and out into the full furniture of mind, senses, and elements. Thirty-six steps from God to ground.

And here is why such an inventory belongs in a book about freedom. The map runs both ways. Read downward, it explains exactly how you came to feel like a small, wanting, time-bound, rule-bound creature stranded in a world of dead matter — every layer of that feeling is accounted for, named, located. But read upward, the very same map is a staircase home. Every limitation, traced back, reveals the infinite attribute it was carved from. Your small power is throttled infinite power. Your flashlight knowing is throttled omniscience. Your restless wanting is the memory-trace of a fullness you never actually lost. Your fear of time is eternity wearing a watch.

So when the seeker meditates on these categories, they are not studying cosmology. They are loosening the sheaths one at a time, climbing back up the staircase rung by rung, until they stand once more at the top, in the pure heights, and recognize that the whole long descent was something they freely did to themselves, for the joy of the play — and can, at any moment, undo.
