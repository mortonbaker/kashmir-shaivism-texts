# Chapter Six, Supplement: The Calendar Hidden in the Breath

*This expands the main chapter on the Path of Time. Read that one first. The main chapter explained the vision — time is the Lord's own action, folded into the breath, and "swallowing time" (kala-grasa) returns you to the timeless. This supplement gives the actual machinery the chapter lays out: the precise way the whole of time — seconds, days, months, years, ages — is mapped onto a single breath, and the argument by which watching that breath collapses time into thoughtless awareness. It is technical, and far more than the spoken version needs.*

A word first. Abhinavagupta here compresses a much longer treatment from the Tantraloka, and Jayaratha's commentary spends many pages on what the root text states in a few lines. What follows is the structure in plain terms — enough to understand what is being described, not a substitute for the living instruction this kind of breath-work requires.

## The Setup: Time Is the Power Called Kali, Living in the Breath

The chapter belongs to the larger scheme of the six "courses" (the *adhvas* — sound, mantra, word, segment, principle, world), all of which are said to live in the vital breath. Of these, the path of *Time* governs the first three (the subtle, sound-side courses), and it rests in the forward part of the Power of Action; the path of *Space* (next chapter) governs the rest.

Time itself is defined precisely: it is the power of effecting things *in sequence* (or out of sequence). Seen in its source, inside the Supreme Lord, that power is the Goddess **Kali** — she who measures out and withdraws. Seen in duality, it shows up as nothing more exotic than the movement of your breath. So the breath is where the whole of time can actually be found and worked with.

Three things drive the breath's movement, in descending order of importance: the Power of the Lord, the power of the limited individual, and effort. And the breath makes its clearly-felt movement "from the heart" outward — which is why it can be investigated at all.

## The Measure: The Whole Calendar on a 36-Finger Breath

Here is the concrete machinery, and it is remarkable. The movement of the breath — out in exhalation, in during inhalation, from the heart to the point about twelve fingers beyond the nose (the *dvadashanta*) — spans **thirty-six fingers' breadth**, measured in each being by its own fingers (so it scales from Brahma down to a worm). Onto that single span, the entire structure of time is mapped:

- A **chashaka** (about 24 seconds) occupies roughly one and a fifth fingers.
- Sixty chashakas make a **ghatika** (about 24 minutes) — seventy-two fingers.
- A **tuti** here is a measure of two and a quarter fingers; four tutis make a *prahara* (three hours). (Note: in this chapter the *tuti* is a unit of **time**. The Tantrasara uses the same word differently in chapter nine, where it measures the inner beats of a single act of perception — don't confuse the two.)
- One full exhalation is a **day**; one inhalation, a **night**.
- The fifteen middle tutis of the cycle become the fifteen lunar days of a **fortnight**, with a half-tuti of immeasurable repose at each end. Day is the shining-forth of the object; night is its dissolution back into the knower.
- The dark and bright fortnights, with the moon of *apana* and the sun of *prana* exchanging their phases, make a **month**; the eclipse and the juncture of fortnights fall at the turns.
- The northern and southern courses of the sun (the **solstices**) map onto exhalation and inhalation across the finger-span, building up the **year** — and Abhinavagupta even threads the six stages of birth (embryo, the urge to be born, and so on) and the twelve Rudras-and-powers onto the zodiacal passage.
- Pile the years up and you reach the great **ages** (the yugas): twelve thousand divine years to a set of four yugas, and so on.

The hinge number is this: in one full day and night the breath moves **21,600** times — and that is exactly the number of lunar days the system assigns across the year-cycle. The macrocosm of the calendar and the microcosm of the breath are the same count. Beyond the sixty-year cycle Abhinavagupta stops calculating, because the regress is simply infinite — the point has been made: *all* of time is contained in the movement of the breath.

## The Turn: One Breath, One Knowing, and the Swallowing of Time

Then comes the move that makes this a path and not just an astronomy. When the breath-movement grows quiet — gross or subtle — and "time is consumed" (*kala-grasa*), one single, perfectly full Consciousness, brimming with all its powers, shines forth.

The argument is exact and worth following. What makes one moment of knowing differ from another, Abhinavagupta says, is the difference in *time*, not a difference in the object — the way knowledge had standing on a mountain summit differs from knowledge of the same scene below. The minimum span in which any knowing exists is a single instant, and **in one rising of the breath there is exactly one knowing**. It must be so: even a thought strung on words is gradual, adhering to its syllables one after another (he quotes Panini on how even an accent occupies a fractional measure of a vowel). So until the next vibration arises, there is just one cognition, held whole.

Therefore, at the moment the practitioner holds the whole structured course of time in awareness — the "eighty-one steps" of the time-path entered through its manifold attributes — that single contemplation, whose object is the Supreme Lord, becomes **thoughtless, free of all sequence, by the swallowing of Time**. The before-and-after has nowhere left to run; it collapses into one timeless knowing.

## The Essence

That is the whole of it. The path of Time takes the most ordinary thing about you — that you breathe, and that time passes — and shows that they are one fact. Every scale of time, from a 24-second chashaka to the cosmic ages, is folded into the thirty-six fingers of a single breath. Watch that breath closely enough, let its movement grow quiet, and the sequence that *is* time is swallowed — and what remains is the one full Consciousness in which all the rising and falling of every age forever appears. As the closing verse of the chapter puts it: seeing the breath-power of the Self, and its form passed beyond time, together with the wheels of creation, maintenance, and dissolution — ever-engaged, one becomes Bhairava.
