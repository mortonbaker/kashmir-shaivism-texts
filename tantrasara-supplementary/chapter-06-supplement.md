# Chapter Six, Supplement: How the Time-Practice Actually Works

*This expands the main chapter on the Path of Time. Read that one first. The main chapter explained the vision — time is the Lord's own action, folded into the breath, and "swallowing time" returns you to the timeless. This supplement is the practical mechanics: what the yogi actually does, how the breath is measured, and the inner states that arise as the practice deepens. It draws on Abhinavagupta's own text and the classical commentaries that unpack it.*

A word before the detail. This is the most technical chapter in the early part of the book, and Abhinavagupta is compressing a much longer treatment from the Tantraloka. The commentators (Jayaratha on the Tantraloka, and the modern translators who follow him) spend many pages on what the root text states in a few lines. What follows is the practice in plain terms — enough to understand what is being described, not a substitute for the living instruction the tradition insists this kind of breath-work requires.

## The Core Idea: The Six Courses Live in the Breath

The whole of manifested reality can be laid out as six "courses" or measuring-rods (the *adhvas*): sounds, mantras, words, segments, principles, and worlds — six parallel ways of mapping everything from the subtlest vibration down to the most solid world. The teaching of this chapter is that all six of those vast cosmic maps are present, in miniature, **inside a single breath**. The breath is a working scale-model of the entire universe. So instead of trying to contemplate the cosmos out there, the yogi contemplates the breath, because the breath contains the same structure.

And what is *time*, in this scheme? Time is simply the power that makes things happen **in sequence** — one thing after another. When that sequencing power is seen in its source, inside Consciousness, it is the Goddess **Kali** — "she who measures out and withdraws." When it is seen in the limited person, it shows up as the movement of the breath. So your breath *is* time, running through you. Watching the breath rightly is watching time itself, and learning to stand free of it.

## The Measure: Breaths, Tutis, and the Counting-Down

Here is the concrete machinery the main chapter left out.

The classical texts measure the breath in fine units. A single full cycle of the vital breath is divided into **sixteen tutis** — a *tuti* being a small measured "beat" or sub-unit of the breath's movement. The whole outward-and-inward span of one breath extends across these sixteen, and across that one span the entire array of principles (tattvas) can be mapped, beat by beat, from the densest up to Shiva.

The practice is a graded **withdrawal**, and this is the heart of it. As the yogi steadies attention on the breath and the sense of being a separate knower begins to relax, the count of active units **diminishes by twos**. What needed sixteen beats to be perceived as a sequence collapses to fewer; the fifteen-fold becomes thirteen-fold, and so on downward, as the distinctions that made experience "sequential" fall away. The commentary describes this as the gradual disappearance of difference: the more the knower stops standing outside the experience as a separate measurer, the fewer the divisions, until the sequence contracts toward a single point.

At the limit, the unit comes down to **two tutis**: one regarded as "full" (the complete, resting aspect) and the other having the character of Shiva-Shakti. The Tantraloka makes a striking claim here, which the commentators highlight: the yogi who can absorb awareness into that **second tuti** — the Shiva-Shakti beat, right at the vanishing-point of sequence — attains the power of omniscience, and finally absorption into Shiva himself. The before-and-after has been compressed until it has nowhere left to run, and what remains is the timeless.

This is what the main chapter called "swallowing time" — and the word is exact: Abhinavagupta writes that the single contemplation, at the moment the whole count is held in awareness, **by the swallowing of time (kala-grasa) becomes thoughtless**, free of all sequence. One cognition, no longer strung out across a series, standing whole.

## The Worked Example: How the Earth Appears

To make this less abstract, the text gives a concrete example using the **earth principle** — the densest, most "solid" reality. It shows how even the earth, the most external and object-like thing there is, shines forth only in relation to a knower (*pramata*) and the things known (*prameyas*). The light that shines, undivided, as the very being of "earth" — that light *is* Shiva. The solidity you take to be dead matter standing outside you is, traced back, the same Consciousness measuring itself out through the breath. The example is there to prove the rule: if it holds even for earth, it holds for everything.

## The States of the Practice

This is what you asked about most directly — what the practitioner actually experiences as this deepens. The text and commentaries describe the ascent in terms of the classical sequence of **states of consciousness**, and the practitioner moves through them not as ideas but as lived conditions:

**Waking.** Ordinary outward awareness, the breath running fully extended across all sixteen units, the world experienced as solidly external and sequential. This is where everyone starts. Time here feels like a river carrying you.

**Dreaming.** As attention turns inward and rides the breath rather than the objects, experience becomes more fluid and inward, less bound to the fixed outer world — the mind's own light becomes more obviously the medium in which things appear. The grip of the external loosens.

**Deep sleep.** The objects fall away almost entirely; awareness rests in a kind of fullness-without-content, the count of units contracting sharply. This is not unconsciousness but a drawing-in — the sense of a separate measuring self growing quiet.

**The Fourth (turya).** The "witness" condition — awareness aware of itself, holding waking, dreaming, and sleep all at once as states that appear *within* it. Here the practitioner is no longer carried by the sequence; they are the still point watching it. This is the threshold where time is genuinely being "swallowed" — the second-tuti absorption belongs here.

**Beyond the Fourth (turyatita).** The witness-stance itself dissolves. There is no longer a watcher standing apart from a watched sequence, because there is no remaining sequence and no remaining outside. Only the timeless Light, in which all the rising and falling forever appears and forever is consumed. This is the Bhairava-state the chapter's closing verse names: *seeing the breath-power of the Self, and its form that has passed beyond time, together with the wheels of creation, maintenance, and dissolution — ever-engaged, one becomes Bhairava.*

A practical note the tradition is careful about: these states are not milestones you collect and keep. They come and go, and the early ones especially can feel unstable — the inward drawing of "deep sleep" can feel like blanking out, the turya witness can feel like dissociation if it is misread. This is one reason the texts are insistent that the time- and breath-practices be done under a living teacher's eye rather than from a page. The map is reliable; the territory still has to be walked with someone who has been there.

## The Essence

Strip away the numbers and the point is simple, and the same as everywhere else in this book. You are not trapped inside time. Time is something *you* are doing — Consciousness measuring itself out, beat by beat, as your own breath. Watch that measuring closely enough, let the separate measurer relax, and the beats contract toward the single timeless point they always came from. The river turns out to be made of the same still water it seemed to flow through, and you turn out to be that water, not the leaf it was carrying away.
